# THE CRADLE CODEX

## A Manual of Collection, Cultivation, and Classification — For Internal Distribution Only

*Classification: Curator-Level Clearance and Above*

*This document is the property of the Order. Unauthorized possession, duplication, or dissemination carries the penalty of memory-bleach and permanent sequestration. The Codex governs the life-cycle of every Sensitive child from the moment of detection to the final determination of their utility. It is the architecture of the Order's most sacred work. Deviations are not permitted. Mercy is in the method.*

---

## FOREWORD: THE NECESSITY OF THE NEST

Magic is not a gift. Magic is a condition. Left untreated, it exposes the afflicted child to predation, exploitation, and—in the fullness of time—a death measured in stolen warmth, borrowed breath, or leached bone. The world outside the Cradle is full of mages who would take what an unguarded child cannot protect. The world inside the Cradle is full of children who learn to protect themselves and, in doing so, become assets to the equilibrium that sustains all peoples.

This is the Order's purpose. Not conquest, not accumulation of power, but the careful, merciful management of a congenital vulnerability. We collect Sensitive children because no one else can. We train them because no one else will. We place them because the realms need mages, and the realms need mages who are legible, accountable, and loyal.

The process detailed in this Codex is the engine of that purpose. It begins with a child who burns too hot or breathes too light or hears the tremor of footsteps a mile away. It ends—if all proceeds as designed—with a placed mage serving their realm, their debt-pattern clean and recognizable, their loyalty to the Order an unspoken constant beneath their daily duties. Between those two points lies the Cradle.

Some children will not complete the journey. Some will prove unsuitable for training, resistant to instruction, or dangerously unstable. These children are not discarded. They are contained, comforted, and—when necessary—released from their condition through humane and painless means. The Order does not waste life. The Order does not prolong suffering. The Order makes the hard choices that the realms are too sentimental to make.

This Codex is the manual for those choices. Read it as a shepherd reads the seasons. Read it as a physician reads a patient. Read it with the understanding that every protocol described herein exists because a child once died without it, and we will not make that mistake again.

# BOOK ONE: THE SENSITIVE CHILD

## 1.1 Definition

A Sensitive is any untrained magical child whose Gate has opened but who has not yet learned to guard their internal reservoir. The term is developmental, not congenital. All mages are born Sensitive. The distinction dissolves upon successful training. A Sensitive is a mage in waiting; a mage is a Sensitive made safe.

## 1.2 The Open Gate

The Gate opens without warning, usually between the ages of two and seven, occasionally later. There is no ceremony, no visible sign, no pain. One day the child's internal reservoir simply becomes accessible, and the child begins to broadcast.

What they broadcast is information. Their Gate identity, their approximate depth, their emotional state, the rhythm of their heartbeat and breath and fear. To a trained Mage-Detector, an unguarded Sensitive is as visible as a bonfire on a dark plain. To a predatory mage, they are a resource—a walking reservoir of heat, water, breath, minerals, or charge that can be borrowed without consent.

The child does not know this. The child feels only that they are warm when others are cold, or that the air moves strangely around them, or that they can feel footsteps through the soles of their feet. The child may think they have a gift. They do not. They have a vulnerability. The Order's first task is to convince them—and their families—of this truth.

## 1.3 The Danger of the Unguarded

An unguarded Sensitive cannot resist Affliction. If a desperate Water-mage in a drought-stricken village encounters an unguarded Water-Sensitive child, the mage can draw water from the child's body without the child's knowledge or consent. The child will grow thirsty, then dizzy, then confused. If the borrowing continues, the child will die of desiccation, and the mage will walk away with a replenished reservoir and a manageable debt.

This is not hypothetical. The Order's archives contain 847 confirmed cases of Sensitive predation in the past century alone, and those are only the cases we know about. The actual number is higher. The realms do not keep records of children who die of unexplained thirst or sudden hypothermia or lungs that simply stop working. The Order does.

Collection is not abduction. Collection is protection. A child in our custody is a child no mage can exploit.

# BOOK TWO: IDENTIFICATION AND COLLECTION

## 2.1 The Detection Network

The Order maintains a continent-wide network of Mage-Detectors—trained mages whose sole function is to locate unguarded Sensitives. Detectors are selected for perceptual sensitivity, patience, and emotional detachment. They travel continuously, visiting villages, towns, and cities on a rotating schedule that ensures no populated area goes more than eighteen months without a sweep.

Detection methodology:
- **Active Scanning:** The Detector extends their own perception, listening for the "leakage" of an unguarded reservoir. This is a learned skill, not a mutation. Any mage can be trained to do it, though aptitude varies.
- **Passive Monitoring:** Detectors embed themselves in communities for weeks or months, observing children for subtle signs—unexplained frost in warm rooms, localized still air, ground-tremors without seismic cause, electrical disturbances around a particular household.
- **Savant Confirmation:** When a Detector identifies a potential Sensitive, a Savant is dispatched to confirm. The Savant reads the child's debt-signature—or, in the case of a newly opened Gate, the absence of debt that paradoxically confirms Sensitivity. No child is collected without Savant confirmation.

## 2.2 The Legal Framework

The Order operates under treaty with every major realm. The relevant clauses are standardized:

- The Order has right of entry to any dwelling where a Sensitive child is believed to reside.
- The Order has right of seizure of any untrained Sensitive, regardless of the family's wishes.
- The Order has right of confinement of any Sensitive until such time as they are trained and placed, or otherwise managed.
- The family is entitled to compensation (see Appendix A: Compensation Schedules) and to notification of the child's eventual placement or, in the event of the child's death in service, a sealed letter of condolence.
- The family is not entitled to visitation, correspondence, or knowledge of the child's training location.

These treaties are renegotiated every twenty-five years. No realm has ever refused to renew them. The alternative—a realm responsible for its own Sensitive management—is a realm that will soon be responsible for a magical catastrophe. The realms know this. The treaties stand.

## 2.3 The Approach

Collection begins with the approach. The Detector, having confirmed the child's Sensitivity and location, contacts the regional Collection Team. The team consists of:

- **One Handler:** Trained in Sensitive management, child psychology, and de-escalation. The Handler is the public face of the collection. They speak to the family. They explain the necessity. They offer the compensation. They bind the child's wrists.
- **One Savant (or Savant-trained Detector):** Confirms the child's identity and reads the family for any hidden magical anomalies.
- **Two Escorts:** Provide security. Escorts are typically mundane soldiers or low-level mages who are trained to remain silent and impassive throughout the collection. Their presence is a statement, not a threat.
- **One Cradle-hound (optional):** Deployed if the Detector suspects the presence of additional Sensitives in the household or vicinity.

The approach is conducted during daylight hours. The Handler knocks on the door. The family is informed that their child has been identified as Sensitive and that the Order, under the authority of the relevant treaty, will now assume custody. The family is permitted to say goodbye. The goodbye is brief. Prolonged farewells increase trauma for the child and the family. The Handler intervenes if the goodbye exceeds five minutes.

Resistance is rare. When it occurs, it is met with patience. The Handler explains again. The Escorts remain still. If the family becomes violent, the Escorts restrain them with minimal force. The child is collected. The family is not harmed. The Order does not punish resistance; resistance is a natural response to an unnatural situation. But resistance does not alter the outcome.

## 2.4 The Silk

The child's wrists are bound with white silk.

Silk is chosen for specific, practical reasons:
- Silk does not chafe. The child will not arrive at the Cradle with abrasions, bruises, or ligature marks.
- Silk does not conduct magic. Metal restraints might accidentally ground a Lightning-Sensitive's charge or absorb moisture from a Water-Sensitive's skin. Silk is inert.
- Silk is a psychological tool. It communicates restraint without communicating cruelty. The child feels bound but not harmed. The family sees the child bound but not bruised. The Order's reputation for gentleness is maintained.
- Silk is clean. White silk shows no stains. If the child struggles, the silk holds. If the child weeps, the silk absorbs nothing visible. The Handler's hands remain clean.

The binding is a figure-eight loop around the wrists, snug but not tight. The child can flex their fingers, rotate their hands, and maintain circulation. They cannot free themselves. The knot is a standard slip-knot that tightens under tension but can be released instantly by the Handler in an emergency.

Some children ask why they are being tied. The Handler's response is scripted: "The silk is to keep you safe. Some mages might try to borrow from you. The silk helps remind you that you are protected now."

Some children cry. Some children scream. Some children go silent. The Handler has been trained for all three. The silk does not discriminate.

## 2.5 The Carriage

The carriage is black-lacquered with the Order's insignia—a circle bisected by a single vertical line, representing the equilibrium. The windows are set high, above the child's eye level. This is intentional.

The high windows serve multiple purposes:
- **Security:** The child cannot be seen from outside. The carriage is indistinguishable from any other Order vehicle.
- **Sensory Regulation:** A newly collected Sensitive is often overstimulated. Limiting visual input reduces panic and prevents the child from fixating on landmarks (which could aid escape attempts or be used to identify the Cradle's location later).
- **Psychological Conditioning:** The child learns, in their first hour of Order custody, that they are not in control of their environment. The lesson is delivered without words. It settles deeper than words ever could.

The interior of the carriage is plain: padded benches, a water-skin, a blanket if the climate is cold. The Handler sits opposite the child. The Escorts ride on the exterior running-boards. The Savant rides separately. The journey to the Cradle may take days or weeks, depending on the distance. During that time, the Handler does not speak unless spoken to. The Handler does not offer comfort unless the child is visibly distressed to the point of hyperventilation or shock. The Handler does not threaten, coerce, or punish. The Handler simply *is*—a calm, neutral presence, the first lesson in the Order's way.

## 2.6 The Family and Compensation

Upon collection, the family is given a disbursement in accordance with the realm's compensation schedule. Amounts are calibrated to be meaningful without being life-changing. The goal is to acknowledge the family's loss, not to purchase their child.

The family is also given a sealed certificate of collection, which serves as legal proof that the child was taken under treaty authority and not abducted. The certificate includes the child's name, the date of collection, and the seal of the regional Detector. It does not include the child's destination or any information about the training they will receive.

Families who inquire about their child's welfare are told: "Your child is in the Order's care. They will be trained, protected, and placed in service to the equilibrium. You will be notified when they are placed. You will be notified if they die. You will not be notified otherwise."

Families who persist are told: "Further inquiry will be noted."

# BOOK THREE: INTAKE AND ASSESSMENT

## 3.1 Arrival at the Cradle

The carriage enters the Cradle through the Outer Ring gate, which opens only for Order vehicles. The child is escorted into the Intake Hall—a long, low-ceilinged chamber of grey stone, lit by mage-lights that cast no shadows. The walls are lined with silk panels. The air is still and cool. The floor absorbs sound.

The Intake Hall is designed to be overwhelming in its neutrality. There is no color, no decoration, no distraction. The child's attention turns inward. The child begins to understand that they have left the world they knew.

The Handler removes the silk bindings. A new Handler—an Intake Specialist—assumes custody. The first Handler departs without farewell. The child will not see them again. Attachment to individual Handlers is discouraged; the child must learn that the Order itself is the constant, not any single representative.

## 3.2 The Cleansing

The child is taken to a private chamber and bathed. The water is warm. The soap is unscented. The attendants are silent. The child's clothes are removed and incinerated. The child is given a plain grey tunic, trousers, and soft-soled shoes. All children in the Cradle wear the same garments, regardless of origin, status, or Gate. The uniformity is intentional. The child's former identity is being washed away. What remains is the Sensitive, and the Sensitive belongs to the Order.

The child's hair is not cut. The child's name is not changed. These are concessions to psychological stability. A child who loses everything at once may break. A child who loses almost everything will adapt.

## 3.3 The First Reading

Within six hours of arrival, the child undergoes the First Reading.

The child is brought to a small chamber lined with silk. A Savant—not the one who confirmed the child's Sensitivity, but a different Savant, an Assessor—enters and sits opposite the child. The Assessor does not introduce themselves. The Assessor asks the child to hold out their hands, palms up.

The Assessor reads.

The First Reading establishes the child's magical baseline:
- **Gate Identity:** Fire, Water, Wind, Earth, or Lightning.
- **Depth Estimate:** The nascent depth of the child's internal reservoir. Most newly collected Sensitives register at Depth 1-2; occasionally a child with significant unconscious borrowing will register at Depth 3.
- **Mutation Potential:** The Assessor reads the child's Gate structure for indications of mutation aptitude. Not every child can access every mutation. A Fire-Sensitive may show potential for Light, Ash, or Forge; a Water-Sensitive may show potential for Ice, Healing, or Blood. The Assessor notes which paths are open and which are closed.
- **Psychological Markers:** The emotional signature of the child's unconscious borrowings. Fear, anger, loneliness, curiosity—each leaves a trace. The Assessor notes the dominant emotional pattern and flags any indications of instability, defiance, or trauma that may require special handling.
- **Debt Status:** Newly opened Gates rarely carry debt, but some children have already borrowed unconsciously—drawing heat from a sibling during an argument, pulling moisture from the air during a nightmare, grounding a static charge through a pet. The Assessor records any existing debt, however minor.

The First Reading takes approximately ten minutes. The child is not told what the Assessor sees. The Assessor does not speak to the child. The Assessor records their findings in a sealed ledger, which is forwarded to the Placement Council.

The child is then taken to their dormitory.

## 3.4 Initial Classification

Based on the First Reading, the child receives an Initial Classification. This is a provisional determination of the child's likely trajectory through the Cradle's training program.

**Classification Tiers:**

- **Tier I — Promising:** Strong depth, clear mutation potential, stable emotional markers. These children are the future placed mages, the assets that will serve the realms and maintain the Order's influence. Approximately 60% of collected Sensitives fall into this tier.
- **Tier II — Marginal:** Moderate depth, limited or unclear mutation potential, emotional markers indicating possible difficulty with training (excessive fear, defiance, attachment to former life). These children may be trained, but with closer supervision and lower expectations. Approximately 25% of Sensitives fall into this tier.
- **Tier III — Difficult:** Low depth, no mutation potential, or significant psychological instability. These children may not complete training. They require containment rather than cultivation. Approximately 10% of Sensitives fall into this tier.
- **Tier IV — Unsuitable:** The child's Gate is dangerously unstable, or their psychological markers indicate a high probability of self-destruction, violence, or complete resistance to training. These children cannot be trained and cannot be safely contained in standard facilities. Approximately 5% of Sensitives fall into this tier. Management of Tier IV children is addressed in Book Six.

The Initial Classification is provisional. Children may move between tiers as training progresses. A Tier II child who demonstrates unexpected aptitude may rise. A Tier I child who resists instruction may fall. The classification is a guide, not a sentence.

# BOOK FOUR: THE TRAINING APPARATUS

## 4.1 The Dormitory

All children are housed in communal dormitories organized by Gate and age cohort. A typical dormitory holds twelve to twenty children, each with a narrow cot, a footlocker for personal effects (letters from home are not permitted; the footlocker contains a spare tunic, a water-skin, and a small polished stone—the Order's sole concession to comfort), and a silk privacy curtain that can be drawn but not locked.

The dormitory is supervised by a Dormitory Handler, who sleeps in an adjoining room and is available at all hours. The Dormitory Handler's duties include:
- Enforcing lights-out and wake-up schedules.
- Monitoring for illness, injury, or emotional distress.
- Preventing unauthorized communication between children (conversation is permitted during designated social hours only; whispered exchanges after lights-out are discouraged and, if persistent, reported).
- Reporting any child who speaks of escape, resistance, or disloyalty to the Order.

The dormitory is designed to foster a sense of community while discouraging intimacy. Children who become too attached to each other are children who may conspire, who may grieve too deeply if separated, who may form loyalties that compete with their loyalty to the Order. The Dormitory Handler is trained to identify and manage these attachments. Friendships are permitted but monitored. Love—filial, romantic, or otherwise—is noted and, if it threatens operational cohesion, gently dissolved through reassignment.

## 4.2 The Handler

The Handler is the child's primary point of contact with the Order's authority. Every child is assigned a Handler upon arrival. The Handler remains with the child throughout their training, from Intake to placement or other disposition.

**Handler Qualifications:**
- Insensitive (preferred) or a low-level mage who has demonstrated exceptional emotional control.
- Completion of the Handler Training Program, a two-year curriculum covering child development, de-escalation, basic magical literacy, and Order doctrine.
- Psychological evaluation confirming patience, emotional detachment, and absolute loyalty to the Order.
- Annual recertification by a Savant, who reads the Handler for signs of emotional entanglement, sympathy for the children, or ideological doubt.

**Handler Duties:**
- Escorting the child to and from training sessions.
- Monitoring the child's physical and emotional state and reporting any concerns to the Placement Council.
- Administering discipline when necessary (see Section 4.5).
- Serving as the Order's voice in the child's daily life—the calm, constant presence that reminds the child they are watched, valued, and held.

**Handler Prohibitions:**
- Handlers do not touch children except as necessary for escort, restraint, or medical intervention.
- Handlers do not express affection. Praise is limited to acknowledgment of progress. Comfort is limited to the scripted phrases provided in the Handler's Manual.
- Handlers do not answer questions about the child's family, the outside world, or the child's eventual placement.
- Handlers do not share personal information. The child does not know the Handler's name beyond their title. The Handler is not a friend. The Handler is a function.

The Handler's hands are clean. This is a literal requirement. Handlers wash their hands before and after every interaction with a child. The child learns to associate the Handler with cleanliness, order, and the absence of harm. The silk left no marks. The Handler leaves none either.

## 4.3 The Instructor

The Instructor is responsible for the child's magical education. Unlike the Handler, the Instructor is always a mage—usually a placed mage who has returned to the Cradle for a teaching rotation, or a career Instructor who has chosen the Cradle over external placement.

**Instructor Qualifications:**
- Depth 5 or higher in their Gate.
- Mastery of the standard curriculum (guarding, base state, body sense, kinetic amplification, Affliction).
- At least one mutation at the advanced level.
- Completion of the Instructor Training Program, a one-year curriculum covering pedagogy, student assessment, and the ethical management of Sensitive development.
- Approval by the Savant Council, who reads each prospective Instructor for signs of sadism, excessive ambition, or ideological deviation. Instructors who enjoy their students' pain are removed. Instructors who are indifferent to their students' pain are retained.

**Instructor Duties:**
- Conducting daily training sessions with small groups (three to six children, grouped by Gate and aptitude).
- Assessing each child's progress and reporting to the Placement Council.
- Recommending children for mutation testing, accelerated training, or remedial instruction.
- Pushing each child to the limit of their capacity—and, when necessary, slightly beyond.

**The Instructor's Philosophy:**

The Instructor's role is not to be loved. The Instructor's role is to be effective. A child who fears the Instructor will work harder. A child who respects the Instructor will work smarter. A child who hates the Instructor will still work, and the work is what matters.

The Order's pedagogical doctrine holds that magical training is inherently uncomfortable. Borrowing causes pain. Debt causes scars. A child who is sheltered from this reality during training will be unprepared for it in the field. The Instructor therefore does not coddle. The Instructor does not offer empty encouragement. The Instructor states the objective, demonstrates the technique, and demands repetition until mastery is achieved.

"Again," is the Instructor's most common word.

"Again," means the child has not yet succeeded. "Again," means the child will continue until they do. "Again," is not anger. It is not disappointment. It is simply the recognition that the work is not finished. The child learns to hear "Again," as the sound of the Order's patience—boundless, implacable, waiting.

## 4.4 The Training Floor

Training is conducted on the training floor—a vast, grey-stone chamber in the Middle Ring, subdivided into smaller arenas by movable silk partitions. The floor is designed to absorb magic without damage and to dissipate environmental debt without scarring the landscape.

Each arena is equipped with:
- Elemental sources appropriate to the students' Gates (braziers for Fire, water-troughs for Water, wind-tunnels for Wind, stone blocks for Earth, Leyden jars for Lightning).
- Debt-monitoring apparatus that records the magnitude of each working and alerts the Instructor if a child overborrows.
- A medical kit with supplies for treating frostbite, dehydration, barotrauma, bone fractures, and neural shock.

Children train barefoot on the cold stone. The cold is intentional. It reminds Fire-Sensitives that heat is borrowed. It reminds Water-Sensitives that dryness is a debt. It reminds all children that the Cradle is not a home—it is a place of work.

## 4.5 Discipline

Discipline in the Cradle is corrective, not punitive. The goal is to shape behavior, not to break the child. A broken child is a wasted asset. A shaped child is a placed mage.

**Discipline Ladder:**
1. **Verbal Correction:** The Handler or Instructor states the infraction and the expected behavior. First infractions are always met with verbal correction.
2. **Silk Isolation:** The child is placed in a silk-lined isolation chamber for a period not exceeding six hours. The chamber is dark, quiet, and warm. The child is not harmed. The child is simply removed from all stimulation. This is effective for children who are overstimulated, defiant, or emotionally dysregulated. Most children correct their behavior after one or two isolation sessions.
3. **Debt-Awareness Training:** For infractions involving unauthorized magic—attempting to work magic outside training, attempting to borrow from another child, attempting to access a mutation without authorization—the child is assigned additional practice sessions focused on the specific debt incurred. The child learns, through repetition, exactly what their infraction cost. This is not punishment. It is education.
4. **Reclassification:** A child who persistently defies training, attempts escape, or demonstrates ideological resistance may be reclassified from Tier I to Tier II, or from Tier II to Tier III. Reclassification is not announced to the child. The child simply notices that their Instructor is less demanding, their training sessions are shorter, their peers are different. The message is delivered through absence.
5. **Containment Review:** A child who continues to resist after reclassification is referred to the Placement Council for Containment Review. Possible outcomes include intensified supervision, transfer to a long-term containment facility, or—if the child is deemed a danger to themselves or others—permanent sedation (see Book Six).

Physical punishment is not authorized. The Order does not beat children. The Order does not starve children. The Order does not deprive children of sleep. These methods are crude, ineffective, and leave marks. The Order's methods leave no marks. The silk leaves no marks. The Handler's hands are clean.

# BOOK FIVE: TRAINING PHASES AND GATE CURRICULUM

## 5.1 The Five Phases

Magical training proceeds through five phases, each building on the previous. Advancement through phases is determined by demonstrated competence, not by age or time served. A child who masters Phase One in six months advances. A child who struggles with Phase One for three years remains there until the Placement Council decides otherwise.

**Phase One: Guarding.** The child learns to partition their attention, holding their internal reservoir behind a locked door. This is the foundation of all subsequent training. A child who cannot guard cannot be trusted with any other magical skill. Phase One typically requires six to eighteen months. Some children never master it. Those children do not advance.

**Phase Two: Base State Manipulation.** The child learns to borrow and direct their element in its simplest form—flame, water, wind, stone, or arc. This is the combat baseline, the fundamental working from which all mutations derive. Phase Two includes extensive debt-management training: the child learns to track their borrowings, to pay environmental debt when possible, to accept self-debt when necessary, and to recognize the physical markers of overuse in their own body.

**Phase Three: Body Sense Application.** The child learns the non-combat perceptual dimension of their Gate—heat-sight for Fire, life-reading for Water, pressure-reading for Wind, tremor-sense for Earth, electroreception for Lightning. This phase also includes introductory kinetic amplification (thermal updraft leaps, hydraulic exoskeleton, mass-lightening, stone-flesh integration, neural overclocking). Body sense training is physically demanding; the child learns to perceive their environment in a new sensory modality while simultaneously managing the debt of that perception.

**Phase Four: Affliction.** The child learns the darkest application of their Gate—the transfer of debt directly into an opponent's body. Affliction training is conducted under strict supervision and is limited to controlled exercises against insulated targets. The child does not practice Affliction on other children. The child does not practice Affliction on animals. The child is taught the legal and moral weight of Affliction: that it is a distinct category of magical assault, that its use outside sanctioned combat or execution carries severe penalties, and that the debt transferred to an enemy is still debt, and it will settle somewhere.

**Phase Five: Mutation Selection.** The child, having demonstrated mastery of Phases One through Four, is evaluated for mutation potential. The Instructor and the Savant Council review the child's training record, psychological profile, and Gate structure to determine which mutation paths are viable. The child is then guided into their first mutation. This is the point at which the child's trajectory diverges sharply—the promising child enters a path that will define their career, while the marginal child may be denied mutation training entirely and directed toward generalist service.

## 5.2 Mutation Selection Protocol

Not every child accesses every mutation. The Order's taxonomy identifies four mutations per Gate, but individual aptitude varies. The Selection Protocol is designed to match each child with the mutation path that maximizes their utility to the Order while minimizing the risk of self-destruction, rebellion, or psychological corruption.

**Fire Gates:**

- *Light:* For children with exceptional control, high pain tolerance, and a psychological profile indicating discipline and precision. Light-mages are valued for their combat versatility and are often placed in military roles. The debt (internal hyperthermia) is lethal if mismanaged; only the most promising Fire-Sensitives are guided to this path.
- *Warmth:* For children with high empathy, patience, and a psychological profile indicating a desire to nurture rather than destroy. Warmth-mages are placed in support roles—heating outposts, ripening crops, sustaining the wounded. The debt (persistent internal chill) is manageable but permanent.
- *Ash:* For children with a psychological profile indicating curiosity, pattern-recognition, and tolerance for psychological isolation. Ash-mages are forensic assets, valued for their ability to read the history of burned materials. The debt (memory-bleed) is severe; the child must demonstrate exceptional psychological resilience.
- *Forge:* For children with mechanical aptitude and patience. Forge-mages are artisans, not warriors. The debt (shortened lifespan) is non-negotiable; candidates are informed of the cost before acceptance.

A Fire-Sensitive who shows no strong mutation aptitude, or whose psychological profile contraindicates all available mutations, may be trained to an advanced base state without mutation. These generalist Fire-mages serve as combat pyromancers, lacking the specialization of their mutated peers but also avoiding their specific debts.

**Water Gates:**

- *Ice:* For children with discipline, precision, and tolerance for physical cold. Ice-mages are valued for combat and construction. The debt (permanent cold-insensitivity, frostbite risk) is manageable with proper equipment.
- *Healing:* For children with exceptional empathy, emotional stability, and a psychological profile indicating a willingness to suffer for others. Healing is the rarest and most revered Water path. The debt (sympathetic injury) is severe; candidates are warned that they will experience a version of every wound they heal.
- *Blood:* The Order does not guide children toward Blood mutation. Children who demonstrate Blood aptitude are flagged for special monitoring. In rare cases—when the child's psychological profile indicates absolute loyalty and emotional detachment—Blood training may be authorized for use in sanctioned execution and counter-rogue operations. Blood-mages are feared and isolated. The moral debt is severe. Most children flagged for Blood aptitude are instead diverted to Healing or Ice.
- *Poison:* For children with chemical aptitude and high tolerance for chronic illness. Poison-mages are valued for area-denial and assassination. The debt (permanent low-level sickness) is severe and irreversible.

**Wind Gates:**

- *Sound:* For children with auditory sensitivity and precision. Sound-mages are valued for communication, surveillance, and non-lethal control. The debt (progressive deafness) is severe.
- *Silence:* For children with psychological profiles indicating comfort with isolation and stealth. Silence-mages are valued for infiltration and assassination. The debt (reduced lung capacity) is permanent.
- *Breath:* For children with healing aptitude (analogous to Water-Healing). Breath-mages are medics, valued for their ability to clear airways and restart lungs. The debt (sympathetic respiratory distress) is transient but recurrent.
- *Storm:* For children with exceptional power, high debt-tolerance, and psychological profiles indicating comfort with large-scale destruction. Storm-mages are strategic weapons, deployed only in major conflicts. The debt (environmental devastation, barotrauma) is enormous.

**Earth Gates:**

- *Metal:* For children with tactical aptitude and aggression. Metal-mages are valued for anti-matériel combat. The debt (mineral depletion, anaemia) is manageable with dietary supplementation.
- *Flesh:* Monitored closely. Flesh-mages occupy a grey zone between healing and violation. Training is authorized only for children with exceptional psychological stability. The debt (accelerated aging, joint deterioration) is severe.
- *Root:* For children with patience, attunement to place, and willingness to accept geographic limitation. Root-mages are valued for agricultural support and territorial defense. The debt (rootedness, wasting illness if removed from territory) limits placement options.
- *Bone:* The Order restricts Bone training to specific cultural contexts (primarily the Hold-Kingdoms, where ancestor-veneration makes Bone-magic culturally acceptable). The debt (skeletal sentience, psychological disturbance) is severe. Bone-mages are monitored for signs of ideological deviation; the whisper of old bones sometimes contradicts the Order's teachings.

**Lightning Gates:**

- *Nerve:* For children with healing aptitude and precision. Nerve-mages are valued for neurological medicine and, in combat roles, for motor disruption. The debt (neurological misfire) is recurrent.
- *Magnet:* For children with spatial aptitude and tolerance for disorientation. Magnet-mages are valued for anti-metal combat and, at high levels, flight. The debt (chronic vertigo) is permanent.
- *Pulse:* For children with communication aptitude. Pulse-mages are living relays, valued by armies and navies. The debt (brain-noise, aphasia, detachment) is cumulative and severe.
- *Static:* For children with patience and pain tolerance. Static-mages are trap-setters and siege-breakers. The debt (chronic pain, insomnia, tremor) is permanent and disabling.

## 5.3 The Instructor's Role in Mutation Selection

The Instructor is the primary advocate for each child's mutation path. During Phase Four, the Instructor submits a detailed report on each child's aptitude, temperament, and readiness. The report includes:

- A summary of the child's performance in Phases One through Four.
- A recommendation for the child's first mutation.
- An assessment of the child's psychological fitness for that mutation.
- A candid evaluation of the child's likelihood of survival.

This last element is the Instructor's most difficult duty. Some mutations carry a high mortality rate during training. Light, Blood, Storm, Bone, and Static all claim a percentage of their students every year. The Instructor must honestly assess whether the child is likely to survive the training, and whether their death, if it occurs, will serve the Order's interests or merely waste an asset.

The Placement Council reviews the Instructor's report alongside the Savant's baseline reading and any Handler observations. The Council makes the final determination. The child is not consulted. The child's preferences are irrelevant. The child is an asset, and assets do not choose their deployment.

This sounds cold. It is cold. But it is also merciful. A child who desperately wishes to be a Healer but lacks the psychological profile will suffer unnecessarily and die young. A child who is guided away from their preferred path may resent the Order, but they will live. The Order makes choices the child cannot make for themselves. That is the responsibility we bear.

# BOOK SIX: EVALUATION AND SORTING

## 6.1 The Annual Review

Every child undergoes an annual review conducted by the Placement Council—a rotating body of three Curators, two Savants, and one senior Instructor. The review assesses the child's progress, re-evaluates their classification tier, and determines their next year's training plan.

The child is present for the review but does not speak unless asked a direct question. The child stands in the center of the room while the Council discusses them as though they were not there. This is intentional. The child learns that they are legible, that their body and mind and debt are open books, that nothing they do escapes the Order's notice.

**Review Criteria:**
- Magical progress (Phase advancement, technique mastery, debt management).
- Physical health (debt-scar severity, overall conditioning, any training-related injuries).
- Psychological state (emotional stability, social integration, signs of defiance or despair).
- Handler reports (behavioral incidents, rule violations, notable interactions with other children).
- Instructor reports (aptitude, effort, attitude toward training, response to pressure).

**Outcomes:**
- **Advancement:** The child is cleared to continue training at their current tier or, in cases of exceptional performance, to advance to a higher tier.
- **Probation:** The child is placed on a three-month probationary period during which specific improvements must be demonstrated. Failure to improve results in reclassification.
- **Reclassification:** The child is moved to a lower tier, with corresponding changes to their training intensity, mutation access, and eventual placement prospects.
- **Containment Referral:** The child is deemed unsuitable for continued training and is referred to the Containment Division.

## 6.2 The Paths

The Order recognizes five possible outcomes for a Sensitive child. These are the paths.

**Path One: Placed Mage.** The child completes all five training phases, achieves at least Depth 4, and is placed in service to a realm under the standard placement treaty. Placed mages are the Order's finished product—trained, marked, loyal, and legible. They serve their realm for a minimum of fifteen years, after which they may request reassignment, return to the Cradle for advanced training, or (in rare cases) retirement to a monitored civilian life. Approximately 55% of collected Sensitives achieve Path One.

**Path Two: Cradle Service.** The child completes training but is deemed unsuitable for external placement—due to psychological fragility, physical disability, or a debt-profile that would make them a liability in the field. Cradle Service mages remain in the Middle Ring as Instructors, researchers, or administrative staff. They live out their lives within the Cradle's walls, contributing to the Order's work without the pressures of external deployment. Approximately 15% of Sensitives achieve Path Two.

**Path Three: Long-Term Containment.** The child is unable or unwilling to complete training, but their Gate is stable enough that they do not pose an active danger. Long-Term Containment involves residence in a secure facility where the child's reservoir is regularly monitored and their environment is controlled to prevent unauthorized borrowing. Contained Sensitives are comfortable. They are fed, clothed, and given access to books, exercise, and limited social interaction. They are not free. They will never be free. But they are alive, and they are safe. Approximately 20% of Sensitives are placed in Long-Term Containment.

**Path Four: Permanent Sedation.** The child's Gate is dangerously unstable, or their psychological profile indicates a high risk of violence, self-destruction, or successful escape. Permanent Sedation is a humane procedure in which the child is placed in a magically induced coma and their internal reservoir is gradually drained over a period of months until the Gate closes entirely. The child does not suffer. The child does not wake. The body is interred in the Cradle's crypts with full rites. This path is reserved for children who cannot be safely contained and who represent a threat to the equilibrium. Approximately 7% of Sensitives are sedated.

**Path Five: Death in Training.** Some children die during training. The Light mutation induces fatal hyperthermia. The Storm mutation collapses lungs. The Bone mutation triggers catastrophic skeletal rebellion. Accidents occur on the training floor. A child misjudges their reservoir and overborrows. A child's guard slips during Affliction practice and their partner's spell finds purchase. These deaths are regrettable but unavoidable. Magic is dangerous. Training mages is dangerous. The Order accepts this cost because the alternative—untrained Sensitives dying in the world, or worse, becoming rogues—is far costlier. Approximately 3% of Sensitives die before placement.

The percentages shift slightly from year to year, but the proportions have remained remarkably stable across the Order's history. This stability is evidence that our methods are sound. The system produces exactly as many placed mages as the realms require, with acceptable levels of attrition. The equilibrium is maintained.

## 6.3 The Unspoken Path

There is a sixth outcome, unlisted in any official document but understood by every Handler, Instructor, and Curator: escape.

Children sometimes get out. The Order's security is robust but not perfect. A child who is clever, desperate, and lucky may find a gap in the patrols, a door left unguarded, a carriage route that passes near enough to wilderness. Most escape attempts fail. The child is recovered, disciplined, and returned to training—or, if the attempt reveals deep ideological resistance, reclassified for Containment.

Some escape attempts are allowed to proceed.

This is a restricted operational doctrine, known only to Curators and senior Handlers. When a child's psychological profile indicates simmering defiance, when their Instructor reports that they are training well but asking too many questions, when their Handler notes whispered conversations after lights-out and a gaze that lingers too long on exit routes—the Placement Council may authorize a Controlled Escape Exercise.

The door is left unguarded. The patrol is subtly adjusted. The child runs. The Order watches.

The purpose of the Exercise is threefold:
- To confirm the child's disloyalty in a way that leaves no ambiguity.
- To identify the child's co-conspirators—other children who run with them, or who knew of the plan and did not report it.
- To administer a terminal lesson. The child is apprehended, or terminated, depending on the severity of the breach and the child's value as an asset. The other children witness the consequences. The lesson is learned.

A successful escape—a child who runs and is not recovered—is a failure of the Exercise. But even failure serves a purpose. The escaped child carries the Order's debt-pattern in their flesh. A Savant can read them anywhere. A Silencer can be dispatched at any time. The child is never truly free. They are simply on a longer leash, and their eventual recovery—or termination—will serve as another lesson for those who remain.

# BOOK SEVEN: THE HANDLER'S ROLE

## 7.1 The Philosophy of Handling

The Handler is not a guard. The Handler is not a parent. The Handler is a *constant*—the living embodiment of the Order's presence in the child's daily life. The child must come to associate the Handler with safety, predictability, and the absence of harm. The Handler is the answer to every question the child is not allowed to ask: *Am I safe? Is this forever? Does anyone care what happens to me?*

The answer, conveyed not in words but in the Handler's steady presence, is: *Yes. Yes. No. And that is the safest thing of all.*

## 7.2 Daily Duties

The Handler's day begins before the child wakes and ends after the child sleeps. Duties include:

- **Wake-up and lights-out:** The Handler is present when the child wakes and when the child sleeps. The first and last face the child sees each day is the Handler's—calm, neutral, unchanging.
- **Meal escort:** The child eats in a communal refectory, seated by dormitory cohort. The Handler stands at the perimeter of the room, watching. Conversation is permitted during meals. The Handler listens.
- **Training escort:** The Handler escorts the child to and from the training floor. The walk is silent. The child learns that transitions between spaces are not opportunities for conversation.
- **Medical escort:** If the child is injured or ill, the Handler escorts them to the infirmary and remains present during treatment. The child learns that even in vulnerability, the Handler is there—not to comfort, but to witness.
- **Evening observation:** After the evening meal, children are permitted one hour of supervised socialization in the dormitory common area. The Handler observes from a designated post, noting which children speak to which others, who is isolated, who is forming attachments, who is whispering.
- **Report filing:** At the end of each day, the Handler files a brief report on each child in their charge. The report covers mood, behavior, notable incidents, and any observations that may bear on the child's classification.

## 7.3 Scripted Interactions

To maintain consistency across Handlers, the Order provides a scripted lexicon for common situations.

**When a child asks about their family:**
"The Order is your family now. Your former family is safe. Thinking about them will make your training more difficult. I encourage you to focus on the present."

**When a child cries:**
"Crying is a natural response to change. It will pass. If it does not pass, we will help you find other ways to manage your feelings."

**When a child asks when they can leave:**
"You will leave when your training is complete and the Placement Council has assigned you to a realm. Until then, the Cradle is your home. Make yourself comfortable in it."

**When a child says they hate the Order:**
"The Order does not require your love. The Order requires your safety and your compliance. Both are being provided. In time, you may come to feel gratitude. If you do not, you will still be safe. That is enough."

**When a child attempts to touch the Handler—a hug, a hand on the arm, a child clinging:**
The Handler steps back. "Physical contact is not permitted between Handlers and children. This is for your protection as well as mine. Please return to your designated space."

**When a child asks the Handler's name:**
"I am your Handler. That is all you need to call me."

## 7.4 Emotional Detachment

The greatest danger for a Handler is attachment. A Handler who comes to love a child in their charge is a Handler who cannot make objective decisions about that child's welfare. A Handler who pities a child will soften discipline, overlook warning signs, and—in extreme cases—facilitate escape or sabotage the child's assessment.

Annual Savant readings screen for these attachments. The Savant reads the Handler's emotional signature for traces of excessive sympathy, guilt, or protective instinct. Handlers who show these signs are reassigned to non-child-facing duties or, in cases of severe attachment, dismissed from the Order entirely.

The Order understands that Handlers are human. The Order does not punish Handlers for feeling. The Order simply ensures that those feelings never compromise the work.

# BOOK EIGHT: THE INSTRUCTOR'S ROLE

## 8.1 The Philosophy of Instruction

The Instructor is not a teacher in the conventional sense. A teacher imparts knowledge. The Instructor forges a mage.

Forging requires heat, pressure, and the willingness to strike. The Instructor must push each child to the exact edge of their capacity—and, when necessary, slightly beyond. A child who never exceeds their limits never discovers their limits. A child who never fails never learns to recover from failure. A child who is never afraid never learns to cast through fear.

This is not cruelty. This is pedagogy. The Instructor's duty is to produce a mage who can borrow responsibly, manage debt effectively, and cast under pressure—including the pressure of combat, the pressure of exhaustion, the pressure of fear. A child trained in comfort will fail in the field. A child trained under duress will survive.

## 8.2 The Instructor's Techniques

**Repetition:** The Instructor assigns a technique. The child performs it. The Instructor says, "Again." The child performs it again. This continues until the child can perform the technique flawlessly, then continues further until the technique is embedded in muscle memory deeper than conscious thought. Repetition is boring. Boredom is a tool. A technique learned in boredom will persist through terror.

**Progressive Overload:** The Instructor gradually increases the difficulty of training—higher temperatures for Fire-mages, drier air for Water-mages, thinner atmosphere for Wind-mages, heavier stone for Earth-mages, higher voltage for Lightning-mages. The child adapts. The Instructor increases the load again. The child adapts again. The cycle continues until the child's capacity plateaus. Then the Instructor holds them at that plateau until it becomes their new baseline.

**Controlled Failure:** The Instructor occasionally presents the child with a task they cannot complete—a target too distant, a debt too large, a guard too strong. The child fails. The Instructor observes how the child fails: Do they panic? Do they shut down? Do they grow angry? Do they try anyway? The child's failure-response is more informative than their success-response. A child who fails well—who recalibrates and tries again—is a child who will survive in the field. A child who fails poorly requires remediation.

**The Silence:** After a child completes a difficult working, the Instructor says nothing. The child stands, breathing hard, debt-scars fresh, waiting for acknowledgment. The Instructor remains silent. The silence forces the child to evaluate their own performance. Did I do well? Was that enough? What should I have done differently? The child learns to assess themselves. The Instructor's praise, when it eventually comes, is minimal: "Adequate." The child learns that "Adequate" from a silent Instructor is the highest compliment available.

**The Last Push:** When a child is exhausted, when their debt-scars are fresh, when they have nothing left—the Instructor asks for one more working. Just one. The child must decide: Do I refuse, preserving my safety but proving myself unreliable? Or do I obey, borrowing from reserves I did not know I had, proving myself dependable but accepting the cost? There is no right answer. There is only the answer that reveals the child's character. The Instructor notes the answer and adjusts the child's training accordingly.

## 8.3 Identifying Potential

The Instructor's most critical skill is identifying which children have the potential for mastery—and which children do not.

Signs of high potential:
- The child asks questions about the underlying principles, not just the techniques.
- The child experiments with variations on taught techniques, even when those variations fail.
- The child recovers quickly from failure and returns to the task without prompting.
- The child's emotional response to pain (debt-scars, training injuries) is controlled—they acknowledge the pain but do not let it stop them.
- The child is angry. Not at the Instructor, not at the Order, but at their own limitations. This anger, if properly channeled, becomes the fuel for exceptional advancement.

Signs of limited potential:
- The child performs techniques exactly as taught and never attempts variation.
- The child requires constant external motivation to complete difficult tasks.
- The child's emotional response to failure is despondency, blame-shifting, or withdrawal.
- The child avoids pain, even when pain is the necessary cost of advancement.
- The child is content. Contentment is the enemy of excellence. A child who is content with their current level will never push beyond it.

The Instructor does not punish children for limited potential. Not every child can be exceptional. The Instructor simply notes the limitation and adjusts the child's training plan accordingly. A child of limited potential can still become a competent placed mage. They will simply never become an archmage.

## 8.4 When Children Break

Some children break under the pressure. The signs are recognizable: cessation of effort, emotional flatness, refusal to speak or eat, blank staring, catatonia. A child who has broken is no longer an asset. They are a casualty.

The Instructor's duty in these cases is to report the break immediately. The child is removed from training and assessed by a Savant and a medical team. Some broken children recover with rest and reduced pressure; these may be reclassified to a lower tier or diverted to Cradle Service. Some do not recover; these are referred to Containment for long-term care or, if the break is severe and irreversible, Permanent Sedation.

The Instructor is not blamed for a child who breaks—unless the Instructor deviated from authorized training protocols. The Order understands that training mages is inherently stressful, that some children are more fragile than their Initial Classification suggested, and that breaks are sometimes unavoidable. The Instructor's performance is evaluated on aggregate outcomes, not individual casualties.
