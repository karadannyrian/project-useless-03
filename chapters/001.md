# Chapter 1: Arrival

**Alexandria**

---

The sea-haze parted like a curtain drawn back from an unwanted stage, and Salthaven resolved itself before her.

She did not think: Here I am. She did not think: The mission begins. She thought instead about the harbor's shape—a crescent, defensible only from the landward side, vulnerable to any fleet that cared to close the arc. Her eyes traced the limestone spine that jutted into the bay, the way the town climbed it in tiers: docks at the waterline, market and trade houses above, a scatter of rooftops rising to a ridge where the road vanished inland. The ropewalk was visible even from this distance—a long, low shed running parallel to the shore, its roof patched in three places.

She catalogued exits by instinct. Narrow streets. Flat roofs with low parapets. A single main road that switched back twice before reaching the ridge.

The town was smaller than she had expected. Less defended.

Less observed, she corrected herself. That was the point. That was why she was here.

The ship's crew moved around her, their voices salt-roughened, their hands busy with ropes and tackle. She had been invisible for the entire crossing—a quiet girl with a single pack, too young to be interesting, too plain to be memorable. She had practiced invisibility the way other girls practice instruments. She was very good at it.

---

Her boots struck stone.

The harbor smelled of fish and hemp and salt-crusted wood, with the sour undernote of low tide. She threaded through dockworkers hauling nets and fishmongers arranging their morning catch on beds of kelp. Her pack was light. Her posture was unremarkable—shoulders relaxed, gait unhurried. She was a junior escort for a minor merchant house, arriving ahead of her employers to prepare for their trade season. She wore that identity like a coat that fit well enough. It was not her first coat. It would not be her last.

She registered faces without interest—the automatic categorization of someone trained to identify threats before they identified her. A one-armed fisherman mending a net. A boy of maybe twelve unloading crates, his movements quick and practiced. A woman in a dark shawl, her back turned, her hands busy with something Alexandria could not see.

No one looked at her twice.

Good.

---

The climb into Salthaven proper took her through the market square, still sparse at this hour. She noted the stalls that would fill by midday, the positions of the permanent structures, the alleys that cut between them. The Factorage tavern squatted at the square's edge, its iron sign—a set of scales with one pan broken off—creaking in the salt breeze. She filed the name, the location, the number of windows. Mercenaries took contracts there. She would need to visit, eventually. Not today.

The ropewalk dominated the lower town, its long shed exhaling the smell of tar and hemp. She slowed her pace as she passed it—not obviously, not enough to draw attention. The shed's doors were open. Inside, men walked backward down the length of the building, twisting fibers into rigging, their hands moving in rhythms too practiced to be conscious. The smell was stronger here, sharp and resinous. Above the shed, a narrow window, a shutter slightly ajar.

Someone lived there. She noted the proximity to the harbor, the number of exits, the lack of street-facing windows on the ground floor. A defensible position.

She moved on.

---

The scribe's shop was small, wedged between a chandlery and a boarded-up storefront. The sign read Vara's Scripts in faded gold lettering. Inside: ink, old paper, the dry smell of a room that did not see much sun. Shelves lined the walls, stuffed with ledgers and loose scrolls. A counter separated the public space from the work area, where a woman sat with her back to the door, copying something in a neat, unhurried hand.

Vara was older than Alexandria had expected. Grey hair pulled back from a face that had settled into lines of quiet endurance. Her fingers were stained at the tips—ink, not age. She did not smile. She did not rise. She turned, looked Alexandria over with a gaze that was neither warm nor cold, and said:

"You're the new one."

"Yes."

The debrief was brief. Vara did not offer tea. She did not ask about the crossing. She recited the mission parameters in a voice that suggested she had done this before, many times, and had learned not to invest in the outcome. Observe. Catalog. Report. Do not engage. Alexandria was a probe, not a weapon—a distinction Vara emphasized with the faintest curl of her lip, as if she found the distinction meaningless but was required to make it.

She received a key to a rented room above a cooper's shop on the east side of town. A list of local contacts—names she would memorize and destroy before morning. A schedule for her first week with House Kellan. And a warning, delivered without emphasis:

"Salthaven keeps its secrets. Don't expect them to open for you."

Alexandria nodded. She did not ask what secrets Vara meant. She had been trained to find secrets. She would find them.

---

The Kellan compound sat on the lower slope of the ridge, a modest cluster of buildings around a central courtyard. It had the look of a house with aspirations rather than history—the walls recently whitewashed, the gate new enough that its hinges did not yet creak. A servant led her to a receiving room where Vallus Kellan was waiting.

He was young. Early twenties, she estimated, with the soft hands of someone who had never held a sword in earnest and the eager posture of a man trying to fill a role too large for him. He introduced himself with a full name and title, as if she might not know who he was. He asked her name. She gave it—Alexandria Whittier, the name the foundling house had given her, the name the Order never changed because names were useful. He asked about her experience. She recited her cover: trained in basic escort work, familiar with trade routes, looking for steady employment.

He seemed satisfied. Or at least willing to pretend.

He assigned her a room in the compound, a schedule, a place in the house's escort rotation. There was an expedition to the outer islands in three days—supplies, payroll, a mining outpost the house was hoping to bring into its trade network. She would be part of the escort detail.

"It's not dangerous," Vallus said, with the confidence of someone who had never been in danger. "Just routine."

Alexandria nodded. She had read the reports on the route. She knew it was not routine.

She was now a junior escort for House Kellan. The identity settled over her like a second coat. It fit. It did not warm her.

---

She did not go straight to her room. The light was failing, and the market was thinning, but she walked it anyway—reconnaissance disguised as idleness. The stalls were closing. A fisherwoman hauled baskets of silver catch toward the smokehouse. A spice merchant weighed his remaining stock, scooping cinnamon bark into jars. The air tasted of salt and woodsmoke and the faint sweetness of overripe fruit.

She passed a textile stall where a woman was buying children's clothing—small tunics, a woolen blanket. The woman's hands were wrapped in bandages, the linen clean but worn, covering her palms and knuckles. She counted coins carefully, her voice low. Alexandria noted the bandages without breaking stride, filed the detail: healer, probably, hands marked by work. The woman finished her purchase, tucked the bundle under her arm, and turned away.

Alexandria watched her disappear into a side street, then moved on. A healer buying children's clothes. Unremarkable.

She had a mission to conduct.

---

She stopped outside the tavern with the broken-scale sign. The window was smeared with salt and old smoke, but she could see through it well enough. Inside: a low-ceilinged room, tables of scarred wood, a fire burning in a hearth too large for the space. Mercenaries drank in clusters, their weapons propped within reach. A thin clerk wrote in a ledger at a corner table, his pen moving in precise, unhurried strokes. A heavier man—the guildmaster, she assumed—sat at a table near the back, eating walnuts from a clay bowl and watching the room with the lazy attention of someone who had been watching the same room for twenty years.

She catalogued the regulars. The Iron Rib fighter in the corner, his shoulders too broad for his chair. The Red Coin duelist near the fire, her blade on the table beside her cup. The Unorthodox scrappers playing dice by the bar, their movements quick and nervous.

And one man, alone, near the back wall.

He was not drinking. He was not playing dice. He sat with his back to the wall, facing the door, his hands gloved despite the warmth of the hearth. Long sleeves, despite the season. His stillness was not rest—it was readiness, the kind of stillness she had been trained to recognize because she had been trained to embody it. He was watching the door.

She did not know his face. She did not know his name. But something in her chest tightened—a half-second of recognition that she could not explain and did not try to.

She did not enter. She was not ready to be seen yet.

She filed him under interesting and walked on.

---

The cooper's shop was dark when she reached it. She climbed the stairs to the rented room, unlocked the door, and stepped inside. The room was small: a cot, a washstand, a window that faced the street. She checked the window first—the latch, the sightlines, the drop to the alley below. Then the door, the lock, the gap at the threshold. The walls were thin. She could hear the cooper moving in the shop below, the scrape of a tool on wood, the creak of floorboards.

She sat on the cot. Removed her boots.

The quiet was familiar. It was the same quiet she had lived in her whole life—the quiet of dormitories, of training floors, of the Hollowing where children learned to be empty. She did not have a word for loneliness. She had a word for readiness.

She was ready.

But she could not stop thinking about the man with the gloves. The way he watched the door. The way his stillness mirrored hers. She had seen a hundred mercenaries, a thousand fighters, and none of them had made her pause like that. She did not know why. She did not like not knowing.

She lay down. The cooper's work had stopped. The town was silent.

Tomorrow, she would report to the Kellan compound. Tomorrow, she would begin cataloguing in earnest. Tomorrow, she would see the gloved man again—at the Factorage, or on the docks, or in the market—and she would observe him, and she would file him, and she would understand what had made her pause.

Tonight, she was still a stranger in a town that kept its secrets.

She did not sleep for a long time.
