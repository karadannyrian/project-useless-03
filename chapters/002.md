# Chapter 2: First Rounds

**Alexandria**

---

Alexandria rose before the bell.

The Kellan compound was still, the courtyard grey with pre-dawn light. She dressed in the quiet, checked the room's exits by habit, and stepped into the yard. A few servants were already moving—a woman drawing water from the well, a boy sweeping the gatehouse steps. They did not look at her. She did not look at them.

She walked the perimeter of the compound, her boots whispering on packed earth. The low wall on the eastern side. The gate that opened onto the switchback road. The stables where three horses and a mule stood in the dark, their breath fogging in the chill. She counted the guards—two at the main gate, one walking a slow circuit of the wall. Their equipment was adequate. Their posture was not. The one on the wall yawned as she passed, his jaw cracking wide, his eyes half-closed against the grey.

She filed the yawn under liability and continued walking.

---

The dining hall was small, its table set for six. Vallus was already there, stirring a cup of tea he had not drunk. He brightened when he saw her—a reflexive lifting of the brow, as if surprise and pleasure were the same thing to him.

"You're up early. Good. That's good." He gestured for her to sit.

She did.

He talked while she ate. About the house. About the trade routes. About the expedition in two days. He used the word routine three times, each repetition shaving a little more weight from the word. Each time his hand drifted toward his cup and stopped, as if the tea had forgotten to be warm enough.

She asked about the escort complement.

He told her: four house guards, two junior escorts including herself, and he was considering hiring additional muscle from the Factorage. "The roads have been—" He hesitated, his fingers finding the edge of his sleeve. "The reports say bandit activity has been higher than usual. Nothing to worry about. Just a precaution."

She nodded. She did not tell him that bandit activity in this region had been higher than usual for eight months. She did not tell him that the last three caravans on the mining road had been hit, or that one of them had lost half its cargo and two escorts. He would not ask how she knew. He was not the kind of man who asks how people know things.

---

She left the compound and walked the town again, this time with purpose.

The mission parameters were clear: observe, catalog, report. She began with the streets—naming them in her mind, mapping the connections between the market square, the harbor, and the residential tiers. She noted which doors were reinforced, which windows had iron bars, which alleys dead-ended, and which offered passage through to the next street. She noted the baker, the smith, the apothecary. She noted a boarding house with a widow's walk that commanded a view of the entire lower town. A good observation post. A bad place to be cornered.

She passed the textile stall. The woman with the bandaged hands was not there. The stall was closed, its shutters drawn, the morning light catching the grain of the wood.

Alexandria did not pause. She filed the absence and walked on.

---

She entered the tavern this time.

The door was heavy, the hinges greased—no squeal to announce a visitor. Inside, the low-ceilinged room was emptier than it had been at night. A few men drinking at the bar, their voices low and liquid. The thin clerk writing at his corner table, his pen scratching like a mouse in the walls. The fire was banked, the hearth exhaling the smell of cold ash.

The guildmaster was not present.

She walked to the bar and ordered water. The barkeep—a woman with scarred knuckles and a braid of grey hair—poured it without comment, without curiosity. Alexandria paid. She sat at a table near the wall, facing the door, and let her gaze drift across the room with the aimless weight of someone who had nowhere to be and nothing to see.

The clerk did not look up. The drinkers did not look at her.

But she heard a name. Not spoken to her—the barkeep, leaning across the bar toward one of the drinkers, her voice a rough whisper meant to carry no further than the man's ear. Hark took the Ridge Road contract. Graen's annoyed. Said he wanted to wait for something better.

The drinker grunted.

The barkeep shrugged. The conversation moved on.

Hark.

She filed the name. The gloved man had the stillness of a fighter. The name might be his. It might not. She would know when she saw him again.

---

She walked past the ropewalk on her way back to the compound.

The shed doors were open, the workers at their stations, their hands moving in that ancient, unconscious rhythm. The smell of tar and hemp wrapped around her, thick as a second skin. She slowed—just enough—and glanced up at the narrow window above.

The shutter was still ajar.

Nothing moved behind it.

She continued walking.

---

She stopped at the scribe's shop to deliver an initial report—a verbal summary, nothing written. Vara listened without interruption, her hands still moving across the ledger she was copying, the ink drying in her wake.

When Alexandria finished, Vara said, "You've been thorough."

"Yes."

Vara set down her pen. She turned, and for a moment her gaze was not the gaze of a handler but something older, something that had seen young agents come and go like seasons. "There's a difference between thorough and visible. Make sure you know which one you're being."

Alexandria did not ask what Vara meant.

She knew. She had been trained to be invisible. The gloved man had made her pause. The pause was visible. The pause was a mistake.

She filed the correction and left.

---

Vallus found her in the courtyard after supper.

The light had gone amber, the shadows stretching long across the packed earth. He had a list in his hand, a sheaf of papers detailing the expedition's route, cargo, and personnel. He handed it to her without preamble, his fingers trembling slightly—from eagerness or anxiety, she could not tell.

"I've hired additional escorts. Three from the Factorage." He paused, searching for a name among the many in his head. "One of them—Hark. A mercenary. Class C. The guildmaster said he's reliable."

Alexandria looked at the list.

The name was there, in the same precise hand she had seen in the clerk's ledger—neat, unhurried, each letter formed with the economy of someone who wrote for a living.

Hark. Blades.

She handed the list back. "When do we leave?"

"Day after tomorrow. Dawn."

She nodded. Vallus lingered a moment, his mouth opening as if he wanted to say something else—some warning, some encouragement, some human thing that would have made him less a mark and more a man. He did not. He turned and walked away, his footsteps fading into the amber light.

Alexandria stood alone in the courtyard.

The gloved man had a name. She had heard it before she was told it. The name was Hark.

She would observe him. She would file him.

She would understand what had made her pause.
