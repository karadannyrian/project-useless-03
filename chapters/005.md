# Chapter 5: Assessment

**Hark**

---

Hark took the contract because Graen was annoyed and the pay was adequate and he had nothing better to do.

That was what he told himself. He had been telling himself versions of this for twenty-two years, and he had never once believed it.

---

The Factorage was quiet at noon.

Verrol was writing in his ledger, his pen moving with the same unhurried precision it had moved with every day Hark had been coming here. Graen was eating walnuts at his corner table, the shells cracking between his teeth like small bones. Hark sat at his usual table—back to the wall, facing the door—and waited for his drink.

The barkeep brought it. She had scarred knuckles and a braid of grey hair, and she had never once asked him about the gloves. He liked her for that.

"Ridge Road," she said. "Heard it went sour."

"Bandits."

"Heard you came back."

"I came back."

She nodded. She did not ask how many had not. She knew how to read the answer in the silence between words. She had been a mercenary herself, once—he could tell by the way she moved, the way her eyes tracked the room, the way her scars mirrored his without matching them.

She walked away. Hark drank his water. The ice in his chest did not thaw.

He did not want to think about the ambush. He thought about it anyway.

---

The coral road. The bottleneck. The first bolt taking Tem in the throat—a good shot, clean; the bandits had known what they were doing. The crossbow woman going down before she could reload. The two gate guards dropping like sacks of grain. The boy Lennis frozen with his sword half-drawn, his face blank with the particular terror of someone who had never seen violence and was discovering, in real time, that it was nothing like the stories.

And the girl.

He had noticed her before the fight. She was too still. The other escorts had shifted in their saddles, adjusted their gear, talked to fill the silence. She had done none of that. She had ridden with a quiet that was not peace but readiness, her eyes moving across the terrain with the same automatic cataloguing he had been performing since before she was born.

When the ambush came, she did not freeze. She did not scream. She put a blade in the first bandit's thigh with the precision of someone who had done it ten thousand times and could do it ten thousand more. Red Coin. The Closing Strike. He recognized the form, and something cold opened in his stomach.

She was Order-trained.

He had not let himself think about it during the fight. There had been no time. He had been too busy killing—using the wagons as cover, the coral as deflection, the bodies of the other escorts as obstacles because they were dead already and the living needed every advantage. He had not thought about the girl. He had simply covered her retreat when she needed covering, killed the man at her back when she could not see him, and moved on.

But he had seen her. The economy of her motion. The precision. The way she had looked at him afterward—not with the blank terror of Lennis or the hollow shock of Vallus, but with something else. Something assessing. Something that recognized what he was because she had been trained to recognize it.

He should be afraid of her.

He was not.

He did not know what that meant.

---

Vallus Kellan walked into the Factorage like a man who had never been in a mercenary tavern and was trying very hard to look like he had.

He stopped just inside the door, his eyes adjusting to the dimness, his hands twitching at his sides. He saw Hark and walked toward him with the deliberate stride of someone who had rehearsed this conversation and was already forgetting his lines.

"Mercenary Hark."

Hark did not stand. "Vallus Kellan."

"I wanted to—" Vallus stopped. Swallowed. Sat down across from Hark without being invited. "I wanted to thank you. For what you did. On the road. You saved my life. You saved—" He paused, searching for the name. "Alexandria. The junior escort. You saved both of us."

Hark said nothing.

"The house is grateful. I am grateful. And I wanted to—" Vallus's hand went to his sleeve, found the edge, and worried it. "I have a proposal. A contract. Not for a caravan. For her."

Hark's hand stopped on his cup. "For her."

"Alexandria. She's good. Better than the others. But you saw what happened. She needs training. Real training. The kind the academies don't teach." Vallus leaned forward, his eagerness momentarily overriding his nerves. "I've seen you fight. You know things she needs to know. The house will pay your standard rate, plus a retainer. You'll have access to our safehouse in Salthaven—no questions, no records. Your name stays off the books. You train her, you get paid, and you walk away whenever you want."

Hark looked at him. Then past him—at the door, at the window, at the exits he had catalogued before he sat down.

The numbers were good. The safehouse was better—a place off the Factorage's ledgers, somewhere to go if things went wrong. The anonymity was best of all. Vallus was offering him a contract that left no trail.

He should refuse. The girl was too watchful. Too precise. There was something wrong about her, something that did not fit the merchant escort cover. She was Order-trained—he was sure of it now—and training her meant spending time with her, and spending time with her meant exposing himself, and exposing himself was the one thing twenty-two years of survival had taught him never to do.

But she had killed clean. She had not frozen. She had looked at him afterward with that assessing gaze, and she had not been afraid.

He remembered being fifteen. He remembered Joel. He remembered what it felt like to be alone and untrained and surviving on instinct. Joel had not had to teach him. Joel had been dying, and talking was all he had left, and the talking had saved Hark's life anyway.

He told himself it was the money. He told himself it was the safehouse.

"I'll train her," he said. "Three sessions a week. She does what I say, when I say it. No questions. If she breaks, I'm not responsible. If I leave, you don't follow."

Vallus nodded, relief breaking across his face like a wave. "Yes. Yes, of course. Whatever terms you—"

"I have one more condition."

Vallus stopped. "What?"

"She doesn't know about the safehouse. She doesn't know about the terms. As far as she's concerned, this is a training arrangement. Nothing else."

Vallus blinked. "Why?"

"Because I don't want her to think she owes me anything." Hark picked up his cup. "She doesn't."

---

He walked back to the ropewalk through the late afternoon light.

The market was thinning, the stalls closing—the same rhythms he had watched for three years and would probably watch for three more. The ropewalk shed was dark. The workers had gone. He climbed the stairs to his room, unlocked the door, and stood in the threshold.

The room was small. A cot. A washstand. A lockbox. A window that faced the harbor. He had lived here for three years, and it still looked like he had moved in yesterday.

He sat on the cot. He removed one glove—the left one, the one with the crooked finger—and looked at his hand.

The frost-scars were the same as they had been yesterday, and the day before, and the day before that. White lines on white skin, running from his fingertips to his wrist, denser at the knuckles, thinner as they climbed. The ledger of a lifetime of borrowed heat, written in flesh.

He had not used magic during the ambush. He had been tempted. When the second volley came, when the crossbow woman went down and the boy froze and the girl was fighting two men at once—his hand had heated. The fire had risen, instinctive, a door cracking open in the back of his mind.

He had forced it shut. The witnesses were too many. The cost was too high. He had done it the hard way instead, and three people were alive who would not have been if he had been a second slower.

He pulled the glove back on.

The girl was Order-trained. She had not reported him yet. She had looked at him with that assessing gaze, and she had not been afraid, and she had not run.

He did not know what she was. He did not know what she wanted.

He was going to train her anyway.

---

He lay back on the cot.

The ceiling was the same ceiling he had been staring at for three years. The cracks had not moved. The water stain in the corner had not spread. He thought about the girl. He thought about Joel. He thought about the way the fire had risen in his hand and the way he had forced it down.

He did not sleep. He never slept well after a fight. But he closed his eyes, and the ice in his chest did not thaw, and the silence was the same silence that had followed him for twenty-two years.

Before the light failed entirely, he opened the lockbox.

He did not do this often. The box contained the few things he had carried with him across twenty-two years and a dozen identities. Joel's sword was already on his back, where it always was. What remained in the box was smaller: a spare blade, a pouch of coins in three currencies, a strip of leather he used to repair his gloves.

And a dried flower, pressed flat, the color long since faded to a pale, papery brown.

He did not know why he kept it. He had told himself, for seventeen years, that it was a reminder—of a job completed, a contract fulfilled. A riverbank in the Confederacy. A girl with bandaged hands who had asked him no questions and expected no answers.

He had told himself it meant nothing.

He had been lying.

He closed the lockbox. He did not touch the flower. He never touched the flower.

---

The light failed. The harbor grew dark. Somewhere below, the ropewalk's timbers creaked with the cold.

Hark lay on his cot with his gloves on and his eyes open. He did not think about the girl with the bandaged hands. He did not think about the girl with the Red Coin precision. He did not think about the fire that was always waiting, just behind the door in his mind.

He thought about nothing.

He was very good at thinking about nothing.
