# Chapter 6: The Healer

**Renne**

---

The market was loud and bright and full of people who did not know they were being watched.

Renne moved through them with the ease of long practice, her basket over her arm, her bandaged hands tucked against her sleeves. She had been shopping at this market for fifteen years. The vendors knew her. The children knew her. The old women who sat at the square's edge and commented on everyone who passed knew her as the healer who asked no payment, the woman with the sad eyes and the kind hands, the one who hummed tunes that made the air smell like rain.

They did not know the rest. She had made sure of that.

She stopped at the bread stall. The baker's wife wrapped two loaves in brown paper and refused her coin for the third time that month. Renne thanked her, tucked the bread into her basket, and moved on. The fishmonger had saved her a cut of the morning's catch. The spice merchant pressed a small twist of cinnamon into her palm when no one was looking. The market took care of her because she took care of the market, and the exchange was never spoken aloud.

She was at the textile stall, examining a bolt of undyed wool, when Marta found her.

Marta did not announce herself. She never did. She simply appeared at Renne's elbow, a solid grey presence in a faded blue coat, her face as unreadable as the sea.

"There's a new one," Marta said. "Order. Young. Girl."

Renne's hand did not pause on the wool. "I know."

"You've seen her."

"Yesterday. Near the Kellan compound." Renne set down the bolt of wool and nodded to the stallkeeper, who was pretending not to listen. "She's been walking the town. Cataloguing."

"She's good at it," Marta said. "I watched her for an hour this morning. She mapped the market, the harbor, the residential tiers. She noted the boarding house with the widow's walk. She noted the apothecary. She noted the alley that dead-ends behind the smithy." A pause. "She's trained. Better than the last one."

The last one had been a Mage-Detector, three years ago, sweeping through on a routine census. Renne had moved the children to the outer islands and kept her head down, and the Detector had found nothing and left. This one was different. This one was not sweeping. This one was watching.

"How old?" Renne asked.

"Fifteen. Maybe sixteen. Hard to tell. She moves like someone who's been doing this longer than she's been alive."

Renne felt something shift in her chest—a premonition, a warning, the quiet bell that had kept her alive for fifteen years. She ignored it. She was good at ignoring things.

"Keep watching her," she said. "Tell Kel to listen. Tell Doren to keep the Petrel ready. If she gets too close to the safehouses, we move the children."

Marta nodded. She did not ask what "too close" meant. She knew. She had been doing this as long as Renne had.

---

She saw her at noon.

The market was at its peak, the aisles thick with bodies, the air loud with haggling and gossip and the cry of gulls overhead. Renne was at the well, filling her waterskin, when she looked up and saw the girl standing at the edge of the square.

She was young. Younger than Renne had expected—fifteen, as Marta had said, or close to it. Dark hair pulled back from a face that was sharp and watchful and very still. She was not shopping. She was not talking to anyone. She was standing with her back to a wall and her eyes moving across the crowd with the steady, automatic rhythm of someone cataloguing threats.

Renne knew that rhythm. She had learned it in the Cradle, before her escape, when watching meant surviving and missing a detail meant punishment. She saw it now in the set of the girl's shoulders, the angle of her jaw, the way her hands hung loose at her sides—ready, always ready.

The girl turned her head.

For a moment—a fraction of a breath—their eyes met across the square.

The girl's eyes were grey.

Renne's hand slipped on the waterskin. Water skyrocketed across her fingers, cold and sudden, and she looked down at her hands, at the bandages darkening with moisture, and when she looked up again the girl was gone.

She stood at the well for a long time. The water dripped from her fingers onto the stone. Her heart was beating too fast. She could not explain why. She had seen Order agents before. She had been watched before. She had never felt the ground shift beneath her feet because a stranger had grey eyes.

She told herself it was nothing. A coincidence. A girl with grey eyes in a town full of strangers.

She did not believe it.

---

She went to Seris's house because Seris's house was where she went when she did not know what else to do.

The kitchen was small and warm and smelled of bread and dried herbs. Seris was at the table, shelling peas into a clay bowl, her fingers moving with the same unhurried competence she brought to everything. She looked up when Renne entered, and whatever she saw in Renne's face made her set down the bowl.

"What's happened?"

Renne sat down across from her. She did not speak for a long moment. She was still holding the waterskin, she realized. She set it on the table.

"There's a girl," she said. "Order. Young. She's been in town for two days. Marta says she's trained."

Seris waited.

"I saw her. At the market. She looked at me." Renne's hand went to her chest, pressing against the place where the sudden tightness had lodged. "She has grey eyes."

Seris's hands went still.

"Grey eyes," she repeated.

"Hark's eyes."

The words hung in the air between them. Renne had never spoken them aloud. She had never told anyone except Seris about Hark, about the cottage, about the daughter she had surrendered on a winter night seventeen years ago. The secret had lived in her chest like a second heart, beating quietly beneath everything else.

Seris said, carefully, "You think she's—"

"I don't know. I don't know anything. She could be anyone. The Order collects children from everywhere. Grey eyes aren't—" She stopped. Her voice had risen. She forced it down. "Grey eyes aren't proof."

"No," Seris said. "They're not."

"But you think it too."

Seris was silent for a moment. Then she reached across the table and took Renne's hand—the bandaged one, the one that had healed seventy-three children and never once healed itself.

"I think," Seris said, "that you have been looking for your daughter for seventeen years. And I think the universe is not always kind, but it is sometimes precise."

Renne closed her eyes. The tightness in her chest had not eased. It had grown, spreading outward from the place where the girl's grey eyes had met hers—an ache that felt almost like hope and almost like grief and exactly like both at once.

"I can't—" She stopped. Started again. "If she's Order, she's here to find us. To find the network. If I approach her, I expose everything."

"Yes."

"If I don't approach her, I may never know."

"Yes."

Renne opened her eyes. Seris was watching her with the steady patience of someone who had seen too much to be surprised by anything but was still capable of being moved.

"What do I do?" Renne whispered.

Seris squeezed her hand. "You do what you have always done. You protect the children first. You protect the network. And you wait." She released Renne's hand and picked up the bowl of peas. "The truth has a way of finding us, whether we want it to or not."

---

She did not mean to walk past the ropewalk.

She told herself she was taking the long way back to the safehouse—checking the dead drops, making sure the courier had left the signal that the children on the outer islands were safe. All of this was true. None of it was the reason she walked past the ropewalk.

The shed was quiet. The workers had gone. The narrow window above was dark, the shutter closed. He was not home, or he was home and sitting in the dark, which was also something he did. She knew his habits. She had been avoiding him for three years and studying him for just as long, and the contradiction did not escape her.

She stood on the street below the window for a long moment. The last time she had spoken to him, she was seventeen years old and he was sixteen, and she had kissed his cheek and told him the river remembers. She had not known she was pregnant. She had not known she would spend the next seventeen years carrying the weight of a daughter she had surrendered and a man she had left behind.

She knew now. She had known for a long time. And she was still standing on the street below his window, still not knocking, still not speaking the words that had been lodged in her throat since the night she walked away from him in the snow.

The shutter did not open. The door did not open.

She turned and walked back toward Seris's house. She was not ready to warm. She had not been ready for seventeen years.

---

The safehouse was a fisherman's cottage on the eastern edge of town, its back door opening onto a narrow inlet where a boat could dock without being seen from the harbor.

Three children were inside: a boy of eight with a Water Gate that made the air around him perpetually damp; a girl of six with the first stirrings of a Wind Gate that ruffled the curtains when she cried; and an infant whose Gate had not yet opened but whose mother had been Sensitive and whose father had sold her to the Order for the compensation fee before Renne's courier could intercept.

The boy was drawing on a scrap of paper. The girl was asleep on a pallet in the corner. The infant was in the arms of the safehouse keeper, a woman named Lirra who had been with the network for eight years and had never once asked Renne about the scars on her hands.

Renne checked the boy first. His Gate was stable. His guarding was improving. The dampness in the air around him was less than it had been the week before. He was learning to hold the door shut. She told him he was doing well. He beamed at her, and the air warmed by a fraction, and the ice in her chest shifted.

The girl was dreaming. The curtains stirred gently with her breath. Renne knelt beside the pallet and pressed two fingers to the child's forehead—reading her temperature, her pulse, the quiet hum of her unguarded reservoir. The girl's Gate was still forming. In a few months, she would need to learn to guard. For now, she needed sleep, food, and safety, and Renne could give her all three.

The infant was the hardest. She was too young to understand why she had been taken from her mother. Too young to know that the Order would have raised her as an asset. Too young to know that the woman holding her now was not the woman who had given birth to her but was, in every way that mattered, the woman who had chosen to keep her safe.

Renne took the infant from Lirra. She held her against her chest, feeling the small, steady heartbeat against her own. The infant's eyes were dark.

Not grey. Not his.

She closed her eyes. She hummed the old Water-mage lullaby, the one her mother had sung before the Order came, the one she had sung to Alexandria on the night she was born. The air in the cottage cooled and dampened. The infant sighed. The boy stopped drawing. The girl smiled in her sleep.

Marta was wrong about one thing, Renne thought. The Order girl was not the only one watching. Renne had been watching too—watching for grey eyes, for seventeen years, in every child she saved, every stranger she passed, every face in every market in every town she had ever fled through.

She had found them. They were here. They were watching her back.

She did not know what to do with that.

She held the infant, hummed the lullaby, and let the ice in her chest stay where it was, because thawing it would mean feeling everything she had been refusing to feel for seventeen years.

She was not ready.
