# Chapter 8: The Spar

**Alexandria**

---

Hark was already there.

That was the first difference. The training ground was empty behind the Kellan compound—packed earth, olive trees that had not borne fruit in years, the stable wall throwing a low shadow across the western corner—and Hark stood at the center of it with his long blade sheathed across his back and his gloved hands loose at his sides, watching her come through the gate. Not waiting. Watching.

She filed the distinction.

The morning was cold, the earth still holding last night's rain in the seams between the paving and in the hollows her boots had left during the first session. He did not greet her. He said: "You've drilled the stance. You've drilled the step. Today we find out if you can use them."

He drew the practice blade—wood, weighted—and the air between them changed.

---

They circled.

His footwork was the Void Step, which she now recognized immediately: small shuffling adjustments that kept him just outside her striking range, never committing, his weight centered in the arches of his feet and nowhere else. She pressed forward with Red Coin precision. Short thrusts—economical, no telegraph. His blade redirected hers with minimal movement. Not blocking. Redirecting. The geometry of each deflection was different and the difference was the point; he was reading her before she was reading him, absorbing the information in her attack and using it before she knew she'd given it.

She was faster than him. She should be landing.

She wasn't.

Every opening she saw closed by the time she moved. Not retreating—repositioning, by inches, his weight shifting in the same small shuffles, the blade always exactly where it needed to be. The Void Step was not a footwork pattern. It was a philosophy of never being where the attack expected. Trying to hit him felt like trying to cut water, which was the worst analogy her mind had produced in fifteen years of tactical vocabulary, but it was also accurate. She was breathing hard. He wasn't.

She tried the feint—Red Coin's standard entry, a high-line thrust to draw the parry, then the true strike low. He didn't parry. He slid outside the high-line attack and her blade passed through empty air and she was extended and off-balance and his practice edge rested against her ribs before she could recover.

"Again," he said.

---

She reset.

The Void Step beneath her feet felt like wearing the wrong boots—the constant hovering, the refusal to plant, weight distributed to the arches until her calves ached with the unfamiliar demand. She was built for commitment. Red Coin was commitment. Choose the moment, deliver the conclusion, finish. The Void Step asked her to never arrive, only approach, which felt like a moral failing until she began to understand that it was not an aesthetic position. It was a survival position.

He circled. She circled. The packed earth was marked with the prints from the first session—hers, overlapping each other, the pattern of a fighter who had spent the morning arriving and arriving and arriving.

He feinted left. She didn't bite.

Feinted again—a small dip in the shoulder that any trained fighter would recognize as preparation for a high-line attack. She held.

She watched his weight shift, waiting. He feinted a third time, and this time the weight transfer was fractionally deeper than before, an overcorrection—maybe intentional, maybe genuine—but it was real. The hip moved before the shoulder, which meant the blade was committed before the body, which meant there was a window. She struck. Her practice blade tapped his ribs, clean contact, and she withdrew before he could trap it.

He looked at her.

Something in his eyes—not a smile, not approval, something colder than both and more useful than either. Recognition, maybe. The way a trained fighter looks at another trained fighter who has just proved it.

"That's the difference," he said, "between fighting and surviving."

---

The second exchange lasted longer.

He was teaching through pressure rather than instruction—each round adjusted by a half-degree of difficulty, her range slightly shorter, his adjustments slightly smaller, forcing her to recalibrate rather than repeat. She stopped trying to impose Red Coin's single-exchange philosophy and started watching his weight. That was what the Void Step demanded from its opponent: not speed, not aggression, but attention. She had been trained for attention. She was good at attention.

She stopped thinking in terms of openings and started thinking in terms of commitments. Every time he committed—however slightly, however briefly—there was a window. She learned to let the small commitments pass and wait for the real ones.

She was extending on a follow-through when he stepped in and corrected her.

His gloved hand closed over hers on the hilt—adjusting the angle of her wrist, the line from knuckle to pommel, the recovery position after a strike. It was a routine correction. He had done it four times in the first session.

The leather was warm.

She registered this automatically, part of the same continuous assessment she ran on every contact—the warmth above his body temperature, the specific heat-retention of leather that had been worn long enough to memorize its owner. His fingers adjusted her grip by two degrees and started to pull away.

Beneath the warmth: nothing.

She didn't react outwardly. Her expression stayed neutral, her grip compliant under his adjustment. But her internal ledger updated in the same silent, single beat it had updated during the first session, and the contradiction was the same contradiction, and she was no closer to resolving it. His combat form was not ambiguous. On the coral road she had watched him use the wagons, the terrain, the bodies of the dead as obstacles in a way that meant twenty years of survival, and the survival of a man who had been hunted. His footwork was exactly the Void Step he was teaching her, except older and more deeply embedded, and the Void Step was in the Silencer Protocols as the primary counter-movement against a casting arc. Which meant someone had trained him to avoid mages. Which meant he had reason to avoid mages. Which meant—

His hand was withdrawn. He stepped back to striking range.

She filed it again under *requires further observation* because she had no category for the void, because a Fire Gate she could not feel was a logical impossibility that the evidence kept insisting upon, and because she was a Silencer and Silencers did not act on flawed equations.

"Your follow-through is rolling the blade," he said. "You're losing the line at the end of the stroke. It costs you the recovery position."

"Show me," she said.

He did.

---

The session ended without announcement.

She noticed it in the way the tempo changed—his adjustments becoming corrections rather than tests, the rhythms slowing from sparring pace to drill pace to stillness. She sheathed her practice blade. He sheathed his with the same loose, one-handed motion he always used, the hilt rising over his shoulder.

She said: "Why do you fight the way you do?"

He was wiping down his blade. He didn't look up.

"What I mean is—" She stopped. The question was imprecise. She tried again. "The waiting. The refusal to commit. You could close faster. You don't."

He finished with the cloth. He was quiet for a moment—not withholding, she had learned to read the difference, this was a man who paused before he said true things.

"The people who trained me," he said, "trained me to die well." He hung the cloth from his belt. "I decided I'd rather live ugly."

She stood in the cold morning air with the practice blade at her side and held those words in her chest, where they sat alongside the void beneath his glove and the Void Step she could still feel in the arches of her feet and the fact that she had not written any of this in the report she had filed yesterday. She had left it out. She kept leaving it out. She had not examined why.

She filed *unexamined omission* under a heading she hadn't used before, which meant it was new, which meant something had changed.

---

Vallus was waiting near the stables.

She saw him before he saw her—he was carrying the ledger he always carried, his hair wind-blown, his coat unbuttoned at the collar. When he looked up, his face did the thing it always did: brightened, briefly and reflexively, like a window catching sun.

"Alexandria." He fell in beside her as she walked toward the gate. "How was the session?"

"Productive."

"He stopped by the house office this morning. To sign the payroll voucher." Vallus's fingers moved on the ledger's spine, tapping. "He said you had potential. I've heard him tell the town guards they aren't dead yet. It's not the same thing."

She registered this. A small movement in her chest she didn't name.

They walked. The courtyard smelled of wet lime and horse. Vallus was quiet for three steps—his particular silence, the one that meant he was deciding whether to say something.

He said it.

"I still think about the ridge road." His voice had dropped. She tilted her chin to catch it. "How I froze when the horse went down. How I couldn't find the seal on the map." He looked at her—not the way he usually looked at her, which was slightly sideways, slightly eager, monitoring her expression for signs of approval. This was direct. "I want to be useful, Alexandria. To the house. To you."

"You are useful," she said. It was not flattery; it was assessment. He had hired Hark. He had given her the escort rotation without asking questions she could not answer. He had, in the ways that mattered to the mission, been exactly what she needed.

His face cleared. The smile came back, brief and crooked.

"I'm glad you're here," he said. "Most junior escorts would have taken the silver and found a ship after the ambush. You didn't."

She did not know what to do with the phrase *glad you're here*. In fifteen years of foundling houses and training halls and Handlers with clean hands who spoke in scripted responses, no one had measured her presence against their own need for her to remain in it. She stood still, her arms at her sides.

His hand reached—a light pressure at her sleeve, tentative, the tips of his fingers only—and then withdrew immediately. He looked briefly startled by his own hand, which she found almost legible: the gesture that surprised the person making it. He turned and walked toward the main house with the hurried stride of someone who had said more than he meant and was now outpacing it.

Alexandria stood between the stables and the wall.

The salt-drizzle was thickening toward rain but she didn't move. She was aware of two things at the same point in her skin, separated by their geography: the heat of Hark's gloved hand on her right knuckles, the cold of the void beneath it. And the light, tentative pressure of Vallus's fingers on her left sleeve, brief as a question.

She filed both under *unresolved interpersonal friction* because that was the only category she had and she knew, filing them, that the category was wrong.

She walked toward her room.

---

That evening she sat on the edge of the cot with the leather journal open on her knees, the tallow candle on the washstand throwing a long shadow across the lime-washed wall. The shop below was dark. The cooper's hammer had been silent for hours.

She dipped her pen and wrote.

*Spar conducted. Duration forty minutes. Void Step deployment: adequate under low-pressure conditions, degrades under extended engagement. Primary correction: follow-through line. Tactical principle introduced: commitment as information; wait for opponent's real commitment, not manufactured. Hark's methodology principle-based. He corrects through pressure, not instruction. Outcome: meaningful improvement in range discipline.*

She paused.

The nib of her pen hovered above the cream paper. A drop of iron-gall ink swelled at the split until it threatened the line.

She did not write about the void beneath his glove, which was the same void she had felt during the first session and still had no framework for. She did not write about what his hands were saying when he adjusted her grip. She did not write about *I decided I'd rather live ugly*, which was the truest thing she had heard in Salthaven, which sat in her chest with a weight that felt nothing like data. She did not write about Vallus's hand on her sleeve or the way he looked startled by it, or the phrase *glad you're here*, which was still doing something inside her that the taxonomies she had been given in the Hollowing had no designation for.

She closed the journal.

She blew out the candle. In the dark the sounds of Salthaven came through the thin wall—the groan of rigging in the harbor, the creak of the ropewalk's timbers, the rhythmic grind of the tide along the seawall. She lay down with her boots still on, her fingers resting lightly on the hilt of the knife at her belt.

The two sensations were still on her skin: warmth and absence. Leather and wool.

She turned her face toward the small window where the grey sky had dissolved into solid black. She did not know what she was waiting for. She was fifteen years old and she had been a weapon her entire life and tonight for the first time she felt the weight of what she was carrying as distinct from the weight of her own body, which was new, and which she had no category for either.

She lay in the dark with both things and did not sleep for a long time.
