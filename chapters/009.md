## Chapter 9: The Scribe's Shop

**Hark**

---

The third training session ended with the smell of salt and bruised iron on the docks. Alexandria did not look winded. She never did. Her skin remained pale, her breathing a rhythmic, calculated function. As Hark wiped the grease from his practice sword, she turned to him.

"Walk with me to the scribe's shop," she said.

Her voice had that flat, uninflected quality he had grown used to. It was the tone of someone reading a ledger in an empty room.

"An errand?" Hark asked, sheathing the blade.

"I need to pick up documents for House Kellan," she replied, already turning toward the street. "Training reports."

Hark matched her pace, his boots clicking against the damp cobblestones. Curiosity was a dangerous trait in his line of work, but it was a stubborn one. For three years, he had walked past *Vara's Scripts* a hundred times. He had never set foot inside. He knew the type of people who ran those shops. Usually, they were retired placed mages—the Order's castoffs, set up in quiet towns where they could remain useful without becoming dangerous. The Order did not like loose threads, but it liked wasted assets even less.

He had learned to avoid them. Yet the girl was already ten paces ahead, her spine perfectly straight, her grey wool coat clearing the mud with precise, measured strides. Hark followed.

---

The bell above the door gave a single, dull clink.

Inside, the air was thick with the scent of vinegar-based ink, damp parchment, and the dry, settled dust of a room that rarely saw the sun. It was small, narrow, cluttered. Shelves climbed to the low rafters, sagging under the weight of leather-bound ledgers and tightly rolled scrolls tied with hemp string.

Behind the counter sat a woman. Her grey hair was pulled back with brutal efficiency, pinned tight against her skull. Her fingers were permanently stained an archival black around the knuckles. She was copying text onto a sheet of vellum, her hand moving in a neat, unhurried rhythm that spoke of decades spent under the lamp.

Hark stopped two steps inside the threshold. His posture did not change, but his internal inventory shifted instantly to active threat assessment.

He did not recognize her face, but he recognized her *pattern*.

It was in the way her shoulders were set—a controlled stillness that had nothing to do with peace and everything to do with readiness. It was the specific posture of someone trained to receive an impact. And beneath the smell of old paper, there was something else: a faint, acrid scent of old magic clinging to her clothes like cold smoke. It was so faint that a common soldier would miss it entirely. But Hark had spent twenty-two years keeping his skin intact by detecting exactly that scent.

She was a placed mage. Discarded, most likely, but still bound to the system.

A cold drop of sweat traced the line of his spine.

He was standing in a room with an active Order asset.

The deduction he had dismissed a week ago—the impossible, ridiculous thought he had filed away under *settled*—cracked open like dry timber in his chest. Alexandria was too precise. Her movements were too clean, her watchfulness too absolute. During their first exchange, he had felt that distinct, terrifying void where her Gate should have been. He had talked himself out of the truth because the truth violated everything he knew about the Order. And now the proof was sitting behind the counter, scraping iron gall ink onto calfskin. He had told himself she was a fugitive, a deserter hiding under a merchant's cover.

He was wrong.

The proof was sitting behind the counter, scraping iron gall ink onto calfskin. Vara was her handler. The training reports were not for the Kellan estate; they were for the archives in the Cradle. The girl was exactly what his blood had told him she was from the very first second.

Hark kept his hands loose at his sides. He did not touch his hilt. He stayed near the doorway, calculating the distance to the street, the thickness of the glass in the front window, the blind spots behind the shelves.

Alexandria stepped up to the counter. The exchange was brief, conducted in low, even murmurs. Vara's eyes flicked up. They found Hark.

It was a surgical assessment. Cold, swift, entirely clear. She looked at his shoulders, his boots, the angle of his head. She knew he was no simple mercenary hired to guard a noble's courtyard. Hark looked back, his jaw locked. He knew she was no simple scribe.

Neither of them broke the silence. Neither of them gave a name to what was in the room.

---

Alexandria gathered a small bundle of parchment tied with black silk ribbon. She tucked it into the inner pocket of her coat without inspecting the contents. Vara asked no questions about the man standing by the door. Hark remained a shadow against the frame.

The silence between the three of them stretched, taut as a tripwire.

When Alexandria turned toward the exit, her boots clicking against the floorboards, Hark did not immediately follow. He waited until she cleared the threshold, her attention already fixed on the street outside. Then he stepped closer to the counter.

"The girl," he said. His voice was barely more than a breath, pitched low enough that it would not carry past the glass. "She's Order-trained."

Vara did not stop her pen. The iron nib continued its steady scratch across the vellum. "Is she?"

"You knew."

"I know a lot of things," Vara said, her voice as dry as the paper beneath her hand. "It's my job."

Hark waited. He did not move. The small shop seemed to contract around them, the smell of old ink turning sharp. Vara finished a line, lifted the pen, and looked up at him. Her face was a map of quiet endurance, but deep within her eyes, something shifted. It was not anger. It was a profound, heavy weariness—a grief that had nothing to do with Hark and everything to do with the system that owned them both.

The words formed in Hark's throat before his logic could choke them out. A door opened in his mind that he had spent years barring shut.

"She's not what they made her to be," he said.

Vara watched him for a long, unblinking moment. The lines around her mouth tightened. "No," she said softly. "She's not. None of them are."

Hark turned and left before the silence could demand anything more from him.

---

The grey afternoon light of Salthaven offered no warmth. They walked side by side down the coral-dusted road toward the Kellan compound. Alexandria did not speak, and Hark had no words left to waste.

His mind operated with the cold, mechanical efficiency of a siege engineer. He catalogued every alleyway they passed. He noted the high walls of the fishmarkets, the deep shadows under the eaves of the warehouses, the structural choke points of the main thoroughfare. He was updating his escape routes. Everything he thought he knew about the girl had been inverted.

She was not a deserter running from the whip. She was an active operative. A Silencer. A weapon forged by the Order for the explicit purpose of hunting down and ending mages like him. Every day she had spent in his company, she had been collecting data. She had been compiling her training reports. She was mapping the town, mapping the network, mapping *him*.

And she had not turned him in.

That was the thought that snagged in his chest like a rusty hook. He analyzed it from every angle, and the logic refused to align. She had seen his combat form on the coral road. She had felt the void where his Gate should be—a Silencer was trained specifically to recognize that precise absence, the hollow echo of an unmarked mage who had spent a lifetime hiding his ledger. She had more than enough evidence to hand him over to the Curators.

Yet the ribbon around her reports was still intact. She had filed her updates, and she had deliberately left out the only facts that mattered.

He watched her from the corner of his eye. Her jaw was set, her gaze fixed on the grey sky above the harbor. She looked entirely detached from the mortal peril she had brought to his doorstep. Her grey eyes had a familiar, piercing clarity—a look that reminded him of someone else, a name he refused to bring to the surface of his thoughts.

He did not understand her intent. He did not understand why he was still walking beside her, why his feet were carrying him back to her training court instead of the northern road out of the League. He only knew that he was still here.

---

He left her at the heavy iron gates of the Kellan compound without a parting word. He turned back into the salt-rimed streets and walked until he reached the Factorage.

The tavern was half-empty at this hour, smelling of sour ale and wet tallow. Hark took his usual table in the far corner. Back to the solid oak wall. Face to the main entrance. Both exits within line of sight. He ordered water. When it arrived, it tasted of nothing but iron and river silt.

He laid the findings out on the scarred wood before him, cold and bare.

*First:* The girl was a trained Silencer.

*Second:* She was actively reporting to the Cradle through Vara.

*Third:* She had had multiple opportunities to flag him as an unmarked mage, and she had chosen silence instead.

*Fourth:* Maybe Vara was protecting her. From what, or from whom, remained outside his current data set.

*Fifth:* He had just walked into an active Order outpost and explicitly signaled that he was invested in the fate of their weapon.

The conclusion was non-negotiable. He should run. He should terminate the contract with House Kellan before the sun went down, pack his kit, vanish into the foothills. There were a dozen quiet towns along the border where the Order's eyes were blind. He had done it before. He knew how to disappear.

He looked down at his glass. His hand was perfectly steady.

He did not run. He finished the water, his throat dry. He sat in the dim public solitude of the tavern, the cold settling deep into his bones, while the phantom warmth of her hand—the grip from their training session—kept ghosting against the leather of his empty glove.
