# Chapter 13: The Serpent

**Hark**

---

The contract sat on the table between them—a folded square of oilskin, the kind Graen used for jobs likely to get wet.

Hark did not pick it up. He read it where it lay. Three boats. Four men. A stretch of the outer channel that had been impassable since the first week of the season. The bounty was substantial, which was a word that barely covered it. He noted the names of the fishing collective that had pooled it. Men who could not afford another week.

"Reliable people," Graen said. He was cracking walnuts between his back teeth, each one a small, damp percussion. "Verrol's already putting the list together. I thought of you."

"You thought of me for a serpent hunt."

"I thought of you for the pay rate." Graen set a shell on the table. "You've been sitting in that ropewalk for three weeks on a training contract. I'm not judging. I'm observing."

Hark turned the oilskin over once and set it down. The bounty would cover six months at the ropewalk and leave enough in two currencies for the lockbox, for the possibility of needing to move quickly. The calculation was simple.

That was the reason he gave himself. He filed it.

"I'll take it," he said.

Graen stopped chewing.

"You?"

"Me."

Graen looked at him for a moment, then tapped the oilskin once across the table.

"Dawn. Harbor side. Bring your own rope."

---

He told Vallus that afternoon. The boy was in the courtyard with his ledger open on his arm, the pages lifting in the salt wind. He brightened when he saw Hark, which was a liability Hark had stopped trying to correct.

"The serpent contract," Hark said. "Three days, possibly four. I'm requesting the junior escort as field attachment."

Vallus blinked. "Alexandria?"

"She has good instincts and no battlefield depth. The first will keep her alive. The second will get her killed."

"A serpent hunt is—"

"More dangerous than another ambush on the mining road, where she'll be without me." Hark looked at him. "You hired me to train her."

Vallus opened his mouth. His hand found his sleeve. He closed his mouth, and after a moment he nodded—the jerky motion he always used when he had already decided and just needed his body to finish it.

"Three days," Vallus said.

Hark walked back to the gate. He was bringing her for the field experience. He had filed that reason. He did not examine the others.

---

The *Sea Warden* was a flat-bottomed fishing vessel, wide in the beam, built for shallow outer channels rather than open water. She smelled of blood and salt and the particular sourness of nets left to dry too slowly. Captain Rallis stood at the stern watching his deckhands ready the harpoons. A lean man, weathered to the color of old rope, with the hollow eyes of someone who had spent two weeks sleeping in the same room as a grief he could not set down.

His son had been nineteen. He had told Graen. He had given the collective's money. He was here because standing on the dock watching other men go was something he had already tried.

Jera arrived next—Red Coin, narrow and precise, her blade slung over one shoulder. Fifteen years on the League's southern ports, and the particular stillness of someone who had learned not to be surprised by anything as a precondition of survival. She looked at the boat, the water, Hark.

"Harpoons," she said.

"Two. Your blade for close work."

"Close work on a forty-foot serpent."

"If it comes to that."

She held his eye, then stepped aboard.

Tomas after: Iron Rib, the same short measured tread he always used, the kind of man who never moved faster than necessary because he had learned that speed was usually fear wearing useful clothing. He nodded at Hark. Said nothing about the gloves. He never said anything about the gloves. That was why Hark had put his name on the list.

The two deckhands—brothers, the younger one struggling with a coil of harpoon line—stayed near the stern and spoke to each other in low urgent murmurs. Their fear was visible in the way they moved. Predictable was acceptable. Panic was not.

Alexandria arrived last.

She came down the harbor steps with her short blade at her hip and her pack over one shoulder, her grey wool coat dark with the morning damp. She moved the way she always moved now—weight centered, the Void Step in her stride without her noticing. He had put it there. He watched her read the angle of the gangway, find the gaps in the dockworkers' traffic, and step aboard in a single motion.

She met his eyes. Brief. The way she had been doing it since the scribe's shop.

Something had shifted in her then. He did not know what. She was the same in the training ground, the same in the minimal reports she presumably filed with Vara. She watched him more carefully, that was all. He had been not-knowing it for two weeks.

He turned back to the water.

"Listen," he said. "It's large and it moves fast and it has not been harpooned recently, which means it's going to be surprised and then angry. Two shots at the flanks to slow it, one at the tail if we get position. You," to the deckhands, "chum the water to draw it shallow, then stay on the port side. You do not go into the water for any reason."

He looked at Alexandria. "Stay at my shoulder. Don't commit until you know the line. It uses the tail for distance work. The head is the target but you'll need to draw it out first."

She nodded. She never asked questions during briefings. She had worked that out by the second session.

Rallis released the lines. The *Sea Warden* pulled away from the harbor and the dock fell behind them.

---

Two hours out through grey water.

The outer channel was a scatter of coral atolls and limestone outcrops, the water between them clear and flat in the overcast morning. Hark stood at the bow. He watched the clarity. Clear water was hunting water—everything visible from below as well as above, every shadow a target.

Rallis came to stand beside him after a while. He did not speak immediately. When he did, his voice was the voice of a man reporting weather.

"My boy was nineteen," he said. "Third boat in three weeks. He was first in the water."

Hark said nothing.

"I'm not asking for anything. I wanted someone to know that before we started."

Hark looked at him. The hollow eyes. The deliberate calm of a man who had burned through everything else.

"I know," Hark said.

Rallis went back to the tiller.

The water was very still. Somewhere below, the reef formations ended and the channel floor dropped away into dark water that held nothing visible and everything probable. The birds were gone. All of them, from one moment to the next, simply absent—no call, no wing, no motion on the surface. The silence that followed something large passing beneath.

"Harpoons," Hark said.

---

The water exploded from the port side.

The surge hit the *Sea Warden* hard to starboard, the deck tilting, water washing cold over the rail. The head came first—long, flat, scaled in the grey-green of deep water, the skull built for striking rather than seeing. Then the body, coiling up out of the channel in a rising column of muscle and noise, forty feet of it, and the shadow it threw crossed the full length of the boat.

Higher than the mast.

The younger deckhand lost his footing on the wet deck and went over the rail. The older caught a line and held. Rallis shouted something. Tomas put the first harpoon into the serpent's flank—good strike, deep, the line going immediately taut—and the creature thrashed, its tail cracking down on the water with the sound of a mainsail blown out in a gale.

Hark was already moving. Blade out, timing his approach to the lunge pattern, driving the serpent toward the shallower water where the coral would limit its reach. It was faster than the reports had said. They were always faster. Jera drove the second harpoon home and the serpent made a sound too high for its size—the sound of something that had not been hurt in a very long time—and its body torqued.

Alexandria was at his right. She moved exactly as drilled—reading the line, not committing, waiting for the serpent's reach to extend before closing the distance. Her blade found the junction between two scales on its lower flank and she drove it home and twisted and withdrew in a single motion. Clean. The blood in the water was black against the grey.

The serpent turned.

Its tail came low and fast and below his sight line. He called the warning but it was already in motion—a single whipcrack sweep across the deck at knee height. Jera jumped. Tomas hit the mast with his shoulder. Alexandria was at the rail, repositioning, her weight back on her heel, the Void Step—

The tail caught the rail's edge. The wood shattered.

She went over.

The sound she made was brief—not a scream, just the shock of cold and impact compressed together—and she went under fast, the coat pulling her down, and the water closed.

---

The ice in his chest cracked.

Not broke. Cracked—hairline, the kind that runs through stone in the moment before the stone gives. Twenty-two years of it and he had never felt it do this. He was not on the deck.

He was ten years old. The forest was dark. Frost on the grass. There was a girl on the ground in front of him with grey eyes and a bolt in her chest, and her breath was going out of her in small gusts—the last reflex of a Wind Gate expiring, the last working she would ever do, and it was only breath. She was eleven. She had wanted to go back to a river. She had wanted to make the water shiver.

He was on his knees saying her name. He was opening his Gate and the light was coming and it was too late, it had already been too late before he got to his knees—

*Lea.*

The word tore out of him. Not a shout. Below the register of language. A sound sealed in the back of his chest for twenty-two years, coming out now as something he did not recognize as his own voice.

He opened the door.

The lock had been there so long he had stopped feeling its weight, the way you stop feeling a scar after enough years. It gave all at once—not slowly, not carefully—and the heat came up from beneath his sternum in a pressure that was almost nausea and exactly the opposite, something old and uncontrolled and very large. His palms lit from the inside out.

The debt came first. It always came first, before the working, before the fire reached the air—frost spreading from his wrists to his knuckles in the half-second it took the heat to climb, the cold writing its line into his skin before the price was even paid. His fingers went numb to the second knuckle. The leather stiffened, creaking, then darkened. Then ignited.

He aimed at the surface—not at the serpent, at the channel between the rail and the place she had gone under—and the fire left his palms in a flat directed sheet and the top four inches of the sea flash-boiled. Steam erupted, a column of white that blinded the serpent's heat-sense and drove it back from Alexandria's last position, the creature recoiling from the scalded surface in a thrash of confused muscle.

The door slammed shut.

The cold drove inward from his palms like a nail through the center of each hand. His fingers were numb to the wrist. The frost-scars blazed with it—the debt settling into the old lines, finding every path it had burned before, threading deeper where it could. He would not feel his hands properly for three days. He knew this the way he knew the lock had given: from experience, not thought.

He went over the rail.

The water was cold enough to stop thinking. Steam above, churn below, blood in the water turning everything grey and formless. He reached. Found her arm—her shoulder—the weight of the sodden coat—and pulled upward with everything his numb hands had left.

---

The crew hauled them both over the rail.

He came up on his knees still holding her and she was coughing, and that was the only information that mattered. He let go. She got her hands under her and coughed the water out and breathed. He stayed where he was on the wet planking and breathed with her.

His hands were bare.

The gloves were gone—the leather vaporized from his palms outward, the wristbands blackened and curled, nothing remaining below the charred cuffs. His hands lay on the wet wood: frost-scars from knuckles to elbows, white against the cold skin, dense at the base of each finger and thinner as they climbed toward the crease of his arm. The webs of old light. Every working written and rewritten in the same pale lines for twenty-two years. The ledger, open on the deck for anyone to read.

The serpent was gone. Driven back into the deep channel, wounded, the harpoon lines trailing loose behind it. Not dead. Off.

The deck was silent except for Alexandria coughing.

Jera on her feet, jaw already purpling, eyes on his hands. Tomas at the mast, one hand on the stays, not moving. Rallis at the tiller, his hollow eyes traveling from Hark's face to Hark's hands and settling there. The deckhands—the older holding a line, the younger dripping and white-faced from his time in the water—looking at the deck, at Hark, at the deck again.

Alexandria got up to her hands and knees.

She looked at him.

He waited. He had been waiting for this since he saw her walk aboard. Maybe longer. She was a Silencer. She had protocol and the Order's full machinery behind it, and she had just watched him use fire in front of seven witnesses and then dig her out of the sea with his bare hands. He waited for the threat-geometry to come in behind her eyes, for the cold calculation that decided what to do with what she had seen.

It didn't come.

She looked at his hands. She looked at his face. Her breathing was slow and deliberate—not calm, something that had chosen to be steady.

She had heard him say the name. He could see that she had heard it. He did not know what she knew about Lea. He only knew that she was holding his gaze with those grey eyes and not flinching, and that the name was still somewhere in the air between them, reverberating off the wet wood and the salt and the distant sound of the serpent retreating into the deep channel where no one would follow it.

Neither of them spoke.

---

Jera broke it.

She was wringing seawater from her braid, completely matter-of-fact, her voice carrying the flat pragmatism of someone who had made worse decisions than this and survived all of them.

"Anyone here planning to report this?"

The deckhands looked at each other, then at the deck. They were alive. They had just seen a man shoot fire from his hands and dive into the sea after a girl.

Tomas said: "I didn't see anything. Steam. Serpent thrashed. A lot of confusion."

His voice was the same voice he used to describe weather.

Rallis set the tiller and came forward. He looked at Hark's bare hands for a long moment. His son had been nineteen. He had said so before they started. The hollow eyes did not fill—they were past filling—but something in them settled, a small and exhausted shift.

"My report to the collective says the serpent was driven off by harpoon and fire-pot," he said. "Fire-pot was my idea." He looked directly at Hark. "The League's kept bad books on my family for three generations. I don't owe them anything I haven't already paid in blood."

The younger deckhand nodded. The older: "Aye."

Jera looked at Alexandria.

"Junior escort."

Alexandria was still on her knees. Seawater dripping from the hem of her coat, pooling around her hands on the wet planking. She was looking at Hark's hands—at the frost-scars, white against the grey wood, still holding the faint residual cold of the debt finishing its work.

A pause. Long enough to mean something.

"He saved my life," she said. Her voice was rough with seawater. Low. Stripped of inflection.

"That's all I saw."

Hark looked at her. She looked back.

She knew exactly what she had just done. He could see it in the set of her jaw, in the absolute absence of hesitation—not bravado, not recklessness, but the deliberate stillness of someone who had weighed the thing and decided. She was a Silencer. She had protocol and doctrine and the Order's full architecture of consequence pressing down on her, and she had said *that's all I saw* in the flat, unhurried tone of someone stating a fact.

He could not thank her. The language did not exist in any form he spoke. He could not close the distance or explain the name he had shouted, could not tell her that it had nothing to do with her and everything to do with her, that he had carried that name for twenty-two years and it had come out of him the moment she hit the water. He could not say any of it.

He pulled his charred sleeves down over his wrists. It covered nothing. The scars were already seen.

He got to his feet and walked to the bow.

---

The *Sea Warden* made Salthaven as the light failed.

The harbor lamps came on in sequence along the breakwater, amber in the grey dusk. Tomas sat beside Hark at the bow and said nothing, which was the most useful thing anyone had done for him since the harbor that morning. Jera was at the stern watching the horizon, her bruise darkening. The deckhands were below. Rallis held the tiller with the steadiness of a man who had been doing exactly this for thirty years and intended to do it for thirty more.

Hark had wrapped his hands in cloth from his pack. Not to hide them. The scars were seen. Just to keep the cold from settling further while the debt finished its accounting. His fingers were returning—pins, then pressure, then something close to sensation. By tomorrow, full use. By the day after, the same white lines in the same places, and the ledger unchanged except for the entries added today.

Alexandria sat with her back against the mast. Her eyes were closed, her breathing slow and deliberate. Not sleeping. The controlled rhythm of someone who did not want to appear awake.

He knew the rhythm. He had spent years producing it himself.

He had called her Lea.

She had heard it. He knew she had heard it. And afterward she had looked at him with those grey eyes—Lea's grey, the same particular depth—and there had been something in her face that he had no category for, because he had spent twenty-two years not allowing categories for it.

He looked at the water. The harbor was close.

She opened her eyes. Found him across the deck. Six feet of wet planking and something else, something with no units of measurement. She did not speak. Neither did he.

The *Sea Warden* came in off the water. Lines were thrown. The mooring was made. The crew moved around him and he stood at the bow until the last moment, looking at the harbor lights, at the town he had been hiding in for three years.

The lock was back on the door. He had checked it twice on the return trip. It held.

The rules did not.

He stepped off the gangway onto the dock. He walked back toward the ropewalk alone, the wrapped cloth on his hands stiff with salt. He did not look back. He knew without looking that she had not gone below while the boat was mooring—had stood at the rail, watching the dock approach with the same controlled stillness she brought to everything that had earned her attention.

He knew it the way he knew the debt was settled: not from evidence, but from the body.

He climbed the ropewalk stairs. He sat on the cot in the dark, his wrapped hands in his lap, and did not reach for the lockbox. He did not need the flower tonight to remember what he was carrying.

He already knew.
