# Chapter 15: The Leaving

**Hark**

---

The linen took three attempts.

His fingers wouldn't do what he told them. The left hand was the problem—the thumb and first two fingers stiff to the second knuckle, refusing to oppose the way they were built to—and he sat on the cot in the grey morning light and started the wrap again and watched his hands fumble it. The linen fell slack. He started a third time, working more slowly, leaving the knuckles looser, accepting that tight was no longer possible. When he finished, he held both hands in his lap and looked at them like documents someone had forged badly.

He unwrapped the left one. He checked.

The frost-scars had moved overnight. Not by much—a fraction, a spread of new white threading up from the old boundary on his wrist, past the line he had spent twenty-two years keeping them below. The new tissue was pale, the skin around it blotched with the particular mottled pink of settling debt. He touched one of the new lines with his right thumbnail and felt nothing. Below the wrist, the sensation was normal. At the new boundary, a dull ache. Above the wrist, silence.

He wrapped the left hand again and made a fist. The resistance was there. The pull of tissue that had not been there a week ago, that would still be there a year from now, that would still be spreading if he ever used the Light again. He held the fist as long as he could and then let go and the fingers fell open. He had not chosen to open them. They had simply surrendered.

He lay back on the cot and stared at the ceiling.

---

The nights were worse.

He'd learned the cycle by the third day: an hour or two of shallow sleep, then the sweats coming on fast, his body heat spiking past what was comfortable and then past what was manageable, his shirt soaked through, his hands hot despite the scarring—the debt from the drawing still unsettled, the reservoir still trying to calibrate itself against a depletion it hadn't experienced in twenty-two years. Then the temperature would plunge. He'd wake shivering in a wet shirt with his hands cold to the elbow and his mouth tasting like copper.

He'd trained himself to use as little of his Fire Gate as possible, as rarely as possible, as quietly as possible. Over two decades the internal reservoir had settled into a long, stable torpor—deep and still and never disturbed, a cistern sealed against any real drawing. The serpent had cracked the seal and he'd drawn through the crack with everything he had. Now the body was relearning what it was.

The name was the worst part. Not the scars, not the sweats, not the stiff fingers. The name.

He hadn't meant to say it. That was what he kept coming back to in the dark hours: the name had not been a decision. It had been below the register of decision, in the same place the fire had come from—a door he'd kept locked for twenty-two years, kept painted over, kept convincing himself was a wall, and the moment she hit the water the door had opened and both things had come out together: the fire and the name, Lea, her name, the name he hadn't said aloud since the night he'd held her hand in the dark forest and waited for his reservoir to fill enough to kill the Instructor with it.

He wasn't ten years old anymore. He knew what the name meant, coming out of him like that, in front of witnesses. He knew what it meant that it was the wrong name.

He lay in the dark and did not examine it and it sat there anyway, enormous and specific.

On the second day, he'd seen her at a distance.

She was walking back from the Kellan compound, her grey wool coat dark at the hem with the damp from the lower streets, her spine straight, the Void Step in her stride without her noticing. He'd stopped on the far side of the market square and watched her navigate the morning crowd with the same automatic route-reading she brought to every space—cataloguing exits, noting faces, her gaze moving across the stalls in that steady scanning rhythm. She didn't see him. The crowd was thick enough. He watched her until she rounded the bend of the switchback road and the grey wool coat disappeared, and then he went back to the ropewalk and sat on the cot and counted the days.

Three days. The Order hadn't come.

He'd been counting those too.

She'd lied in front of six other people. *He saved my life. That's all I saw.* She'd said it with the flat, unhurried certainty of someone stating a fact, and everyone on the *Sea Warden* had accepted it because she was a Silencer—because even if they didn't know her name for what she was, they knew the quality of her stillness, the economy of her attention, and people who moved like that weren't liars. She had told the truth about the part that was true. She had simply not mentioned the fire, the scars, the name.

He could not explain why.

He'd tried seventeen different explanations and they all failed at the same place: the moment on the deck when she'd looked at him with his hands bare and his secret all over the exposed ledger of his skin, and there had been something in her face that wasn't the cold threat-geometry she gave strangers. He didn't have a name for what it had been. He didn't try to name it. He filed it under the same category as the dried flower in the lockbox: things he carried without explanation, things he told himself meant nothing.

On the fourth morning, he made the decision. Or the decision finished making itself, the way they always did—gradual and then sudden, the resolution already present before the moment of noticing it.

She'd almost died.

Not the magic. Not the scars. Not the name. The fact that she went over the rail because the serpent was faster than the reports had said and he'd been one second too slow covering her position. He'd put her on that boat. He'd told himself it was training—the kind of real-world field experience that the coral road ambush hadn't fully provided, a chance to see how she moved under conditions where the terrain was actively working against her. He'd been testing her. He'd almost drowned her. The arithmetic was very simple.

He flexed his left hand inside the linen and felt the resistance of the new scars. If there was a fight today—a real one, the kind that showed up without announcement—he'd lose. Not because he lacked skill. Because his hands were unreliable and his reservoir was depleted to the point where a sustained drawing was beyond him, and he'd watched himself nearly use the Light mutation on the deck of the *Sea Warden* and burn himself worse than the first time, and he knew, the way he knew structural weaknesses in buildings and bad odds on roads, that he was not capable right now of doing what the job required.

He couldn't protect her. He could barely protect himself.

He stood up. He wrapped the linen tighter than it wanted to go and pulled his gloves on—new ones, purchased yesterday from the harness-maker, plain leather without the extra thermal lining, because the old pair was ash on the bottom of the serpent channel—and walked to the Factorage.

---

Vallus was already there.

He was at a table near the window with his ledger open and untouched, a cup of something gone cold beside his elbow. He looked up when Hark entered and did what his face always did—a brightness, a readiness—and then something more complicated shifted in beneath it. His eyes went to Hark's hands. To the linen visible at the cuff of the new gloves. To the way Hark moved across the room, which was the same as ever in direction and different in how much each step cost.

"Hark." He pushed the ledger aside. "I've been looking for you. I heard about the serpent. I heard—" He stopped.

Hark pulled out the chair across from him and sat without being invited. Graen was in the back. Verrol at his corner table with his pen moving in the same unhurried strokes it had moved in every day Hark had been coming here. The barkeep was polishing glasses at the far end of the bar. Nobody was listening.

"I'm terminating the contract," he said.

Vallus's face went still. "What?"

"The mentorship. It's done. The injury is worse than it looked. I can't run the sessions. Find someone else."

He raised his left hand—not to demonstrate, just as evidence, the stiff line of the fingers, the diminished arc of the grip. Vallus looked at it. Then he set down his cup and set it down with care, like someone putting a weight on a document they didn't want to blow away.

"You're lying," he said.

Hark's expression didn't shift. "I don't lie about contracts."

"Not about the contract." Vallus's voice had something in it Hark hadn't heard before: a steadiness that came from having been scared enough times that some portion of the fear had simply burned away. "About the reason." He leaned forward slightly. "I've watched you train her for weeks. You don't cut sessions short. You don't soften the drills. You gave her something I haven't seen you give anyone in three years at this Factorage. And now you're telling me a hand injury is going to stop you."

Hark said nothing.

"You fought a serpent with both hands burned and walked off the boat. You're not stopping because of your hands."

"You don't know what my hands can do."

"I know you're the best fighter I've ever hired and you're sitting in front of me with your left hand wrapped in linen telling me you can't work." Vallus looked at him steadily. His soft hands were flat on the table. They weren't shaking. "She's going to ask me why. When I tell her. She's going to ask why you left without saying anything, and she's going to—" He stopped. "She's not going to show it. That's the point. She's going to file it somewhere, and it's going to sit there, and she won't have a word for it, and neither will I, because you didn't give us one."

Hark was quiet.

"Is it dangerous? What you're protecting her from."

"Yes."

"Is staying more dangerous than going?"

The answer was the same one he'd been arguing with for four days. Staying was worse. Not for the logical reasons—the scars, the depleted reservoir, his reduced combat capacity. For the other reasons, the ones the rules existed to suppress. He was emotionally invested in a Silencer's welfare. He had used magic in front of witnesses to save her. He had called her by a dead girl's name. Every one of those facts was a weapon someone could use against him and, worse, against her. An unmarked mage with documented attachments to an Order asset was a liability the Order had contingency plans for.

"I don't know," he said. He hadn't meant to say it out loud. It came out before the rules could contain it. "But staying is worse."

Vallus looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded—a slow, tired motion, the nod of someone who has understood something he didn't want to understand and isn't going to argue about it.

"I'll tell her. But I want you to know that I think you're making a mistake."

Hark stood.

"She won't think it's a mistake," Vallus said behind him. "She'll think she did something wrong."

Hark walked out into the grey afternoon. He didn't answer. He didn't look back. He walked until the Factorage was behind him and the harbor was in front of him and the cold sea air was doing nothing useful to the temperature in his chest.

---

The bell above Vara's door gave its dull, familiar clink.

She was at her desk. She looked up from her copying and her eyes went immediately to his hands—the new gloves, the stiffness visible in the way he held them, the careful deliberateness with which he closed the door—and then to his face. She didn't ask what happened. She set down her pen.

"The mentorship," he said. "I'm terminating it."

"Does she know?"

"No."

Vara's expression didn't change. She had the face of a woman who had processed too many decisions that couldn't be undone, and had learned that the appropriate response to most of them was simply to continue. "You're going to tell me why."

"My hands aren't reliable. The combat training isn't—"

"The combat training isn't the reason." She said it without heat. She picked up her pen and didn't use it, just held it. "You're going to tell me why you're actually terminating it."

He was quiet.

Vara looked at him with the particular attention of someone who had spent forty years reading documents that didn't say what they meant. "I know what you are," she said. "I knew when you walked into this shop. I knew before you told me." She gestured at his hands with the pen—not pointing, just indicating. "I can see what the serpent cost you. I can see what you paid for it. I've been a placed mage long enough to read debt in a body without needing a Savant's gift." She set the pen down. "You think leaving will protect her. You think the connection you've formed is what's dangerous. That if you remove yourself, the threat diminishes."

Hark didn't answer.

"You're not wrong," she said, which surprised him. "Attachments are exploitable. If the Order has any reason to believe you're entangled with their operative, they'll use it. Against you and against her." She paused. "But you're also assuming you're the threat. You're not. The threat is what she's already filed in her reports. And what she's already started not filing." She met his eyes directly. "I've been watching her reports for weeks. She's been leaving things out. Not because she's incompetent. Because something has changed for her, and she's not ready to name it."

The information settled into him like cold water. He'd known she wasn't reporting him. He hadn't thought about what else she might be leaving out.

"She's going to need someone," he said. "When I'm not here. Someone who knows what she is and won't use it against her."

"You're asking me to protect a Silencer from the Order that controls me."

"I'm asking you to keep watching."

Vara was quiet for a moment. She looked at her hands—the ink-stained fingers, the slight tremor in the right one that had been there for years. Then she nodded. Once. Small.

"I was doing that before you came in," she said. "I'll continue after you leave."

He looked at her. She looked back. It wasn't trust—trust was a category that required more than shared interest and mutual exposure. But it was an acknowledgment: two people who had, without planning it, ended up on the same side of a line they hadn't been born to.

He left without thanking her. The bell clinked. The door shut. He walked back into the grey afternoon light.

---

The harbor was emptying of the midday traffic. He walked it with his hands in his pockets and his collar up against the salt wind, watching the boats move through the inner channel.

His hands ached. The new gloves didn't fit the way the old ones had, and the damp harbor air had found the seam at the left wrist, working its way through to the linen below, and the cold was sitting in the new scar tissue in a way that was going to be familiar from now on—a new location for the same old cold, the debt writing itself into a different part of his body and staying there. He flexed his fingers and felt the resistance and let them fall open and felt the resistance ease. A little better today than yesterday. Not better enough.

He thought about the girl.

The way she'd absorbed the Void Step in the training ground, her calves aching with the unfamiliar demand of never committing. The hunger in her face when she finally got the rhythm. The way she'd said *not this* when he asked what she'd expected, and he still didn't know exactly what she meant, and he was going to leave without finding out.

She hadn't reported him. She'd lied in front of six people without hesitation. She'd looked at his bare hands—the frost-scars mapped across his skin, the ledger open for anyone to read—and she hadn't flinched. The cold threat-geometry that she brought to strangers hadn't appeared. Instead something else had been in her face, something he'd spent four days not examining because examining it meant something he wasn't equipped to do.

She thought she had done something wrong.

That was what Vallus had said. She would file it under *operational change* and she would think she had done something wrong, and she would not have a word for it, and neither would he, because he had chosen not to give her one.

He told himself it was better this way. Clean. She was a Silencer. She'd been trained to accept tactical realities, to adapt to changed conditions without requiring explanation.

The thought arrived at its own conclusion before he could stop it, and he stopped on the harbourside with his hands in his pockets and stood there for a moment.

He was going to walk away from her without a word. He was going to let her stand in the training ground and wait for him and not come. He was going to let her decide she had done something wrong. The Order had been teaching her to file that lesson since before she could have named it—he'd seen enough of their training to recognize the shape of it in her, the way she moved through the world with her hands loose at her sides and everything important tucked behind a door. He was going to leave and that door would close a little tighter, and she wouldn't know why, and neither would he, because he had chosen not to give her a reason.

He'd been told that lesson himself. He'd spent twenty-two years enforcing it on himself, carving it into his chest where it sat alongside everything else he couldn't name. He knew exactly how it settled.

The rules were very clear. *I will not name fear. I will not let myself want. I will not need anyone.* The rules had kept him alive for twenty-two years. The rules had built the architecture of his survival, room by room, door by door, until the man who had been Damian Whiters was almost invisible inside the man who was Hark and that was fine, that was the goal, the goal was to be invisible even to himself.

The rules felt hollow. Like armor he'd outgrown years ago and kept wearing because he no longer knew what he looked like without it.

He walked back to the ropewalk. He climbed the stairs. He sat on the cot and did not pack his kit and did not sleep.

---

He didn't say goodbye.

He'd told himself all the reasons. He'd gone through them in the dark, after the fever-sweats receded and the cold took over and the frost crept its half-inch up his forearms while he lay in the wet shirt staring at the ceiling. He'd arranged the reasons in their proper order. She was a Silencer. She'd been trained for this. The contract was over. The mentorship had been a liability from the beginning. He couldn't protect her right now—couldn't close his left hand properly, couldn't draw on his Gate without paying a cost that would leave him worse than he already was. He couldn't protect her and he almost got her killed once already and he was going to leave before he did it again.

She would understand. She was a Silencer. She filed things.

He knew she wouldn't understand.

He knew she would stand in the training ground behind the Kellan compound and he wouldn't be there, and Vallus would find her and tell her the contract was over, and her face wouldn't change—she didn't let her face change—but something behind it would, in the place where she filed the things that didn't have categories, in the place that was getting crowded. He'd watched that place get crowded for weeks. He didn't know what was in it. He knew the look of someone running out of room.

He sat on the cot.

The frost-scars ached. The reservoir was a hollow pull behind his sternum, not empty—never quite empty—but drawn down to a level he hadn't felt since the forest years, since he was fifteen and running and barely keeping himself alive. He opened and closed his left hand, feeling the resistance, the new scar tissue pulling at the extension of his fingers. A little better each day. By next week, the grip would be functional. By the week after, reliable. The debt always settled. The body always found its way back to whatever equilibrium it was capable of.

He thought about the name. He thought about it the way he always thought about things he wasn't allowed to think about—briefly, through a door he closed immediately afterward, just enough to confirm it was still there.

She'd heard it. She knew it was the wrong name. She'd seen the scars—still spreading, still settling—and she was smart enough to know what frost-scars on an unmarked mage meant. She had enough to destroy him and she hadn't used it. He still didn't know why. He'd spent four days unable to construct an explanation that held. There was the loyalty explanation—that she was biding her time, building a case—but she'd lied for him in front of six people. There was the tactical explanation—that she saw more value in a living asset than a reported one—but it didn't account for the way she'd looked at his hands on the deck. He'd run out of explanations that fit the evidence.

He hadn't been thinking about training when he went over the rail. He hadn't been thinking about the contract or the mentorship or the Order or the Silencer. He'd been thinking about a girl with grey eyes and a bolt in her chest and eleven years and her breath going out of her in small gusts, and he'd thought *not again*, and the door had opened.

He didn't examine that. He had gotten very good, over twenty-two years, at not examining that.

He lay back on the cot. He stared at the ceiling. He breathed the smell of hemp and salt and old timber and let the cold settle where it was settling and the ache sit where it was sitting and the rules close back over the door.

*I will not name fear. I will not let myself want. I will not need anyone.*

The rules were back in place.
