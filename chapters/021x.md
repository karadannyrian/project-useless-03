# Chapter 21x: Lea

**Damian**

---

He was burning.

Not with fever---his mother had pressed her hand to his forehead a dozen
times in the weeks before the carriage came, and each time she'd
frowned and pulled away, muttering that he was warm but not sick, warm
but not sick. The word she was looking for was *sensitive*, and she
didn't have it. Neither did he.

Damian Whiters was seven years old, and his body was a signal fire he
didn't know how to put out.

He sat in the black-lacquered carriage with silk around his wrists and
heat crawling under his skin like a second heartbeat. The windows were
set too high. He couldn't see out. He could only see the handler across
from him---grey tabard, clean hands, eyes that didn't look at him---and
the door that was locked from the outside, and the narrow slot near the
roof where a sliver of sky slid past, grey and indifferent. The carriage
jolted. The silk didn't chafe. Someone had chosen silk because silk was
kind, and none of this was kind.

He was too hot. The space was too small. The handler's hands were
folded in his lap, still and pale, and Damian couldn't stop looking at
them. The fingers. The knuckles. The way they didn't move. He'd never
thought about hands before, but now he couldn't stop. Hands told you
what was coming. These hands told him nothing. That was the first
lesson.

He didn't know where they were taking him. He knew he'd been
collected. A man in grey had come to the house and spoken to his mother
in a voice too low to hear, and his mother had cried, and then the man
had tied Damian's wrists with silk and led him out. The neighbours
watched. No one stopped it.

His mother's hands had shaken when she touched his face. Hers were the
last warm hands he'd felt before the carriage. He held onto that. The
warmth. The shaking. The way she'd said his name---*Damian*---like it
was something she was losing.

In the carriage, he was already learning not to say it himself.

The training floor was stone. Cold stone, grey stone, stone that drank
heat and never gave it back. Damian learned to hate it before he learned
to guard.

There were others. Children, like him, collected from villages and
cities and farmholds across the territories. Some had been in the
Order's halls for years already. Some were new, still blinking, still
crying at night when the instructors couldn't hear. Damian didn't cry.
He'd stopped crying in the carriage, when he'd realised the handler's
hands weren't going to move no matter what sound he made.

The instructor called him Damian. That was the first surprise. He'd
expected them to take his name along with everything else, but the Order
didn't need to take names. Names were useful. Names let them call you
forward when it was your turn to demonstrate a working, your turn to
fail, your turn to be corrected.

"Damian." The instructor's voice was flat, patient, the voice of
someone who had said a hundred names a hundred times and would say them
a hundred more. "Show me the flame."

He was eight years old. He'd been there for eleven months. He still
couldn't guard, not properly, not the way the older children could. His
internal reservoir was a furnace without a door, heat bleeding out of
him constantly, the way it had bled since birth. The instructors could
sense him from across the training floor. The Savant who'd examined him
on arrival had looked at his hands and nodded once, and someone had
written something in a ledger, and that was the moment Damian understood
that his body was not his own.

He reached for his internal reservoir and pulled.

The flame came. Small, unsteady, flickering at the edges. It sat in his
palm like a thing that didn't want to be there. The heat left his
fingers first---the familiar numbness, the cold creeping in as the debt
began to tally. His knuckles went white. Frost spidered across his
fingernails. He'd learned to expect it. Payment. The flame cost warmth,
and the warmth came from him, and his body kept the ledger.

The instructor watched. "Again."

He cast again. The cold spread to his wrists. His hands were shaking
now, the fine tremor of a body that had given too much and hadn't
learned how to stop. The flame in his palm guttered, steadied, guttered
again.

"Again."

"I can't---"

"You can. Again."

He cast a third time. The flame was weaker, the cold deeper, his fingers
so numb he couldn't feel the heat of his own working anymore. The frost
had climbed past his wrists, white lines tracing the veins. Somewhere
behind him, one of the other children made a sound. Someone else was
crying. He couldn't tell who.

"Enough."

The instructor stepped forward and closed a hand around Damian's wrist.
The grip was firm, clinical, the grip of someone checking a tool for
damage. The man's thumb pressed against the frost-lines, tracing them,
reading them. Damian didn't pull away. He'd learned not to pull away.

"You're pulling from yourself too quickly. Your body can't replenish
at this rate. You'll burn out before you reach ten." The instructor
released his wrist. "We'll work on environmental sourcing tomorrow.
Until then, eat. Rest. Your body pays what you borrow. Don't borrow
more than you have."

That night, Damian sat on his cot with his hands wrapped in a blanket,
waiting for the cold to fade. It didn't.

Lea was in the cot next to his.

She was newer than him, collected later, still carrying the flinch of
someone who remembered life before the Order and hadn't yet learned to
stop missing it. She was small and dark-haired and her eyes were grey
like a winter sky, and she talked in her sleep. Not words---just sounds,
little gusts of breath that made the air around her cot stir and shift.
Wind Gate. Unguarded, like him. Her body leaked pressure the way his
leaked heat, and in the dormitory at night, with the other children
breathing around them, the air near her bunk was never quite still.

He noticed her because she was loud in a way no one else was. Not loud
in voice---she barely spoke during training. Loud in presence. When she
entered a room, the candles flickered. When she was afraid, the air went
tight and strange, pressing against his eardrums. He could feel her fear
before he saw her face. She could feel his heat before he spoke.

They recognised each other the way animals recognised their own species
in a foreign habitat.

"You run hot," she said one night, her voice barely above a whisper.
The dormitory was dark. The instructor's footsteps had faded down the
corridor.

"You make the air weird."

"That's not a word."

"It's what you do."

She laughed---a small sound, quickly stifled. They both froze, listening
for footsteps. None came.

"Lea," she said, after a moment.

"I know. I heard the instructor."

"What's yours?"

He told her. Damian. The name felt strange in his mouth, a thing he
carried but rarely used. The instructors used it. The other children
used it. But it was his, and saying it to her in the dark felt like
handing over something that could be broken.

"Damian," she repeated, testing it. "You're Fire."

"You're Wind."

"Obviously."

They talked most nights after that. Whispered exchanges in the dark,
tucked between the rhythms of the other children's breathing. She told
him about the village she'd come from, the river, the way the wind came
down from the hills and made the water shiver. He told her about his
mother, her hands, the way she'd touched his face before the carriage
came. He didn't tell her about the handler's hands. He didn't have
words for that yet.

She was the first person he trusted. He didn't know that's what it
was. He wouldn't have named it if asked. He just knew that when Lea was
in the cot beside his, the dark was less dark, and the cold in his hands
was easier to ignore.

One night, she showed him.

"Give me your hand," she whispered.

He did. She pressed her palm against his---her skin was cool, always
cool, the way his was always warm. Her grey eyes were focused, intent,
the way they got when she was about to do something she'd been
practicing in secret.

The air between their palms began to move.

A small vortex, no bigger than a candle flame, spinning in the space
where their hands met. He could feel it tugging at his skin, a tiny
pressure differential, a miniature storm contained in the cup of their
fingers. It was precise. Controlled. The kind of working that took more
finesse than force, and she was doing it in the dark of the dormitory
with no one watching except him.

"It's beautiful," he said.

"It's nothing. The instructors want me doing pressure-blades and
gust-forcing. Combat aeromancy." She said the words with a flatness
that was becoming familiar. "I don't want to fight. I want to make the
water shiver."

The vortex spun, silver and silent, a ghost of the wind she'd described
from her village. It lasted three more seconds. Then it guttered out.

Lea's breath caught.

Not the normal catch of exertion. Something deeper, wrong. She pulled
her hand back and pressed it to her chest, and her breathing went
shallow---a thin, reedy sound, the sound of someone who couldn't get
enough air and was trying not to show it. Her lips lost colour. The
space around her cot went still, utterly still, the pressure collapsing
inward as her body clawed back whatever it had spent.

Damian sat up. "Lea---"

"Don't." She held up her other hand, the one not pressed to her
chest. "Don't call anyone. It passes."

He didn't call anyone. He watched, helpless, as she fought for breath.
Her shoulders heaved. Her face was pale, beaded with sweat. The sound of
her breathing was thin and ragged, like wind through a cracked window.
It went on for a full minute. Two. Slowly, incrementally, the colour
crept back into her lips. Her breathing steadied. She lowered her hand
from her chest and lay back on the cot, staring at the ceiling.

"The healers say my lung capacity is permanently reduced," she said.
Her voice was hoarse. "Every working shaves a little more off. By the
time I'm thirty, I might not be able to run." She turned her head on
the pillow. Her grey eyes found his in the dark. "I'm eleven years
old."

"You shouldn't have shown me."

"I wanted to." She closed her eyes. "You showed me your frost-scars.
I wanted to show you mine."

He didn't know what to say. He'd never known what to say, not in the
moments that mattered. He reached across the gap between their cots and
took her hand---the one that had held the vortex, the one that was cool
and still now, resting on the blanket. He held it until her breathing
evened out. He held it until she slept.

The next morning, she was coughing. She told the instructor it was a
cold. The instructor believed her. Damian watched her walk to the
training floor with her shoulders straight and her chin up, and he
understood for the first time that she was stronger than him in the ways
that mattered.

The instructor pushed him toward Light.

"You have the control for it," the man said. They were in a smaller
room now, one of the private training chambers, stone walls scorched
black from years of fire. "Most Fire Gates plateau at base state. Bolts
and walls. Combat pyromancy. You're refining faster than expected.
We'll test for Mutation A."

Damian was ten years old. He'd been with the Order for three years. His
guarding was solid now---the furnace of his internal reservoir had a
door he could close, and the constant heat-bleed of his childhood was
something he could silence with a thought. It had taken a year to learn.
The relief was indescribable. For the first time in his life, he wasn't
broadcasting.

He was also angry.

He didn't know when the anger had started. Maybe it had always been
there, a low flame burning under everything else, banked but never
extinguished. The Order didn't encourage anger---emotions were
volatile, emotions made magic unpredictable---but the Order also didn't
discourage it in Fire Gates. Fire fed on anger. Rage raised heat. Heat
raised power. A Fire mage who could harness their emotions cast hotter
and longer than one who couldn't.

Damian didn't know how to harness it. He just knew it was there. A hot
knot in his chest. A thing that flared when the instructor pushed him
too hard, when one of the other children was punished, when he thought
about his mother's face and the way the neighbours had watched and done
nothing. He didn't name it. He fed it to the flame.

"Strip the heat," the instructor said. "Leave only the light. You're
not burning anything. You're illuminating. The light will be cold, but
your body will pay the thermal debt. You'll run hot internally.
Fever-heat. It's dangerous if held too long. You'll learn your
limit."

Damian reached for his internal reservoir. Not to pull heat---to pull
light. The flame in his palm flickered, guttered, then changed. The
orange bled out of it. The warmth bled out of it. What remained was
white, pure, so bright it hurt to look at. The beam shot outward, narrow
as a needle, and scored a line across the stone wall.

The heat didn't leave his fingers. It stayed inside him, building,
pressing against his ribs from the inside. His skin flushed. Sweat
beaded on his forehead. His body was paying the debt differently
now---not in cold, but in heat, a furnace with the door closed.

"Good," the instructor said. "Again."

He cast again. The fever-heat climbed. His vision swam. The beam was
sharper this time, more focused, and the stone wall hissed where it cut.

"Again."

He cast a third time. His skin was steaming in the cold air of the
training chamber, his blood a hot river under his skin. The light was so
bright it left afterimages burned into his vision, and his hands were
steady, and the anger in his chest was a sun, and he was burning,
burning, burning---

"Enough."

The instructor's hand closed on his shoulder. Damian gasped. The light
died. The fever-heat stayed, throbbing behind his eyes, in his teeth, in
the marrow of his bones. He was shivering despite the heat. His body
didn't know which way was up.

"You'll build tolerance," the instructor said. "Or you won't. The
Light mutation is lethal if overused. Your body pays the debt
internally. Hyperthermia. Organ failure. Some mages survive it. Most
don't reach mastery." He released Damian's shoulder. "You have the
potential for mastery. Whether you reach it depends on whether you learn
your limits before you exceed them."

Damian didn't answer. He was watching the frost on his
knuckles---because there was frost, even now, even through the
fever-heat. The Light mutation burned him from the inside, but the debt
still wrote itself on his skin. Thin white lines. Permanent. The ledger
in flesh.

Lea noticed the frost-scars before he did.

"Your hands," she said one night, her voice low in the dark.
"They're worse."

He looked down. The white lines had spread, threading from his knuckles
up toward his wrists. They didn't hurt. They never hurt. They were just
there, a map of every working he'd ever done, every debt he'd paid
with his own body.

"The instructor says I'm advancing fast."

"The instructor wants you to burn out." Her voice was flat, the way it
got when she was angry and hiding it. The air around her cot was tight,
pressure building. "He pushes you harder than anyone. You know why."

"Because I can take it."

"Because you're Fire. Because Fire Gates are weapons and the Order
wants weapons. You're not a student to them, Damian. You're an
investment."

He didn't answer. He knew she was right. He'd known for a while. The
Order collected Sensitives to protect them---that was the stated
purpose, the legal justification---but the Order also used them. The
children who showed promise were trained harder, pushed further, shaped
into tools. The ones who didn't show promise disappeared into other
parts of the facility, and no one talked about them.

Damian showed promise. The Light mutation was rare. The instructor had
said as much. He was an investment, and investments were protected, and
investments were also spent.

"When I get out," Lea whispered, "I'm going back to the river. The
one near my village. The wind comes down from the hills and makes the
water shiver. I'm going to stand there and breathe, and no one's going
to tell me to do it again."

"You're not getting out."

"I am. And you're coming with me."

He turned his head on the cot. In the dark, her face was a pale blur,
her grey eyes catching the faint light from the high window. She was
serious. She'd been planning this. He could feel it in the way the air
pressure shifted around her---steady, deliberate, not the tightness of
fear but the focus of intent.

"When."

"Soon. There's a night watch rotation. The handler at the east door
sleeps through the third bell. I've been watching. He sleeps through it
every time."

"That's not enough."

"There's a gate. Unlocked. I found it last week during kitchen duty.
It leads to the delivery yard. From the yard, there's a wall. Low
enough to climb if you're light."

"And after the wall."

"We run."

He stared at the ceiling. His hands were cold. The frost-scars were
white lines in the dark, and his ribs ached from the day's training,
and the anger in his chest was a low, banked fire. He thought about the
carriage. The silk. The handler's hands. He thought about his mother's
face, the last warmth before the cold.

"Fine," he said. "Soon."

They ran on a night with no moon.

The handler at the east door slept through the third bell, just as Lea
had promised. His hands were folded on his chest, rising and falling
with his breath. Damian watched them as they passed. Still. Harmless.
Not like the handler in the carriage, whose hands had been still and
terrible. This man was just a man, and he was asleep, and they were
leaving.

Lea led. She'd memorised the route---the corridor, the turn, the
unmarked door that led to the delivery yard. Her footsteps made no
sound. The air around her was perfectly still, perfectly controlled.
She'd learned to guard her breath the way Damian had learned to guard
his heat, and now she moved like silence itself.

The delivery yard was empty. The wall was low enough. Damian boosted Lea
up first, and she reached down to pull him after her. The night air was
cold on his face. The sky was huge and black and full of stars he
hadn't seen in three years. He'd forgotten stars.

They dropped down on the other side and ran.

The forest swallowed them. Trees, dark and dense, the ground soft with
old leaves. Damian's heat-sight flickered open---just a thread, just
enough to read the warm bodies of small animals in the undergrowth and
the cooler void where a stream cut through the dark. The debt was
negligible. He paid it. His fingers numbed. He didn't care.

They ran until Lea stumbled.

"Keep going," she gasped. "I just need---"

The bolt took her through the chest.

Yellow light. Crackling. The smell of ozone and burning cloth. She went
down without a sound, her grey eyes wide and surprised and already going
dim. Damian heard himself shout her name---heard it from outside his own
body, a raw sound, a child's sound---and then he was on his knees
beside her, his hands pressing against the wound, blood hot on his
fingers, her blood, her warmth.

The instructor stepped out of the trees.

He wasn't alone. Two handlers flanked him, grey tabards, clean hands.
One of them was holding a crossbow. The other was holding a length of
white silk.

"You're predictable," the instructor said. "Both of you. The east
door handler has been awake for weeks. We've been waiting for you to
try."

Damian couldn't speak. Lea's blood was still hot on his hands. Her
breath was a wet, shallow thing, fluttering in her chest. The air around
her was wild, unguarded, pressure spiking and crashing as her body lost
its grip on the gate.

"Lea," he said. "Lea, guard. Guard your gate. You have to---"

She couldn't. Her eyes were on his face, grey and fading, and her lips
moved, and a small gust of air escaped her mouth. Not a word. Not quite.
Just breath. A Wind mage's last working, and it was only breath.

Her hand found his. Squeezed once. Went still.

The pressure in the air collapsed. The silence that followed was
absolute.

Damian looked up. The instructor was watching him, patient, unblinking.
The handlers stood behind him, their hands still clean, their faces
blank. The crossbow was still raised. The silk was still ready.

"The Light mutation is lethal if overused," the instructor said. "You
have the potential for mastery. Let's see if you've learned your
limits."

Damian opened his gate.

Not from the environment. The environment was cold---night forest, no
sun, no hearth, nothing to borrow. He pulled from himself. Everything he
had. His body heat. His blood-warmth. His metabolic fire. The anger that
had been banked in his chest for three years, the rage he'd never been
able to name, the grief that was already replacing it---he fed it all
into the working.

Light. Not flame. Pure, white, searing light. A cutting beam, thin as a
wire, brighter than the stars he'd just remembered existed. He aimed it
at the instructor, and the instructor's eyes went wide, and the beam
punched through his chest before he could raise a hand to stop it.

The handler with the crossbow fired. The bolt went wide---Damian was
already moving, already spinning, the light carving an arc through the
dark. The second handler went down. The first handler dropped the
crossbow and ran.

Damian let him go.

He stood in the forest, steam rising from his skin, his blood a furnace,
his hands shaking with the fever-heat of the Light mutation pushed past
every limit he'd been taught. The frost was spreading across his
knuckles, his wrists, his forearms---the debt settling, the ledger
writing itself in permanent white lines. He was burning alive from the
inside, and he was freezing to death from the outside, and Lea was dead
on the ground beside him.

He knelt. Closed her eyes. Her face was still warm. The last of her
breath was still stirring the air around him, a faint pressure against
his skin, already fading.

He stayed there until the cold in his hands was too deep to ignore. Then
he stood. He walked. The forest swallowed him, and the stars watched him
go, and the boy who had been Damian Whiters left his name beside Lea's
body and didn't go back for it.

Hark opened his eyes.

The ironwood canopy was dark overhead, branches tangled against a sky
that was grey with coming dawn. His body was a catalogue of pain---ribs,
hands, the bone-deep chill of a debt that had accumulated interest and
called itself due. His left hand was a frozen claw. His right wasn't
much better.

Rane's hand was on his chest.

The boy was beside him, small and still, his ruby eyes watching Hark's
face with that same unblinking steadiness. His palm was pressed flat
against Hark's sternum, and it was warm---that impossible, generated
warmth that came from nowhere and owed nothing. It was cutting through
the cold. Not fast. Not evenly. But steadily, like a hearth fire in a
room that had been freezing for hours.

Hark tried to speak. His mouth was dry. His voice was a rasp. "How
long."

It wasn't Rane who answered. It was Silla, from somewhere to his left.
"Three hours. Maybe four. You stopped shivering an hour ago. We
thought---" She stopped. Didn't finish. Didn't need to.

Korvan was propped against a tree nearby, his arm still in its sling,
his grey eyes open and watching. The fire was low, banked embers, but
Rane's warmth was brighter than any fire Hark had ever drawn from.

The boy hadn't moved his hand.

Hark looked at him. The ruby eyes looked back. The gold hair was a mess
of tangles and forest debris. The plain wool cloak was filthy. Rane was
still here. Still warm. Still.

Then the boy spoke.

"Lea," he said. Quiet. Steady. The same voice that had said *Dino* in
the shack, that had shouted *Korvan* when the old man fell. "You said
her name. While you were asleep. Three times."

Hark's heart stopped.

"You said another name too." Rane's head tilted, just slightly.
"Damian. You said Damian."

The name hit like a blade slipped between ribs. Clean. Precise. The name
he'd left in the forest. The name he'd buried so deep he didn't even
think it in his own interior voice. The name no one knew. Not Graen. Not
Verrol. Not Malla. No one. And this boy---this strange, silent,
impossible boy---had heard it in his sleep and was giving it back to
him.

"Who is she," Rane said. Not a question. Not quite. "Lea."

Hark couldn't speak. His throat was closed. His chest was a knot of cold
and warmth and something else that he didn't have a word for. The boy's
hand was still on his sternum. The warmth was still flowing.

"She didn't make it," Hark said. His voice was someone else's voice.
"A long time ago. She didn't make it."

Rane nodded. Once. He didn't ask who Damian was. He'd already figured
it out. The boy who noticed everything, who read Hark the way Hark read
hands, who'd sat beside him without being asked---he'd put the pieces
together in the dark while Hark was dreaming.

Hark closed his eyes. The warmth held him. The boy held him.

When he opened them again, it was dawn.
