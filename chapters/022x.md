# Chapter 22x: Joel

**Damian**

---

He followed Joel's camp for three days before he decided Joel wasn't going to kill him.

The camp was a half-day's walk off the trade road, tucked into a hollow between two ridges where the wind couldn't find it. He'd been circling it since dawn on the first day, staying downwind, reading the smoke from Joel's fire and the pattern of his movements and the way he talked to himself when he thought no one was listening. Joel talked a lot. Most of it was complaints—about the cold, about his knee, about a merchant in some town he'd never heard of who'd tried to short him on a contract. Some of it was jokes. Most of the jokes were bad. Joel laughed at them anyway.

He was twelve. Maybe thirteen. He'd stopped counting somewhere in the second winter.

The frost-scars were up to his elbows by then, white lines that stood out against skin that hadn't been clean in weeks. He kept them wrapped in strips of scavenged cloth, the same way he'd been doing since the forest—kept his left arm cradled against his chest like it was broken, because a broken arm was something people understood. A broken arm got you a bowl of soup from a kitchen door. Frost-scars got you questions. Questions got you the Order.

He hadn't spoken in two years. The voice had just—stopped. After the forest. After the light. After the body on the ground and the stars he'd forgotten and the long walk through the dark that ended somewhere and started again and never really stopped. The silence had filled his throat like water and he'd never tried to cough it out. Words were for people. There were no people. There was only the cold and the heat inside him and the daily arithmetic of staying alive.

Joel was dying. He could see it from the treeline—the way Joel moved, slow and deliberate, the way he coughed into a rag and didn't look at what came out. The way he'd built his camp like someone who wasn't planning to move it again. His fire was small and tidy. His bedroll was patched in three places. His sword was plain steel, nothing special, but it was clean and oiled and propped against a stone where he could reach it from the bedroll.

Joel knew he was there. He didn't figure that out until the third day.

"You going to come in or keep skulking?" Joel called out, not looking up from the fire. His voice was gravel and phlegm. "You're not as quiet as you think. The birds go still when you move. Been tracking your circuit since yesterday."

He didn't move. His hand was on the knife he'd stolen from a farmhouse two towns back. The blade was dull. He'd been meaning to sharpen it.

"Relax. If I wanted to hurt you I'd have done it the first night. You talk in your sleep, by the way. Or make sounds, anyway. Not words. Just—sounds."

He hadn't known that. The silence wasn't just in his throat, then. It was in his sleep too.

"Come down. Fire's warm. Food's terrible but there's enough."

He came down. Not because he trusted Joel. Because Joel was dying, and dying men were safe, and he hadn't eaten anything but foraged roots in three days. The food was terrible—a thin stew of dried meat and something that might have been turnips—but it was hot. He ate it crouched on the far side of the fire, the knife still in his hand.

Joel watched him. His eyes were bloodshot but sharp, the kind of sharp that came from a lifetime of reading people who might kill you. He looked at his face, his wrapped arm, the way he held himself like something that expected to be struck.

"What happened to your arm?"

He didn't answer.

"Not broken," Joel said. "You're not carrying it right for a break. Hiding something, then. What are you hiding?"

Silence.

"Fine. Don't tell me." Joel coughed into the rag. The sound was wet and ugly. "I'm Joel. Class B mercenary. Unorthodox, which is a polite way of saying I never went to a real School and I fight dirty. You got a name?"

He didn't answer. The fire popped. A spark lifted into the dark and died.

"Nothing, huh." Joel studied him for a long moment. His eyes dropped to his wrapped hands, then back to his face. Something shifted in his expression—not pity, he'd have left if it was pity. Something else. The look of a man who'd seen enough to fill in the blanks. "You're a mage."

His grip on the knife tightened.

"Don't bother. I'm dying anyway, and I don't work for the Order. Never did." Joel settled back against his pack with a grunt. "You want to loot my camp, go ahead. Coin in the pack, dried meat in the satchel, and the sword you've been eyeing since you got here. Take it. Just don't take the waterskin. I'm still thirsty."

He didn't take the coin. He didn't take the sword. He sat by the fire and ate Joel's food and didn't speak, and when the cold crept into his fingers he held them toward the flames and let the heat soak through the wrappings.

Joel talked. It was what he did. He talked about contracts gone wrong, about the Schools he'd stolen techniques from, about a woman he'd loved in a port city whose name he couldn't remember. He talked about the blood cancer that was eating him from the inside. He talked about dying.

"I got maybe a month. Maybe less. Been looking for a place to do it. Keep finding reasons not to." He looked at the boy. "You're a reason, I guess. Someone's got to listen to all this."

He listened. He couldn't have spoken if he'd wanted to—the silence was a locked room and he'd lost the key somewhere in the dark—but he listened.

On the third day, Joel said: "You got a name?"

He shook his head.

"Nothing, still." Joel studied him. The firelight carved deep shadows under his eyes. "I'm gonna call you Hark."

He tilted his head.

"It means listen. Old word. Or maybe I made it up. Point is, you don't talk, but you hear everything. You've been sitting there for three days and you haven't missed a word. So that's you. Hark."

The name landed. It wasn't his name—his name was something else, something he'd left behind with the body in the forest and the stars he'd forgotten—but it was a name, and he hadn't had one in two years. He nodded once.

Joel grinned. It was a gap-toothed grin, more gaps than teeth, but it was real. "Good. Hark it is."

Joel didn't intend to teach him. He was too sick, too tired, too far past the age where he'd cared about passing anything on. But he was a talker, and talking about fighting was what he knew, and Hark was there, and something about his silence made Joel want to fill it.

He started with swords. Basic stances. Basic cuts. He couldn't demonstrate—he could barely stand—so he talked Hark through it, correcting his form with words alone.

"Don't grip it so tight. You're holding a sword, not a drowning man. Loose fingers, firm palm."

"Your feet are too close together. A stiff breeze would put you on your ass. Wider. There."

"Block like that again and I'll haunt you. You're stopping the blade with your arm. That's what the sword is for. Sword takes the hit, you redirect."

Hark learned fast. He'd always learned fast. The Order had said as much, before everything. But Joel's lessons were different—no frost-scars, no pushing past limits, no instructor watching him burn. Just an old man in a bedroll, correcting his footwork between coughing fits.

Survival skills came next. How to negotiate a contract. How to spot a bounty hunter. How to read a room for threats in the first three seconds. How to hide from mages.

"They can't track you if you don't use magic," Joel said. "That's the trick. You've got a gate, but you don't have to open it. No fire, no pattern. No pattern, no hunt. You've been smart about that, from the look of you. Stay smart."

He didn't ask about the scars. He knew—he'd seen enough mage-scars to recognise the ledger—but he didn't push. That was the kindness. He let Hark keep the silence.

Joel joked about dying. It was his way of coping. Turning the cancer into material.

"Body's a bad contract," he said one evening, staring up at the canvas of his tent. "You put in all this work, all this maintenance, and at the end it just stops paying out. No renegotiation. No extension. Bad terms."

"I had a good run. Better than most. Killed more people than I should've, saved fewer than I wanted. That's the trade."

"When I'm gone, take the sword. It's nothing special—plain steel, no name. But it's balanced right and it won't break. A sword that won't break is worth more than a sword with a story."

Hark absorbed it all. The jokes. The lessons. The voice in the firelight. He didn't laugh—he couldn't laugh, any more than he could speak—but he memorised the words. He would need them later. He didn't know it yet, but he was building a voice out of Joel's, a way of talking that would keep the world at arm's length when the silence finally broke.

On the last day, Joel was too weak to sit up. His breathing was shallow and wet. His skin had gone grey. The grin had faded into something smaller.

"All right," he said. "I'm done. You should go. Don't stay for the end. It's not pretty, and you've seen enough ugly things."

Hark didn't move.

"Stubborn. Fine." Joel's eyes found his. "One last thing, then. Listen."

Hark listened.

"You got out. I don't know how, and I don't want to know. But you got out. Most don't. Most never see the sky after they're collected. You saw the sky. You're still seeing it."

Joel coughed. Rode it out.

"So here's the advice. It's simple. Just survive long enough and never get caught again."

He closed his eyes. "That's it. Survive. Outlast the bastards. Every day you're still breathing is a day they didn't win. Got it?"

Hark nodded.

"Good." His voice was fading. "Now go. Take the sword. Take the coin. Take the name—it's yours. You're Hark. You listen. That's enough."

Hark stayed until Joel's breathing stopped. He didn't cry. He hadn't cried since the carriage, since the silk, since the hands that didn't move. But his chest was tight, and the silence in the tent was the wrong kind of silence—the kind that had weight, the kind that pressed.

He took the sword. He took the coin. He took the name.

He buried Joel under a cairn of stones, the way Joel had described once in passing. The mercenary's burial. Practical. Anonymous. He didn't speak over it. He didn't have words yet.

But he carried the jokes with him when he walked away. And somewhere in the months that followed, alone on the road with Joel's sword on his back, his voice began to come back. Slowly. Sparingly. A word here. A sentence there. The silence didn't break. It cracked. Just enough to let the minimum through.

*You're a mage. That means you're already damned. Might as well be damned on your own terms.*
