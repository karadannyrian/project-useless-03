Hark — Complete Character Profile

Compiled from suppressed Order records, fragmented field reports, and direct observation. This document exists outside the Order's archives. The man it describes has spent twenty-two years ensuring no such document could be assembled.

SECTION ONE: IDENTITY AND CLASSIFICATION

Identity

Birth name: Damian Whiters
Operational alias: Hark (assigned age fifteen by mercenary Joel; only name he answers to)
Age: 32
Place of origin: Tenant farm, eastern Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Specific village unnamed in surviving records.
Bloodline: Commoner. Father absent before birth; mother unnamed in intake records. No known living relatives.
Gate: Fire
Mutation: Light
Estimated Depth: 6 (last confirmed Savant reading: age ten; current estimate based on observed capability)
Order classification: Escaped, unmarked, presumed deceased (file suppressed)

Current Status

Class C mercenary operating out of Salthaven, Maelstrom League. Resident of rented room above ropewalk for three years. Takes contracts through the Factorage guild. Uses no magic in front of witnesses. Has evaded Order detection for twenty-two years.

SECTION TWO: PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

Lean build, wiry rather than broad. Medium height. Dark hair, grey eyes. Posture is a contradiction: at rest, he seems almost slack, but the slackness is a held breath, a coiled readiness that anyone trained to read fighters recognizes immediately.

His hands are gloved at all times. Beneath the gloves: extensive frost-scars, the permanent marker of Fire Gate debt. The scars begin at the fingertips and run up both arms in dense, layered webs of white. They are deepest on the palms and knuckles, spreading thinner as they ascend. They reach the elbows; after heavy magic use, they spread further—toward the shoulders, the neck, the jaw. The pattern is old and deep and unmistakable to anyone who knows how to read it.

He wears long sleeves in all seasons. He does not remove his gloves to eat, to sleep, to bathe in public facilities. The scars are his most recognizable feature, and he has built his life around ensuring no one recognizes them.

Other physical markers: a crooked finger on his left hand, badly set after a fall in childhood. A collection of mundane scars—knife nicks on his forearms, a burn on his ribs from a cooking fire, a pale line across his left shoulder blade where a bandit's blade caught him years ago. None of these are magical. All of them are survival.

His face is unremarkable by design. He has practiced the art of being forgettable. His expression defaults to neutral, his gaze to watchful. He smiles rarely, and when he does it is usually bitter, self-deprecating, or both.

SECTION THREE: ORIGINS AND COLLECTION

Childhood

Damian Whiters was born on a tenant farm in the eastern Grain-Lords' Confederacy. His father was absent before he could form memories. His mother was a thin-wristed woman who loved him with a ferocity she had no other outlet for. Her name is not recorded in any surviving Order document. He does not speak it.

He was a warm child. As an infant, he ran hot in his mother's arms. By the time he could walk, his skin steamed faintly in cold air. The village called it a quirk. His mother called it nothing. She pressed her palm to his forehead a dozen times a day, frowning, muttering that he was warm but not sick. The word she was looking for was Sensitive. She did not have it.

Collection

The Order's Mage-Detector arrived when Damian was seven. The man in grey spoke to his mother in a voice too low to hear. She cried. He bound Damian's wrists with white silk—silk that did not chafe because silk was chosen not to chafe—and led him to a black-lacquered carriage with windows set too high to see out of. The neighbors watched from their doorways. No one stopped it.

His mother touched his face before the carriage door closed. Her hands were the last warm hands he felt for years. He held onto that warmth. He would hold onto it through everything, a small bright coal in a chest that would later fill with ash.

The Handler across from him had grey sleeves and clean hands that never moved. Damian learned his first lesson in that carriage: hands tell you what's coming. These hands told him nothing.

SECTION FOUR: CRADLE TRAINING

Intake and Assessment

Damian arrived at the Cradle within weeks. He was bathed, clothed in grey, and subjected to the First Reading by a Savant whose name he never learned. The Assessor held his hands, turned them over, traced the heat-bleed that radiated from his unguarded reservoir. Nodded once. Someone wrote something in a ledger. Damian understood, at that moment, that his body was not his own.

He was classified Tier I—Promising. Strong depth, clear mutation potential, stable emotional markers despite recent trauma. The Assessor noted: Anger present but banked. Grief present but suppressed. Both may serve as fuel.

Guarding

For eleven months, Damian could not guard. His internal reservoir was a furnace with the door missing, heat bleeding out of him constantly. Every mage in the facility could sense him from across the training floor. He burned, and the burning was a beacon.

Learning to guard took eleven months. It was not a spell—it was a conscious partitioning of attention, holding his internal element behind a locked door. When it finally clicked, the relief was indescribable. For the first time in his life, he was not broadcasting. He was still hot inside, still burning, but the heat was his. He could hide.

The Instructor pushed him relentlessly. Again. Again. You can. Again. Damian's knuckles whitened with frost after every session, the debt writing itself in thin lines across his fingers. He learned that magic was borrowing, and borrowing meant payment, and payment was carved into the body.

Lea

Her cot was next to his. Wind Gate. Small, dark-haired, grey eyes like a winter sky. She talked in her sleep—not words, little gusts of breath that stirred the air around her. When she was afraid, the air went tight, pressing against his eardrums. He could feel her fear before he saw her face. She could feel his heat before he spoke.

They recognized each other the way animals recognized their own species in a foreign habitat. They talked at night, after the Instructor's footsteps had faded, whispered exchanges in the dark between the rhythms of other children's breathing. She told him about her village, the river, the way the wind came down from the hills and made the water shiver. He told her about his mother's hands. He did not tell her about the Handler's hands. He did not have words for that yet.

She was the first person he trusted. He would not have named it if asked. He just knew that when Lea was in the cot beside his, the dark was less dark, and the cold in his hands was easier to ignore.

One night she showed him a vortex—a miniature storm, spinning silver and silent between their palms. It was beautiful. It cost her. Her breathing went shallow, her lips lost color. The healers had told her that every working reduced her lung capacity. By thirty, she might not be able to run. She was eleven.

"You shouldn't have shown me," Damian said.

"I wanted to. You showed me your frost-scars. I wanted to show you mine."

He reached across the gap between their cots and took her hand. He held it until she slept. That was the first time he understood she was stronger than him in the ways that mattered.

Light Mutation Initiation

The Instructor pushed him toward Light. Damian was ten. His guarding was solid. He was also angry—a low, banked fire that had been burning under everything else for as long as he could remember. The Order did not discourage anger in Fire Gates. Rage raised heat. Heat raised power.

"Strip the heat," the Instructor said. "Leave only the light. Your body will pay the thermal debt internally. Fever-heat. It's dangerous if held too long. You'll learn your limit."

The first beam was white, pure, so bright it hurt to look at. It scored a line across the stone wall. His skin flushed. Sweat beaded on his forehead. His body was paying the debt in rising heat, a furnace with the door closed.

"Again."

He cast again. The fever-heat climbed. The beam was sharper.

"Again."

A third time. His skin steamed in the cold air. The frost-scars on his knuckles were spreading, threading up toward his wrists, white lines on white skin. He was burning alive from the inside and freezing to death from the outside. The Instructor's hand closed on his shoulder.

"You'll build tolerance. Or you won't. The Light mutation is lethal if overused. Hyperthermia. Organ failure. Some mages survive it. Most don't reach mastery. You have the potential for mastery. Whether you reach it depends on whether you learn your limits before you exceed them."

That night, Lea looked at his hands. "They're worse."

"The Instructor says I'm advancing fast."

"The Instructor wants you to burn out." The air around her cot was tight. "He pushes you harder than anyone. Because you're Fire. Because Fire Gates are weapons and the Order wants weapons. You're not a student to them, Damian. You're an investment."

He knew she was right.

SECTION FIVE: ESCAPE

The Plan

Lea planned it for weeks. The handler at the east door slept through the third bell—she had watched, she knew. There was an unmarked door to the delivery yard, a low wall, the forest beyond. She led. Damian followed.

They ran on a night with no moon. They cleared the wall. The sky was huge and black and full of stars he had forgotten. He had forgotten stars.

They ran until Lea stumbled.

The Death

The bolt took her through the chest. Yellow light, crackling, the smell of ozone and burning cloth. She went down without a sound, her grey eyes wide and surprised and already going dim.

The Instructor stepped out of the trees. Two handlers flanked him. One held a crossbow. The other held a length of white silk.

"You're predictable," the Instructor said. "Both of you. The east door handler has been awake for weeks. We've been waiting for you to try."

Damian was on his knees beside Lea, his hands pressing against the wound, her blood hot on his fingers. She couldn't guard. Her lips moved. A small gust of air escaped her mouth—not a word, just breath. A Wind mage's last working, and it was only breath. Her hand found his. Squeezed once. Went still.

The pressure in the air collapsed. The silence that followed was absolute.

"The Light mutation is lethal if overused," the Instructor said. "You have the potential for mastery. Let's see if you've learned your limits."

The Light

Damian opened his gate. Not from the environment—the night forest was cold, no sun, no hearth. He pulled from himself. Body heat. Blood-warmth. Metabolic fire. Three years of banked rage, the grief that was already replacing it, the memory of his mother's hands and Lea's voice and the carriage and the silk—he fed it all into the working.

White light. Pure, searing. A cutting beam, thin as a wire, brighter than the stars he had just remembered existed. It punched through the Instructor's chest before the man could raise a hand. The handler with the crossbow fired wide. Damian was already spinning, the light carving an arc through the dark. The second handler went down. The first dropped the crossbow and ran. Damian let him go.

He stood in the forest, steam rising from his skin, his blood a furnace. The frost was spreading across his knuckles, his wrists, his forearms—the debt settling, the ledger writing itself in permanent white lines. He was burning alive from the inside and freezing to death from the outside, and Lea was dead on the ground beside him.

He knelt. Closed her eyes. Her face was still warm. The last of her breath was still stirring the air around him, a faint pressure against his skin, already fading.

He stayed there until the cold in his hands was too deep to ignore. Then he stood. He walked. The forest swallowed him, and the stars watched him go.

He did not speak her name. He did not speak at all.

The boy who had been Damian Whiters left his name beside Lea's body and did not go back for it.

SECTION SIX: SURVIVAL

The Forest Years (Age 10–15)

He did not remember deciding to survive. Survival was simply what his body did, the same way it had learned to guard: automatically, without conscious instruction, because the alternative was unthinkable and he had stopped thinking.

The first year was the worst. He was ten years old, alone, carrying a debt so deep his body barely knew how to function. The hyperthermia from the Light overdraw came in waves—fever spikes that left him shaking and vomiting, then plunging chills as the frost-debt reasserted itself. He learned to read the signs. He learned to find water, to build shelter, to hunt small animals with base-state fire he barely had the strength to cast. Every working cost him something. His internal reservoir was shallow, depleted, slow to replenish. He paid in frost-scars and fever-sweat and a bone-deep exhaustion that never fully lifted.

He did not speak. Not from a decision—his voice simply stopped. The shout he had made when Lea fell had been the last sound he produced for years. Words were for people, and there were no people.

He concealed the frost-scars early. Wrapped his hands and forearms in scavenged cloth. Pretended his arm was broken when he passed through villages to steal food. The sling invited sympathy, questions—he learned to deflect with silence and speed. By twelve, he had graduated to thin leather gloves, the kind a farmhand might wear, worn soft and stained. He replaced them when they wore through, always the same style. Unremarkable. Forgettable.

He stole what he needed. He learned to pick pockets, to read the rhythm of a street, to know which merchants watched their stalls and which did not. He learned to run. He was always running.

Joel (Age 15)

He found the camp by smell—woodsmoke, old blood, a body that had not moved in a while. A single tent, a cold firepit, a bedroll with a man in it. The man was dying.

Hark—though he was not Hark yet—watched from the treeline for a full hour before slipping into the camp to search for valuables.

"You've got terrible instincts, kid."

The voice was gravel and phlegm. The old man was propped on one elbow, watching him with bloodshot eyes and a grin that had more gaps than teeth. He looked like death had already started the paperwork.

"First rule of looting," the old man said. "Check if the corpse is actually a corpse. I could've put a bolt in your back." He coughed, spat something dark. "Relax. I'm not going to. I'm too tired. What's your name?"

No answer. He had not answered anyone in five years.

"Nothing, huh? Fine." The old man's eyes dropped to the gloved hands. Something shifted in his expression—recognition, maybe. "You're a mage."

Grip on the knife tightened.

"Don't bother. I'm dying anyway, and I don't work for the Order. Never did." The old man settled back onto his bedroll. "I'm Joel. Class B mercenary. Unorthodox, which is a polite way of saying I never went to a real School and I fight dirty. You want to loot my camp, go ahead. There's coin in the pack, dried meat in the satchel, and a half-decent sword I won't be needing. Just don't take the waterskin. I'm still thirsty."

He should have left. Every instinct told him to run. He did not run. He sat down by the cold firepit and waited.

Joel talked. It was what he did—had been doing for decades, to anyone who would listen, and now the only person around was a mute boy with suspicious gloves and a knife he had not sheathed. Joel talked about mercenary contracts gone wrong, about the Schools he had stolen techniques from, about a woman he had loved in a port city whose name he could not remember anymore. He talked about the blood cancer that was eating him from the inside.

On the third day, Joel said: "You got a name?"

No answer.

"I'm gonna call you Hark." A pause. "It means listen. Old word, I think. Or maybe I made it up. Point is, you don't talk, but you hear everything. So that's you. Hark."

The name landed. It was not his name—his name was Damian, and Damian was dead in a forest beside a girl with grey eyes—but it was a name, and he had not had one in five years. He nodded once.

The Lessons

Joel did not intend to teach. He was too sick, too tired, too far past the age where he had cared about passing anything on. But he was a talker, and talking about fighting was what he knew, and something about the boy's silence made Joel want to fill it.

He started with swordsmanship. Basic stances, basic cuts, the economy of motion that separated a trained fighter from a brawler. He could not demonstrate—he could barely stand—so he talked Hark through it, correcting form with words alone.

"Don't grip it so tight. You're holding a sword, not a drowning man. Loose fingers, firm palm. Let the blade do the work."

"Your feet are too close together. A stiff breeze would put you on your ass. Wider. There. That's your foundation."

"Block like that again and I'll haunt you. You're stopping the blade with your arm. That's what the sword is for. Sword takes the hit, you redirect. Stop taking hits meant for the steel."

Hark learned fast. He had always learned fast. He had good instincts, a low center of gravity, and five years of survival-honed reflexes. Joel's lessons filled gaps he had not known he had.

Survival skills came next—how to negotiate a contract, how to spot a bounty hunter, how to read a room for threats in the first three seconds after entering. How to hide from mages.

"They can't track you if you don't use magic," Joel said. "That's the trick. You've got a gate, but you don't have to open it. The Order looks for patterns—mage-debt, environmental anomalies, reports of unexplained fire. No fire, no pattern. No pattern, no hunt. You've been smart about that. Stay smart."

He did not ask about the scars. He did not ask what Hark was running from. He knew. He let Hark keep his silence.

Joel joked about dying. It was his way of coping—turning the cancer into material.

"Body's a bad contract. You put in all this work, all this maintenance, and at the end it just stops paying out. No renegotiation. No extension. Bad terms."

"I had a good run. Better than most. Killed more people than I should've, saved fewer than I wanted. That's the trade. You try to balance it out, but the numbers don't work. The numbers never work."

"When I'm gone, take the sword. It's nothing special—plain steel, no name, no history. But it's balanced right and it won't break. A sword that won't break is worth more than a sword with a story."

Hark absorbed it all. He did not laugh—he still could not laugh—but he memorized the jokes.

The Death

On the final day, Joel was too weak to sit up. "All right. I'm done. You should go. Don't stay for the end. It's not pretty, and you've seen enough ugly things."

Hark did not move.

"Stubborn. Fine. One last thing, then. Listen." He coughed, rode it out, continued. "You got out. I don't know how, and I don't want to know—plausible deniability, you understand—but you got out. That means you're one of the lucky ones. Most don't. Most never see the sky after they're collected. You saw the sky. You're still seeing it." He closed his eyes. "So here's the advice. It's simple. Just survive long enough and never get caught again. That's it. Survive. Outlast the bastards. Every day you're still breathing is a day they didn't win. Got it?"

Hark nodded.

"Good. Now go. Take the sword. Take the coin. Take the name—it's yours. You're Hark. You listen. That's enough."

Hark stayed until Joel's breathing stopped. He did not cry. He had spent five years not crying, and he was not going to start now. But his hands were cold, and his chest was tight, and the silence in the tent was the same silence that had followed Lea's last breath.

He took the sword. He took the coin. He took the name.

He left Joel's body wrapped in the bedroll, a cairn of stones over the grave—the mercenary's burial, practical and anonymous. He did not speak over it. He did not have words yet.

But he carried the jokes with him when he walked away. And somewhere in the months that followed, his voice began to come back. Slowly. Sparingly. A word here, a sentence there, always short, always declarative. The silence did not break. It cracked, just enough to let the minimum through.

SECTION SEVEN: RENNE

The River (Age 15)

He was not trying to die. He was trying to wash away the memory of Lea's eyes going dim, the Instructor's blood on his hands, Joel's last rattle. He was trying to silence something that would not be silenced. He was failing.

The river was cold. Not cold enough.

He knelt in the shallows, just past a bend where willows trailed their fingers in the current. His hands were submerged. The frost-scars on his forearms stood out against his pale skin. His sleeves had ridden up. He did not move to cover them. He did not care.

A shadow fell across the ripples.

"You're leaking heat," a voice said.

He looked up. A girl stood at the river's edge, small-framed, dark hair, eyes that had seen too many things for someone still carrying a girl's roundness in her cheeks. Her hands were wrapped in clean bandages. A Water-mage—he could feel it, the way the river hummed around her presence.

His scars were visible. She did not flinch. She just looked at them, then at his face, and said: "Come inside. You'll freeze."

She extended a hand—not her scarred one, but the other—and waited.

His chest ached. He did not have a word for it. The word was trust. The word was want. The word was please. He did not have any of those words.

He took her hand.

The Cottage (Age 15–16)

Her name was Renne. She was a year older than him. She had escaped the Order at fifteen, two years before. She ran a network—small, fragile, already saving children. She asked no questions. She fed him. She gave him the bed and took the root cellar for herself.

He stayed. He told himself he was resting. Healing. His body needed it. But he had needed rest before, and he had never stayed. He stayed because she did not push.

She talked. He listened. She told him about the Order, about the children she had saved, about the ones she had not. She told him about the healing mutation—how every working left a scar, how she remembered every one. She showed him her hands. Fine white lines crisscrossed her palms and knuckles, like cracks in a dry riverbed.

"Seventy-three," she said. "Seventy-three workings since I escaped. I remember every one."

He had nothing to say to that. He went back to sharpening his knife. She smiled. It was the first time he had seen her smile without sadness behind it.

"Seventy-four," she said. "I'm counting this one."

He looked away. Counted the exits. The hearth was not an exit.

The Four Months

They had four months. Mornings by the river. Afternoons in the cottage. Nights in front of the hearth with Hark learning to laugh again—broken, rusty sounds that surprised him every time—and Renne learning to believe that maybe, maybe, she could have something that was not just survival.

He dreamed of Lea. Every night. The same dream. He woke with his hand on his knife, the frost-scars aching. He did not tell Renne about the dreams. But she knew.

One night she sat beside him on the bed. Not touching. Her shoulder an inch from his. "You don't have to talk," she said. "You don't have to tell me anything. But you don't have to be alone, either."

His hand went to his knife, then stopped. He did not pull away.

The Rules

He went to the river. Alone. He sat on the bank where they had first met and he carved the rules into himself. Not into his flesh—there were no scar-mages here, no magical prohibitions. He carved them into his mind through repetition, through punishment, through sheer force of will.

I will not name fear.
I will not let myself want.
I will not need anyone.

When he felt fear, he counted exits until his pulse slowed. When he felt want, he sharpened his knife until his hands bled. When he felt loneliness, he walked until his legs burned and his lungs ached and he could not feel anything except the road.

He came back to the cottage. He was gone all day. Renne was sitting at the table, her eyes red.

"Where were you?"

"I fixed myself."

She stood. Touched his face. "You broke yourself," she said. "You fool."

He did not deny it.

The Separation

The last night, a courier arrived. An Order patrol was three days north. Not hunting Renne specifically—not yet—but sweeping through the Confederacy. They would find the cottage. They would find the children in the root cellar. They would find her.

"You should go," Renne said. "Your face is known in the Confederacy. If someone sees you with me—"

"I know."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet. "This isn't goodbye. It can't be. I won't let it be."

He wanted to say something. The words were there—I want to stay. I want to come with you. I want—but his chest tightened and his jaw clenched and the rules held.

He reached into his pocket. He pulled out a dried flower—one he had picked from the riverbank, months ago, pressed between the pages of a book she had lent him. He had kept it. He had never told her.

He held it out.

She took it. Her fingers brushed his. "You kept this."

He nodded.

"You kept it for four months."

Another nod.

"You're not allowed to want," she said. "But you kept it anyway."

He did not answer. He could not.

She put the flower in her pocket. She kissed his cheek—a brief, soft press of her lips against his jaw. "The river remembers," she said.

Then she was gone.

He stood in the empty cottage for a long time. Then he walked to the river. He knelt in the shallows. He did not cry. He had not cried since Lea died.

He stayed until dawn. Then he walked south. He never looked back.

He did not know she was pregnant. He would not know for seventeen years.

SECTION EIGHT: MERCENARY YEARS (Age 16–32)

The Long Drift

He became a mercenary because it was what Joel had been, and because it was work that did not ask questions, and because the skills he had acquired—survival, blades, reading a room, hiding from mages—were the skills a mercenary needed.

He started small. Caravan guard. Message runner. Muscle for hire in towns too small to have their own law. He drifted across the continent: the Confederacy, the Dominion for a season, the Hold-Kingdoms for a winter contract guarding a caravan through the mountain passes. He learned the terrain of each realm, the local customs, the local dangers. He learned which cities had Order branches and which did not. He never entered the ones that did unless he had no choice.

He never climbed higher than Class C. Not because he lacked skill—he was better than his rating, sharper than half the B-class mercenaries he worked beside—but because rating up required reputation, and reputation required visibility, and visibility was the one thing he could not afford. Class C was the sweet spot: respected enough to get work, not prominent enough to be remembered.

He used magic sparingly. A flicker of heat to start a fire. A thread of warmth to stay alive in a blizzard. Never more than the minimum when there were witnesses. When there were no witnesses—when a job went wrong and the terrain was used up and his body was used up—he let the gate open, and he paid the debt in frost.

The Voice

He learned to talk again, after a fashion. Short declarative sentences. No conjunctions if he could avoid them. The voice was flat, dry, sardonic—Joel's dark humor filtered through years of practice. He made jokes about his own body, his own failures, because if he called himself broken first, no one else could use it against him.

He measured everything in transactions: cost, worth, debt. He trusted no one, but he learned to work with people anyway, because solo survival had limits and a crew meant cover.

The other mercenaries called him strange but harmless. The quiet one. The one with the gloves. He deflected questions with jokes and silences and the occasional hard look that discouraged follow-up. No one knew he was a mage.

Salthaven

He ended up in the Shattered Archipelago almost by accident. A contract had gone sour in the Hold-Kingdoms—a merchant who had tried to pay in mineral scrip instead of coin, a local lord who had taken the merchant's side, a border he had decided to cross rather than argue. The first ship out was bound for the League. He took it. He stayed.

Salthaven was a minor port town of no particular importance, built on a limestone spine that jutted into a shallow bay. The kind of place the Order's attention slid past. He checked. He always checked. The nearest Order branch was two days' sail away. He rented a room above the ropewalk—small, bare, functional, a window facing the harbor. He paid by the week. A week was a bet you could walk away from. A month was a commitment.

He took contracts through the Factorage, a mercenary guild run by a man named Graen who always tried to short on payment. The clerk, Verrol, offered the same flat neutrality to everyone who walked through the door. Hark appreciated Verrol in a way he had never articulated and never would. The man's hands were clean, steady, a clerk's hands. The safest kind.

He had been in Salthaven for three years. The longest he had stayed anywhere since the cottage.

SECTION NINE: PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

Cognitive Architecture

Hark processes the world through an observational hierarchy refined by twenty-two years of survival:

1. Hands. Are they empty? Twitching? Too still? Reaching? Hands predict action. He reads them before faces.
2. Exits. Doors, windows, alleys, gaps in a crowd. The geometry of leaving. Automatic since childhood.
3. Weapons and weight. Where is the knife? The shift of a coat over a holster? Is his own blade positioned correctly?
4. Faces. Reluctantly. Eyes specifically. Micro-expressions of contempt or pity—the two he expects. He looks away immediately after reading.
5. Small beautiful things. A clean line of light. A coin's wear pattern. A child's unguarded laugh. Noticed, then immediately suppressed as irrelevant.

The Rules

The rules are self-inflicted, non-magical, and absolute. They were carved at fifteen, after Lea's death, after the forest, after he learned that love is loss and wanting is the door through which grief enters.

· I will not name fear.
· I will not let myself want.
· I will not need anyone.

These are not aspirations. They are the architecture of his personality. Every decision he has made for seventeen years has been shaped by them. He does not say trust, afraid, want, or lonely. He cannot say his birth name—Damian belongs to the dead boy in the forest. He describes the mechanics of reliability rather than naming the feeling. He reduces desire to the absence of objection. He states external facts that imply internal states without acknowledging them.

Forbidden Vocabulary

Forbidden Word Strategy
Trust Describe mechanics of reliability instead. "He pays on time. Hasn't run yet. That's something."
Afraid Becomes "not worth the trouble," "bad odds," "exposure," or silence.
Want (genuine desire) Becomes "wouldn't mind," "could be worse," "had worse."
Lonely Becomes observational: "Tavern's loud tonight." "No one at the fire."
Damian (birth name) Never appears in interior monologue. Only "Hark" as a label for the version others see.

Cognitive Refuges

When reality presses too hard, Hark retreats through four escalating levels:

1. Weapon maintenance. Physical, repetitive, tactile. Counting strokes. Checking edge. The body knows the motions so the mind can stop. Triggered by mild social friction, a question too personal, a long silence.
2. The running joke. Internal sardonic commentary on everything happening. Turns pain into material. The voice in his head is a bitter stand-up. Triggered by sustained interaction, being watched, someone being earnest at him.
3. Cold inventory. Military-style catalog of exits, weapons, cover, threats, and his own physical state. Reduces the world to a threat matrix. Triggered by fear he will not name, a job going wrong, being cornered.
4. A specific image. A single mental image—something small, fragile, and his alone. No narrative attached. Just the picture. Holds it like a stone in the mouth. Triggered by impending collapse, the moment before he would do something irreversible, grief.

Emotional Landscape

Hark's emotional range is fully intact. He has simply built a fortress around it. The fortress is called the rules, the humor, the cold inventory. Inside the fortress: a fifteen-year-old boy who watched his only friend die, who loved a girl and could not say it, who has been running for twenty-two years and is very, very tired.

He experiences grief as a permanent cold in his chest, indistinguishable from frost-debt after a certain point. He experiences love as a tightening in his throat that he immediately suppresses. He experiences hope as a dangerous warmth that he has learned to fear.

His primary emotional register is watching. When he is afraid, he catalogs exits. When he is sad, he sharpens his blade. When he is lonely, he counts the weeks he has stayed in Salthaven and tells himself it is practical, just practical.

Attachment Patterns

Hark has loved four people in his life: his mother, Lea, Joel, and Renne. His mother was taken by the Order. Lea was killed in front of him. Joel died slowly while Hark watched. Renne he left before she could leave him.

He has concluded, not unreasonably, that attachment is the prelude to loss. The rules are his attempt to preempt the loss by refusing the attachment. They have not worked. He still carries a dried flower in his lockbox. He still dreams of Lea. He still avoids Renne rather than face what he feels when he sees her.

He is profoundly lonely. He does not have the word.

Self-Perception

Hark thinks of himself as a salvage job—a collection of broken parts held together by spite and habit. He jokes about his own body, his own failures, because if he calls himself broken first, no one else can weaponize it. The jokes are armor. The armor is also a cage.

He believes he is a bad contract—written in provisional ink, ready to be voided at any moment. He does not believe he deserves permanence. He does not believe he deserves love. He believes survival is the only metric that matters, and he has survived. That is enough. It has to be enough.

SECTION ELEVEN: RELATIONSHIPS

The Dead

Lea (deceased, age 11). Wind Gate. His first friend. His first loss. Her death shaped the rules. Her name is the only one he speaks aloud in moments of extreme stress—a slip, a haunting. She is the reason he cannot say trust or want. She is the wound that has never closed. He carries her in the frost-scars, in the dreams, in the silence where her voice used to be.

Joel (deceased, age unknown). Class B mercenary. Named him. Taught him. Died of blood cancer in a tent. Hark still uses his jokes, his techniques, his philosophy of survival. Joel is the closest thing to a father he ever had. He does not speak of him.

His mother (presumed deceased). Thin-wristed woman who loved him. Her hands were the last warm hands he felt. He does not know if she is alive. He does not look for her. He holds the memory of her warmth like a coal.

The Living

Renne. Water-mage, Healing mutation. He spent four months with her at fifteen and has been running from what he felt ever since. He knows she is in Salthaven. He has avoided her for three years. He tells himself it is operational security. It is not. He carries her dried flower in his lockbox. He does not know she had his daughter.

The Absence

Hark has no friends, no family, no crew. He has contacts, employers, people whose names he knows but would not cross a street to greet. The isolation is structural: the rules forbid attachment, and the rules are all he has.

He is not a misanthrope. He is not incapable of connection. He is terrified of it. Every person he has ever loved has been taken or left. He has concluded that the common factor is him.

SECTION TWELVE: CURRENT STATUS

Hark lives in a rented room above a ropewalk in Salthaven. He has been there three years. He takes Class C contracts through the Factorage. He wears gloves and long sleeves. He uses magic only when there are no witnesses. He keeps a dried flower in his lockbox and tells himself it means nothing.

He is waiting for nothing. He is surviving. The distinction, if there ever was one, has grown too thin to see.
