# THE RIVER

## A Comprehensive Document on the Underground Network for Sensitive Child Extraction

*Operational for fifteen years. Founded by the Water-mage Renne. Centered in the southern Maelstrom League. Dedicated to the movement of Sensitive children from the point of detection to locations beyond the Order's reach.*

---

## PART ONE: MISSION AND PHILOSOPHY

### The Work

The River does one thing: it moves children. Specifically, it moves Sensitive children—those born with an open Gate and no training to guard it—from the place they are found to a place the Order cannot collect them. It is a smuggling operation, not a rebellion. It does not fight the Order. It does not stockpile weapons or train mages in combat magic or plan to overthrow the Cradle. Its victories are quiet and invisible: a child who grows up uncollected, a Gate that never appears in the Order's ledgers, a family that never watches their daughter's wrists bound in white silk.

The River's philosophy holds that the Order's collection is not protection but predation. The Order frames its work as humanitarian—saving Sensitive children from the dangers of their own unguarded magic. The River frames it differently: the Order takes children from their families, trains them as assets, marks them with debt, and deploys them as tools of political control. A child who is never collected is a child who never carries the Order's debt-pattern in their flesh. That is the only victory the River claims.

### Scale and Scope

The River moves between twelve and twenty children per year. This is a deliberate limitation. Larger numbers would attract attention. Faster throughput would compromise security. The network survives by being small, slow, and forgettable.

At any given time, between three and eight children are in the network's care. They are spread across safehouses in the southern third of the Maelstrom League, never concentrated in a single location. The average stay is three to six weeks before transfer to permanent sanctuary.

Sanctuary destinations vary. Most children are placed with remote island communities—fishing villages too small to warrant regular Order sweeps, settlements on atolls accessible only by shallow-draft boats that Order vessels cannot navigate. Some are taken in by sympathetic families in larger ports, given new names and new histories. In rare cases, children are moved north to the Hold-Kingdoms of Khaz-Vor, where the mountain lords are quietly building an Insensitive army and have use for Sensitive children the Order cannot track. These northern transfers are the most dangerous and the least common.

### Geographic Center

The River's center of operations is Salthaven, a minor port town in the southern Maelstrom League. Salthaven was chosen not for its strategic value but for its lack of it. It has no naval base. No noble houses of political consequence. No Order branch within two days' sail. Its insignificance is its greatest defense.

Salthaven's residents protect the network with collective silence. They know the healer with bandaged hands. They know the fishing boat that sometimes sails at odd hours. They know the children who appear and disappear. They do not speak of these things to outsiders. This complicity is not organized; it is organic, a shared understanding that the Order is not welcome and that silence is the only weapon ordinary people have against an institution that can read debt in flesh.

## PART TWO: THE INNER CIRCLE

The inner circle consists of five individuals who know the full scope of the network's operations. They are not mercenaries or ideologues. They are survivors, exiles, and debtors who found in Renne's work a purpose they could not find elsewhere. Every member has been with the network for at least ten years. Trust is absolute because it has been tested across decades.

### Renne — The Heart

**Full name:** Renne (surname unknown; Order records list her as "Renne, Water Gate, escaped age fifteen")

**Age:** 33

**Status:** Founder and operational leader. Alive.

**Gate and Mutation:** Water Gate, Healing mutation. Depth estimated at 5-6, though she has never been formally assessed by a Savant.

Renne was collected by the Order as a child. She escaped at fifteen, trained and desperate, carrying the first of what would become a lifetime of debt-scars. At sixteen, she met a fifteen-year-old Fire-mage named Damian Whiters—a boy who had also escaped, who had watched his only friend die, who could not say the words for what he felt. They spent four months together in a cottage near a river in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. He gave her a dried flower because he could not say he wanted to stay. When Order patrols forced them apart, he walked away. She did not know she was pregnant.

At seventeen, she surrendered their daughter—Alexandria—to an Order foundling house. She named the child, held her for a long time, and walked into a snowstorm with no name of her own. She has spent every year since trying to save other people's children because she could not save her own.

Renne performs all the network's magical healing. Her Healing mutation allows her to mend injuries and cure illnesses that mundane medicine cannot touch, but the cost is severe: sympathetic injury. Every wound she heals, she experiences a version of in her own body. A broken leg mended leaves a hairline fracture in her own bone. A lung fever cleared leaves her coughing for weeks. The scars on her hands—always wrapped in bandages—are the ledger of seventy-three such workings, each one remembered, each one a child.

She has never healed herself. Not the exhaustion. Not the chronic pain in her hands. Not the grief that sits in her chest like a stone. She considers her body a tool, not a person, and tools are maintained, not healed. This is not sustainable. Everyone who loves her has told her so. She listens, nods, and continues working.

She keeps a small leather-bound book of pressed plants from every region she has passed through. The first entry is the dried flower Hark gave her at seventeen. She has never told anyone what it means.

### Marta — The Left Hand

**Full name:** Marta (surname unknown; she claimed to have forgotten it)

**Age:** Mid-sixties at time of death

**Status:** Deceased. Killed during the purge.

**Background:** Fisherwoman. Mundane.

Marta was Renne's oldest ally and the network's logistical architect. She joined Renne at the very beginning, when Renne was a desperate eighteen-year-old with a newborn she could not keep and a grief she could not name. The story of their meeting changes depending on who tells it—Renne says Marta found her half-frozen on a beach; Marta always said Renne tried to steal her boat and was too incompetent to succeed. Both versions are probably true.

For fifteen years, Marta managed the network's physical infrastructure. She knew every safehouse keeper by name. She knew which boat captains could be trusted and which harbors asked questions. She maintained the communication protocols—dead drops, signal flags, coded messages—and changed them seasonally. She was the voice of caution in Renne's ear, the one who argued against any expansion that might increase risk. She was the first to notice Alexandria's surveillance in the market, before Renne saw her, before Kel heard her footsteps. Something about the girl's posture bothered her. She was right.

Marta had lost her own children to a plague decades before the network existed. She never spoke of them, but Renne knew their names because Marta whispered them sometimes in her sleep. She treated Renne with a gruff protectiveness that masked a depth of love she had no other outlet for. She was the closest thing Renne had to a mother, though neither woman would have used that word.

She died holding a safehouse exit during the purge, ensuring every child was through the tunnel before she followed. A Cradle-hound and its handler cornered her at the threshold. She killed the handler with a fisherman's gutting knife—a blade she had carried for forty years—and was killed in turn by the hound. Her body was not recovered. The children she died protecting never learned her name.

### Captain Doren — The Sea Bridge

**Full name:** Doren Vask

**Age:** Fifty-six at time of death

**Status:** Deceased. Sank with his vessel, the *Grey Petrel*.

**Background:** Former League naval officer. Mundane.

Doren served twenty years in the Admiralty's fleet, rising to captain of a patrol cutter. His career ended in a political dispute with a superior—the details are known only to him and the Admiralty board that reviewed his case. He was permitted to resign rather than face court-martial, which left him bitter, idle, and living on a modest pension in Salthaven. He was fifty-six years old and had no idea what to do with the rest of his life.

Twelve years ago, he contracted a wasting lung disease that the Order's placed healers declared untreatable. Renne heard of him through the network's contacts and offered to help. She healed his lungs over three sessions, each one leaving her voice a little hoarser, each one carving new debt into the flesh of her throat. The scar across her larynx—which she hides with high-collared shirts—is Doren's. When she speaks, there is a permanent roughness in her voice. It is his echo.

Doren never forgot the debt, though Renne never asked for repayment. He joined the network shortly after his recovery and became its maritime arm. His vessel, the *Grey Petrel*, was a fishing trawler exactly what it appeared to be: a working boat that caught fish and sold them at market. But it also had a hidden compartment below the hold, insulated and ventilated, large enough to hold up to four children for short crossings. Doren knew the southern sea lanes better than any navigator in the League. He knew which routes were patrolled, which harbors were watched, and which islands had coves where a boat could dock unseen.

He was the calmest person in any crisis. He called the children "little passengers" and treated their transport with the same gravity he had once given fleet maneuvers. He was not an ideological man; he did not hate the Order in the abstract. He simply owed Renne a debt, and he paid it with service.

He died during the evacuation. The *Grey Petrel* was intercepted as it left Salthaven harbor. Doren ran a decoy route, drawing the Hunter-Killer skiff into a reef channel that only he knew—a narrow, treacherous passage where the Order's vessel could not follow. The children were transferred to a secondary boat and escaped. Doren went down with the *Petrel*, taking at least one Silencer with him. Renne felt his drowning as a sympathetic echo: a sudden pressure in her chest, a lungful of phantom seawater, and then nothing.

### Kel — The Ears

**Full name:** Kel (no surname; Order records list him as "Kel, Wind Gate, Sound mutation, escaped age ten")

**Age:** 22

**Status:** Alive. Permanently deafened.

**Gate and Mutation:** Wind Gate, Depth 3, Sound mutation (destroyed).

Kel was collected by the Order at age six from a fishing village in the eastern League. He trained at the Cradle for four years. His Sound mutation manifested early—he could hear conversations through walls, footsteps at impossible distances, the subvocalizations mages made before casting. The Order flagged him for accelerated training. He was considered a high-potential asset.

He escaped at ten during a transport accident. The details are unclear, even to him—he remembers fire, shouting, a Handler's body on the ground, and running. Renne's network picked him up half-starved on a remote island, feral and mute and flinching at every sound his mutation forced him to hear. He has been with the network ever since.

For twelve years, Kel served as the River's early warning system. His Sound mutation allowed him to perceive an auditory landscape far beyond normal human range. He could identify individual footsteps by their rhythm and weight. He could hear the distinctive hum of Cradle-hound tracking frequencies from half a mile away. He could listen to conversations on the other side of a safehouse wall and catch the subtle vocal tension that preceded violence. He was the reason the network had never been caught by surprise.

Kel is not good with people. His attention is perpetually split between the conversation at hand and the sonic environment only he can perceive. He speaks in fragments, his sentences trailing off as he tracks sounds no one else can hear. He is fiercely protective of Renne—she is the closest thing to a mother he has ever had—and suspicious of anyone who might hurt her. When Hark was brought into the network, Kel's hostility was immediate and undisguised.

During the purge, Kel pushed his mutation past every known limit to track the Hunter-Killer team's movements through Salthaven. He provided continuous real-time intelligence: their position, their numbers, their rate of advance. The overuse triggered a catastrophic debt-settlement event. His eardrums ruptured. The Sound mutation burned out completely, leaving him with permanent, profound deafness and a constant internal ringing that will never stop.

He survived the evacuation. He is alive. But the gift that made him valuable to the network—that structured his entire identity—is gone. He must now rebuild himself as a deaf man in a world he can no longer hear. The network will help him. Renne will not abandon him. But the silence is absolute, and he is not yet sure who he is inside it.

### Elder Seris — The Shore Anchor

**Full name:** Seris Halen

**Age:** 74

**Status:** Alive. Home destroyed during the purge.

**Background:** Retired schoolteacher. Mundane.

Seris taught three generations of Salthaven's children to read. She retired twenty years ago, but the town has never stopped calling her "Elder"—a title of respect, not authority. She knows everyone. Everyone respects her. Her modest house near the old school is a gathering place for neighbors, a lending library for children, and, for the past fifteen years, the quiet center of the River's shore-based operations.

Seris was the first person in Salthaven to open her door to Renne. A young woman with bandaged hands had arrived in town, asking no questions and offering no explanations. Seris asked no questions either. She made tea. She offered a bed. She has been the network's anchor ever since.

Her home is not a safehouse. No children are hidden there. But it is where Renne stays when she is in Salthaven. It is where the inner circle gathers to discuss operations. It is where couriers leave messages in a loose floorboard, where dead-drop locations are memorized and updated. Seris provides the cover of absolute respectability: no one questions visitors to the former schoolteacher's home.

Seris is the only person to whom Renne has told the full story—Alexandria, Hark, the foundling house, the seventeen years of guilt, the flower pressed in the book. Seris listened without judgment, asked no questions, and has never repeated a word. She is Renne's confessor, though neither woman would use that term.

She is also the network's moral center. When Renne wavered about approaching Hark—afraid of his rejection, afraid of reopening a wound she had carried for seventeen years—Seris was the one who told her that silence was its own kind of cruelty. "You have been quiet long enough," she said. "He deserves to know. She deserves to know. And you deserve to stop carrying this alone."

During the purge, Seris's home was burned. Whether by the Order's doing or as collateral damage, it no longer matters. She escaped with the evacuees. She is seventy-four years old and homeless, but she is alive, and she has become the network's memory-keeper. She will ensure the dead—Marta, Doren, and all the children who did not make it—are not forgotten.

## PART THREE: THE WIDER NETWORK

Beyond the inner circle, the River depends on a diffuse web of allies who provide specific, limited services. They are not told the full scope of the network. They are paid in coin or in kind. They are chosen for reliability, discretion, and a demonstrated indifference to the Order's authority.

### Safehouse Keepers

Six households are scattered across the southern islands, each willing to shelter children for periods ranging from a few days to several weeks. They are fisherfolk, farmers, a retired shipwright, a widow who runs a boarding house on a minor trade route. They know only that a healer with bandaged hands may arrive with a child, that the child must be fed and hidden and asked no questions, and that they will be compensated for their trouble.

Safehouse keepers are not told where the children come from or where they go. They are not told the names of other keepers. If a keeper is compromised, they can reveal only their own location—a single node in a network they do not understand. This compartmentalization is Marta's design. It has held for fifteen years.

### Couriers

A rotating cast of merchant sailors, traveling peddlers, and dockworkers carry messages between safehouses and the inner circle. They are paid to deliver, not to read. Messages are written in a simple substitution cipher that changes every season. No message contains names; everyone is referenced by role or location ("the healer," "the ears," "the anchor"). A courier who is captured can reveal nothing of substance because they know nothing of substance.

Some couriers suspect more than they say. They notice the patterns—the children who appear on certain boats, the healer who moves through the islands like a ghost. They do not ask. In the Maelstrom League, asking questions about the Order's business is a good way to become the Order's business.

### The Salthaven Complicity

The town of Salthaven itself is a layer of passive protection. Its residents are not formal members of the network, but they have decided, individually and collectively, that the Order is not welcome here.

The harbormaster files incomplete manifests, omitting certain vessels and certain passengers. The market vendors sell supplies to Renne at cost—bandages, dried food, children's clothing—and ask no questions. The tavern keeper at the Dock's End listens to strangers' conversations and passes warnings to Marta when someone asks about the healer or the children or the fishing boat that sails at odd hours. The ferryman who runs the crossing to the outer islands has been paid extra a hundred times and has never once asked what cargo he was carrying.

This complicity is fragile. It depends on trust, habit, and a shared dislike of the Order that no one articulates aloud. It has survived for fifteen years because Salthaven is too insignificant to warrant the Order's sustained attention—and because the people who live here know that the Order's mercy is not mercy at all.

## PART FOUR: OPERATIONAL PROTOCOLS

### Communication

The network communicates through dead drops. The locations are mundane and forgettable: hollow stones in the harbor wall, a loose floorboard in Seris's house, a specific fishing buoy that signals "safe to approach" when its marker flag is reversed. Messages are written on paper that is burned after reading. The cipher is a simple alphabetic substitution, changed every season, taught only to the inner circle and to couriers on a need-to-know basis.

No message contains a name. Renne is "the Heart." Kel is "the Ears." Doren was "the Sea Bridge." A safehouse is referenced by its island and a number. A child is referenced by age and Gate. If a message is intercepted, it reads like a merchant's inventory.

### Child Movement

Children are moved at night, by boat when possible, by coastal path when not. They travel in groups of one or two, never larger. Each child is accompanied by an adult who has made the run before and knows the route.

The route changes each time, adapted to patrol patterns, weather, and the specific dangers of the season. Marta maintained a mental map of every viable path through the southern islands; she is dead now, and that map died with her. Rebuilding it is one of the network's highest priorities.

### Safehouse Protocol

When a child arrives at a safehouse, they are bathed, fed, and given a change of clothes. Their original garments are burned or buried—this removes scent trails that Cradle-hounds could follow. The child is examined by Renne if she is available, or by the safehouse keeper if she is not. Injuries are treated. Illnesses are healed, if Renne can reach them in time.

If the child is old enough—typically seven or older—they are taught a crude guarding technique: *breathe slow, think of a door closing, hold it shut.* This is not the Order's method. It is simpler, cruder, and less effective. It does not fully prevent magical leakage, but it dampens it enough to reduce the risk of detection by Mage-Detectors or Savants conducting passive sweeps.

Younger children cannot learn the technique. They are moved faster, held for shorter periods, and never housed near other Sensitive children whose combined leakage might create a detectable signature.

### Emergency Protocol

If a safehouse is compromised—if strangers are seen asking questions, if a courier goes missing, if a Cradle-hound is sighted—the child is moved immediately, regardless of weather, time, or readiness. Speed trumps caution in a breach.

If a courier goes missing, all routes are changed. The compromised courier's contacts are isolated. The dead drops they knew are abandoned.

If the Order is sighted within a day's sail of Salthaven, the network goes silent. No movements. No messages. No contact between inner circle members except through Seris. Renne has ordered a full silence twice in fifteen years. Both times, the Order found nothing and moved on.

### Sanctuary Placement

When a child reaches permanent sanctuary—a remote fishing village, a sympathetic family, or, in rare cases, the Hold-Kingdoms—the network's responsibility ends. The child is given a new name, a new history, and a warning: never use magic where anyone can see, never let a Savant read you, never trust the Order. Some children follow this warning. Some do not. The network does not track outcomes; it only moves children forward.

The Hold-Kingdoms route is the most dangerous and the least used. The mountain lords of Khaz-Vor are building an army of Insensitives—Silencers without the Order's training, warriors immune to magic who answer to no Curator. They have use for Sensitive children the Order cannot track, but their motives are political, not humanitarian. Renne has sent only a handful of children north, and only when no other sanctuary was available.

## PART FIVE: THE COST

### Funding

The River is not free. Every child moved costs resources: food, clothing, transport, the quiet payments that keep harbormasters cooperative and safehouse keepers willing. The network is funded through three primary channels.

First, donations from sympathetic merchants. A few trading families in the League, none powerful enough to attract attention, provide regular contributions. They do not know the full scope of the network; they believe they are funding a charitable operation that helps orphaned children. They are not wrong.

Second, Renne's healing work. She rarely charges for healing, but some recipients insist on payment, and she accepts what is offered. The income is modest and irregular.

Third, in lean times, the sale of valuables. Marta maintained a small reserve of gold, gems, and trade-weights that she acquired through channels she never disclosed. When funds ran short, she sold something. The reserve is now depleted or lost, and the network's financial future is uncertain.

### Renne's Body

The deeper cost is the one Renne pays personally. Her Healing mutation exacts a price for every working: sympathetic injury. She experiences a version of every wound she heals, every illness she cures. The scars on her hands—visible through the bandages she never removes—are the physical record of seventy-three such workings, each one a child she saved.

Specific debts she carries:

- A permanent hoarseness in her voice, from healing Doren's lungs twelve years ago. The scar across her larynx is visible when she tilts her head back.
- A hairline fracture in her left radius that never fully healed, from mending a child's broken arm during the network's third year. It aches in cold weather.
- A persistent cough that worsens each winter, from clearing a pneumonia-ridden infant's lungs seven years ago.
- Phantom pain in her right knee, from healing a dislocated joint on a twelve-year-old boy who had been beaten by his village for "witchcraft."
- After the purge: a permanent low-level ringing in her ears—quieter than Kel's, but always present—from the sympathetic debt of stabilizing him after his eardrums ruptured.

She remembers each one. The child's name. The injury. The moment of healing. She carries them not as a burden but as a ledger. The Order writes its debts in frost-scars and cracked lips and neural tremors. Renne writes hers in bandaged hands and a hoarse voice and a body that ages faster than it should.

She has never healed herself. Not the exhaustion that drags at her limbs after a working. Not the chronic pain in her hands that makes fine manipulation difficult by evening. Not the grief she has carried for seventeen years. She considers her body a tool, and tools are maintained, not healed. When Marta told her this was foolish, she said: "Every healing I do for myself is a healing I can't do for a child."

This is not sustainable. It is, however, who she is.

### The Children Who Don't Make It

The River does not save every child. Some are detected before the network can reach them. Some are captured in transit. Some die of illness or injury before Renne can heal them. The network has lost children. The exact number is known only to Renne and Marta, and Marta is dead.

Each loss is a scar Renne carries that has nothing to do with magic. She does not speak of them. When a child is lost, she continues working. The next child is already waiting.

## PART SIX: PRESENT STATUS

The network has survived the purge of Salthaven but is fundamentally altered. The following is its condition at the conclusion of the Salthaven crisis.

### Losses

- **Marta** is dead. She was killed holding a safehouse exit, ensuring the last children escaped. Her death leaves a void in the network's logistics. No one else knows the full map of safehouses, couriers, and routes. Rebuilding that knowledge is a priority.
- **Doren** is dead. The *Grey Petrel* is lost, sunk in a reef channel while running decoy. The network's maritime capability is crippled. A new vessel and a new captain must be found.
- **Kel** is deafened. His Sound mutation is destroyed. The network has lost its early warning system. Kel himself must rebuild his identity without the gift that defined him.
- **Elder Seris's home** is destroyed. The network's communication hub is gone. Seris herself survived, but her role as the network's shore anchor must be reimagined.
- **The safehouse network** is compromised. All previous safehouse locations are abandoned. The keepers have been warned to scatter. Some may be re-recruited; others will not risk it again.
- **Salthaven** is no longer safe. The Order's attention is on the southern League. Renne's face is known. The town's complicity may not survive scrutiny.

### Survivors

- **Renne** is alive, scarred, and leading the evacuation. She carries new debt from healing during the purge and from Kel's stabilization. Her hands are worse than they have ever been. She is exhausted beyond measure. She is still working.
- **Kel** is alive but profoundly changed. He faces a future without the sense that defined him. The network will support him, but his role must evolve.
- **Seris** is alive and has assumed the role of memory-keeper—the one who will ensure the dead are not forgotten. She is seventy-four years old and homeless, but her moral authority is intact.
- **The children** are safe. Every child in the network's care at the time of the purge was evacuated. This was Marta's doing, and Doren's, and Kel's. It is the one victory the purge did not take.

### New Assets

- **Hark** has been integrated into the network. A former Class C mercenary and escaped Fire-mage—identity now known to the Order as Damian Whiters—he brings twenty-two years of survival tradecraft. His skills in countersurveillance, threat assessment, and knowledge of Order tactics complement the network's existing capabilities. He has agreed to serve as the network's new center of intelligence, responsible for vetting new safehouse locations, designing communication protocols, and training couriers.
- **Alexandria** is a rogue Silencer. She is fifteen years old, the daughter Hark never knew he had and Renne surrendered seventeen years ago. She fought alongside the network during the purge. She is now hunted by the same program that created her. Her relationship with her parents is nascent and fragile. Her future with the network is undetermined.
- **Vara** is a defector. A retired placed Water-mage who served as Alexandria's handler and chose to warn the network of the purge, she is now a traitor to the Order. Her knowledge of Order protocols and her access to archival systems are valuable, but she must be protected. She has not yet decided whether to stay with the network or seek her own sanctuary.

## PART SEVEN: THE FUTURE

The River will rebuild. The work does not end because the people doing it have changed.

The immediate priorities are: establish new safehouse locations, recruit new maritime transport, develop new communication protocols that do not depend on Kel's hearing, and secure long-term sanctuary for the children currently in transit. These tasks will fall to Renne, Hark, and the surviving members of the inner circle.

The longer-term questions are unresolved. Can the network operate without Marta's logistical genius? Can it move children without Doren's knowledge of the sea lanes? Can it survive without Salthaven's complicity? Can Hark, a man who has spent twenty-two years avoiding attachment, become the architect of a system that depends on trust?

The answers will be found in the work. The River flows. The children still need saving. The Order still hunts. The silence is broken, but the silence can be rebuilt.

Renne carries a dried flower in a book. Hark carries its twin in a lockbox. They have not spoken of what this means. Seventeen years of silence do not end in a single conversation. But they are standing on the same side now, facing the same direction. That is more than either of them expected to have.

The River remembers. The River continues. The debt always settles, but some debts are worth paying.
