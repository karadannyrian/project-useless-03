Renne — Complete Character Profile

Compiled from fragmented Order records, network oral history, and the accounts of those she has healed. This document does not exist in any official archive. The woman it describes has spent fifteen years ensuring no such record could be assembled.

---

SECTION ONE: IDENTITY AND CLASSIFICATION

Identity

Operational name: Renne (surname unknown; Order intake records list her as "Renne, Water Gate, female, estimated age six")
Aliases: The Healer (Salthaven), the Heart (network internal), the woman with bandaged hands (local oral tradition)
Age: 33
Place of origin: Unknown. Collected too young to retain reliable memory of birthplace. Linguistic markers in early intake notes suggest southern Hold-Kingdoms or northern Maelstrom League.
Bloodline: Unknown. No recorded family. No known living relatives.
Gate: Water
Mutation: Healing (classified as restricted by the Order; unsanctioned use carries terminal sanction)
Estimated Depth: 5–6 (never formally assessed by Order Savant; estimate based on observed capability and debt accumulation)
Order classification: Escaped, unmarked, active (file inactive—presumed low priority prior to recent Archivists' flag)

Current Status

Leader and founder of the River, an underground network smuggling Sensitive children to safety. Operates primarily in the southern Maelstrom League, centered on Salthaven. Has performed seventy-three documented healings since escape, each incurring sympathetic injury as debt. Has never healed herself. Has not seen the father of her child in seventeen years, though she knows he resides in the same town and has avoided him. Has never told him about their daughter.

SECTION TWO: PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

Small-framed, fine-boned. Dark hair, typically pulled back from her face. Grey eyes with a wintery cast—the kind of grey that looks almost silver in certain light. Her face is younger than her years but her expression is older, the corners of her mouth settling into lines that suggest more sorrow than laughter.

Her hands are always wrapped in bandages. The linen is clean, changed daily, but the wrapping is a permanent feature of her appearance. Beneath the bandages: dense networks of fine white scarring, the hallmark of Water-mage Healing debt. The scars are not the coarse, thick tissue of mundane injury. They are delicate, almost lace-like, running across her palms, her knuckles, the pads of her fingers, the backs of her hands. They are the ledger of seventy-three healings, each one a child, each one remembered.

A single visible scar escapes the bandages: a pale line across the base of her throat, visible when she tilts her head back or wears a low collar. This is the mark of the healing she performed on Captain Doren's lungs twelve years ago—a wasting sickness reversed, the cost paid in permanent hoarseness. Her voice is roughened at the edges, a permanent whisper beneath the words.

She wears high-collared shirts and long sleeves in all seasons, partly for concealment and partly because she runs cold. Water-mages often do; the constant ambient moisture draw leaves their bodies slightly cooler than normal. She hums without realizing it—old Water-mage lullabies she learned before she was collected, the melodies her mother sang. The air around her cools and dampens faintly when she does.

Her posture is not the rigid readiness of a Silencer or the coiled watchfulness of a mercenary. It is the exhausted competence of someone who has been working too long without rest and has learned to carry exhaustion without letting it slow her down. She moves quietly, not from training but from habit—fifteen years of entering rooms where children are sleeping, of leaving places before anyone notices she was there.

SECTION THREE: ORIGINS AND COLLECTION

Childhood

Renne does not remember her birthplace. She has fragments: a cold climate, stone walls, a woman's voice humming a tune that made the air smell like rain. Her mother, she assumes. She does not know her mother's name. She does not know if her mother is alive.

She was collected young—six, perhaps seven. The Order's intake records note that she was found alone in a fishing village in the northern Maelstrom League, already Sensitive, already broadcasting. Her internal reservoir was leaking moisture into the air around her; the villagers had noticed that rain followed her, that the air grew damp when she was upset. Someone reported her. The Order came.

She remembers the Handler's hands—clean, steady, impersonal. The white silk on her wrists. The carriage with the high windows. The Intake Hall where the Savant held her palms and read her blank ledger—she had not yet performed any conscious magic, had no debt to read. The Assessor noted: Water Gate. Moderate depth potential. Emotional markers indicate fear and confusion consistent with recent collection. No mutation indicators at this stage.

She was classified Tier I—Promising. She was bathed, clothed in grey, and assigned to a dormitory with twelve other Water-Sensitive children. She learned to guard within eight months—faster than most. The Instructor noted she was "quietly determined, responsive to correction, does not seek attention but does not resist it."

Training

Renne's training followed the standard Water Gate curriculum. Base-state manipulation: drawing from ambient humidity, shaping standing water, the beginnings of hydraulic exoskeleton. Body sense: life-reading through blood flow and hydraulic pressure—a skill that made her stomach turn at first, the intimacy of feeling another person's pulse from across a room. Kinetic amplification: the hydraulic exoskeleton that could triple her strength for short bursts, paid for in cracked lips and unquenchable thirst.

She was never a combat mage. Her Instructors noted her reluctance during Affliction training—she mastered the technique (desiccation, the draining of moisture from an opponent's body) but performed it with visible distress. The Instructor wrote: "Subject understands the mechanics. Subject does not wish to apply them. This is not insubordination; it is temperament. Recommend against combat placement. Mutation potential may offer alternative path."

Mutation Identification

The Healing mutation manifested late, in her third year of training. She was eleven. A younger child in her dormitory had fallen from the training floor's upper gallery and broken an arm—a compound fracture, the bone visible through the skin. The instructors were summoning a healer. Renne reached the child first.

She did not think. She did not cast. The healing happened through her, not from her—a sudden pull of moisture from the air, a warmth that flowed down her arm and into the child's, and then the bone was knitting, the flesh was closing, and Renne's own arm was screaming with a sympathetic fracture that was not real but felt exactly real, the pain identical, the bone whole but the nerves convinced otherwise.

She passed out. When she woke, her arm was unbroken but aching, and a thin white line had appeared across her palm—the first of what would become a lifetime of debt-scars.

The Savant who read her afterward noted: Healing mutation, unsanctioned manifestation. Sympathetic injury debt pattern confirmed. Subject experienced a complete sympathetic echo of the healed injury. Recommend immediate formal training; uncontrolled manifestation risks severe self-harm.

She was transferred to the Healing track the following week.

Healing Training

The Order's Healing curriculum is the shortest and the most brutal. Healers are too rare to waste time on gradual conditioning. The training philosophy is exposure: the student heals increasingly severe injuries, learning to manage the sympathetic debt through repetition and desensitization.

Renne healed her first fatal wound at thirteen—a training accident, a Wind-mage with a collapsed lung. She saved the child's life. She spent the next three weeks unable to draw a full breath, her own lungs refusing to expand beyond a certain point, the sympathetic echo of the injury she had mended. The healers told her the debt would fade. It did, mostly. She still feels a catch in her chest when she runs.

By fifteen, she had healed broken bones, lacerations, infections, one case of early organ failure. Each healing left a scar on her hands. Each healing cost her a version of the injury she had cured. She learned to budget the pain, to anticipate the debt, to schedule healings with recovery time in between. She was the most promising Healing student in her cohort.

She was also quietly, desperately, planning to escape.

SECTION FOUR: ESCAPE

The Decision

Renne did not escape because she was mistreated. She was not. The Order had trained her well, pushed her hard, and prepared her for a life of service. She was on track for Path One—placement in a realm as a placed Healing-mage, valued, respected, and forever marked by the Order's debt-pattern.

She escaped because she had read the full text of the Healing mutation's cost in the Cradle's restricted archives. The sympathetic debt was not temporary. It accumulated. Every healing she performed would leave a permanent mark—a version of the injury, a shadow of the pain, a scar that never fully faded. A Healing-mage who served the Order for forty years would, by the end, be carrying the accumulated echoes of thousands of wounds, a body that had experienced everything and healed nothing.

She was fifteen. She had already healed a collapsed lung, a compound fracture, a case of organ failure. Her hands were already marked. She looked at her future and saw a woman slowly being crushed under the weight of other people's pain, and she decided, with the cold clarity that would define the rest of her life, that she would not let the Order decide whose pain she carried.

The Escape

She walked out. She did not run. Running attracted attention. She had studied the patrol patterns, the shift changes, the door that was left unguarded for seven minutes between the third and fourth bells. She had befriended a kitchen worker—not befriended, exactly; she had been kind to a woman the other children ignored, and the woman had told her about the delivery door without realizing she was telling her anything useful.

She was fifteen. She walked through the delivery door, crossed the yard, and kept walking. She did not look back. She did not run until she reached the treeline, and then she ran until her lungs gave out, and then she walked some more.

The Order did not catch her. She does not know why. Perhaps they assumed she would return—Healing students sometimes broke under the pressure and fled, and most came back within days, exhausted and remorseful. Perhaps they were embarrassed that a fifteen-year-old had exploited a seven-minute gap in their security. Perhaps someone in the Cradle decided she was not worth the effort of recovery.

She does not know. She does not trust the ease of her escape. She has spent fifteen years waiting for the Order to correct its mistake.

SECTION FIVE: THE COTTAGE AND HARK

The River (Age 16–17)

She spent a year running before she found the river. Not a specific river—any river. Water was her element, and water remembered, and she needed to be somewhere that would not forget her. She settled in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy, a wide green land of enormous skies and slow-moving water, and she began to heal.

Not officially. Not as a profession. She healed because there were people who needed healing and she could do it, and refusing felt like a betrayal of the gift she had been given. She healed a farmer's son who had been gored by a bull. She healed a woman dying of a wasting fever. Each healing left a scar on her hands. Each healing felt like a small defiance of the Order that had told her healing was a tool to be managed, a debt to be tracked, an asset to be placed.

She was sixteen when she found the boy in the river.

The Meeting

She had been practicing at the river's edge, pulling water from the current in thin invisible columns, when she sensed him. Not his magic—his magic was Fire, and she could feel it as a heat-bleed against the cool of the water. He was kneeling in the shallows just past a bend, his hands submerged, his sleeves rolled up. The frost-scars on his forearms were visible even from a distance. He was not moving.

She approached quietly. He did not hear her—or did not care. He was very still. Not the stillness of a predator. The stillness of someone who had stopped running and did not know what to do with the silence.

"You're leaking heat," she said.

He looked up. He was younger than her, lean and sharp-edged, with grey eyes that had seen too much. His face was blank in a way she recognized—the blankness of someone who had learned to hide everything because everything was dangerous.

His scars were visible. He did not move to cover them. He did not seem to care if she saw.

"Come inside," she said. "You'll freeze."

She extended her hand—not her scarred one, but the other.

He looked at her hand for a long time. Then he took it.

The Four Months

He did not speak for the first week. She did not push. She talked enough for both of them, filling the silence with the sound of her own voice, telling him about the cottage, the root cellar, the children she sometimes sheltered. She did not ask his name. She did not ask where he came from. She fed him, gave him the bed, took the root cellar for herself.

He started speaking again on the eighth day. One word. Then another. Short, declarative sentences, no conjunctions. His voice was rusty from disuse. He told her his name was Hark—a name someone had given him, not the one he was born with. He told her about Lea. He told her about Joel. He told her about the forest.

She told him about the Order, the Healing mutation, the escape. She showed him her hands—the scars that matched his, different element but same language. She told him she remembered every working, every child, every scar. Seventy-three, she said, though the number was smaller then. Seventy-three since she had started counting.

He asked: "Why do you keep doing it? If it hurts you."

She said: "Because it hurts me more not to."

He did not have an answer to that. He went back to sharpening his knife.

The Flower

He gave her a dried flower. Not on the first day or the last. Somewhere in the middle, when they had stopped being strangers and had not yet become whatever they were becoming. He had picked it from the riverbank and pressed it between the pages of a book she had lent him. He held it out without explanation, without meeting her eyes.

She took it. She understood, immediately and completely, that this was the only way he could say what he felt—not with words, which were too dangerous, but with a small dead thing that he had kept alive through sheer stubbornness.

"You kept this," she said.

He nodded.

"You're not allowed to want," she said—because she had figured out his rules by then, the invisible architecture of his silence. "But you kept it anyway."

He did not answer. He could not.

She put the flower in her pocket. She has kept it ever since, pressed in a small leather-bound book of plants from every region she has passed through. The first entry. The only one that matters.

The Separation

The courier arrived on a night in late spring. An Order patrol was three days north. Not hunting them specifically—not yet—but sweeping through the Confederacy. They would find the cottage. They would find the children. They would find her.

"You should go," she told Hark. "Your face is known in the Confederacy."

"I know."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet. "This isn't goodbye. It can't be. I won't let it be."

He did not speak. He reached into his pocket, as if checking for something. The flower. He still had his.

She kissed his cheek. "The river remembers," she said.

Then she was gone, and so was he, in different directions, into different kinds of silence.

She did not know she was pregnant. She would not know for another month, when the sickness started and the healer's instinct told her what her body was doing before her mind caught up.

SECTION SIX: ALEXANDRIA — SURRENDER AND SILENCE

The Pregnancy

She was seventeen, alone, and pregnant with a child whose father had walked away without knowing. She was also running a network—fragile, new, already responsible for half a dozen children she had pulled from the Order's path.

The pregnancy was hard. She was small-framed, underfed, operating on adrenaline and stubbornness. The children in her care needed her. The Order patrols were getting closer. She could not stop moving. She stopped anyway, when her body forced her to.

In the eighth month, she found a farmer's abandoned barn far from any road. A midwife came—a woman Marta knew, old and kind and sworn to secrecy. The birth was long and difficult. Renne did not scream. She had learned not to scream in the Cradle, where screaming attracted attention and attention was never kind.

The Daughter

Alexandria was born on a winter night. The barn was cold. Renne held her for three days, singing old Water-mage lullabies that made the air in the barn cool and damp. The baby had grey eyes—Hark's eyes. She looked up at Renne with the unfocused gaze of a newborn and Renne felt something break open in her chest that she had been keeping sealed since she was six years old.

She could not keep her. She was seventeen, hunted, running a network that depended on her survival. If the Order found her with a Sensitive infant—and the child would be Sensitive, with two mage parents—they would take the baby and execute Renne. The child would be raised in the Cradle, trained as an asset, marked with debt. Renne could not let that happen.

She could not keep her. She could not let the Order take her. There was a third option.

The Foundling House

On the third day, she wrapped Alexandria in clean cloth and walked three miles through the snow to an Order foundling house. The matron was a woman with a kind face and tired eyes. Renne did not give her name. She gave Alexandria's name—Alexandria—because a child deserved a name, even if she never knew who gave it. She held her daughter for a long time before letting go. She hummed the lullaby one last time. The air in the room went cold.

"Her name is Alexandria," Renne said. "Please. Let her keep it."

The matron nodded. She did not ask questions. She had seen too many women surrender too many children to ask questions anymore.

Renne handed her daughter to a stranger. She walked back into the snow. She did not look back. She found the river—the same river, miles downstream, where the willows still trailed their fingers in the current. She knelt in the shallows. She pressed her hands to her chest. The healing debt scars burned.

She whispered: "I'll find you again. I swear it."

She did not cry. She had not cried since the Cradle. But she felt something settle in her chest, a weight that was not debt, not magic, not anything but grief—and she carried it with her when she stood, and when she walked back to the network, and when she resumed the work of saving other people's children because she could not save her own.

SECTION SEVEN: THE RIVER — NETWORK AND MISSION

Founding

The River began before Alexandria was born, but it became what it is in the years after. Renne, grieving and exhausted and relentlessly competent, built an escape route that ran beneath the Order's notice. She called it the River—a name that had nothing to do with geography and everything to do with memory. The river where she met Hark. The river where she surrendered Alexandria. The river that remembered.

The network does not fight the Order. It does not stockpile weapons or train mages in combat magic. It does one thing: it moves Sensitive children from the point of detection to a location beyond the Order's reach. It is a smuggling operation, not a rebellion. Its victories are measured in children who grow up uncollected.

Scale and Scope

The River moves twelve to twenty children per year. At any given time, three to eight children are in its care, spread across safehouses in the southern Maelstrom League. The average stay is three to six weeks before transfer to permanent sanctuary.

Salthaven was chosen as the center of operations for its insignificance: no naval base, no noble houses of consequence, no Order branch within two days' sail. The town's residents protect the network with collective silence. They know the healer with bandaged hands. They do not speak of her to outsiders.

Inner Circle

Renne does not lead alone. Her inner circle consists of four people who have been with her for a decade or more, each one essential, each one loved.

Marta — The Left Hand. A fisherwoman in her sixties, Renne's oldest ally. She runs logistics: safehouse locations, supply chains, communication protocols. She is the voice of caution, the one who argues against expansion, the one who noticed the Order's increased attention before anyone else. She lost her own children to a plague decades ago. She treats Renne with a gruff protectiveness that masks a depth of love she has no other outlet for. She is the closest thing Renne has to a mother.

Captain Doren — The Sea Bridge. A former League naval officer, forced into early retirement. He captains the Grey Petrel, a fishing trawler with a hidden compartment for children. Renne healed his lungs from a wasting sickness twelve years ago; the debt left the scar across her throat and the permanent hoarseness in her voice. He has never forgotten. He would sail into a hurricane for her.

Kel — The Ears. A Wind Gate with the Sound mutation, collected at six, escaped at ten, raised within the network. He is twenty-two now and fiercely protective of Renne. His mutation allows him to hear across impossible distances—footsteps, whispers, the hum of Cradle-hound tracking frequencies. He is the reason the network has never been caught by surprise. Renne is the closest thing to a mother he has ever had.

Elder Seris — The Shore Anchor. A retired schoolteacher in her seventies, born and raised in Salthaven. She opened her door to Renne fifteen years ago and has never closed it. Her home is not a safehouse but the network's communication hub—where messages are left, where the inner circle gathers. She is the only person to whom Renne has told the full story of Alexandria and Hark. She listened without judgment. She has never repeated a word.

The Work

Renne's role in the network is healer and leader and heart. She performs every magical healing the network requires. She also makes the hard decisions—when to move a child, when to abandon a safehouse, when to trust a new contact and when to refuse. She has been doing this for fifteen years. She is very tired. She does not know how to stop.

SECTION EIGHT: HEALING MUTATION AND DEBT

Mechanism

The Healing mutation is the rarest and most revered Water path. It allows the mage to accelerate and guide the body's natural healing processes, mending injuries and curing illnesses that mundane medicine cannot touch. The cost is sympathetic injury: the healer experiences a version of every wound they heal, proportional to severity.

A broken bone mended leaves a hairline fracture in the healer's own bone—not real, but felt, the nerves convinced of damage that does not exist on any mundane examination. A lung fever cleared leaves the healer coughing for weeks. A fatal wound reversed—a heart restarted, an organ repaired—can leave the healer bedridden for days, their body processing an echo of death.

The debt is permanent but adaptive. The physical scars (the white lines on Renne's hands) are the external markers. The internal markers—the hoarseness in her voice, the phantom aches, the recurring cough—are the body's memory of injuries it never actually sustained. Over decades, a Healing-mage accumulates a library of sympathetic wounds. The most experienced healers are, by the end, carrying the echoes of thousands of injuries. Most die young.

Renne's Ledger

Renne has performed seventy-three healings since her escape. She remembers every one. The child's name, the injury, the moment of healing, the debt incurred. The scars on her hands are the visible record. The invisible record is longer.

Specific debts she carries:

· The scar across her throat: From healing Doren's lungs twelve years ago. The sympathetic echo of a wasting sickness left her with permanent hoarseness and a visible mark at the base of her larynx. She conceals it with high-collared shirts.
· The hairline fracture in her left radius: From mending a child's broken arm during the network's third year. It never fully healed. It aches in cold weather.
· The winter cough: From clearing a pneumonia-ridden infant's lungs seven years ago. It returns every winter, a deep, wet cough that lasts for weeks and leaves her exhausted.
· Phantom pain in her right knee: From healing a dislocated joint on a twelve-year-old boy who had been beaten by his village for suspected witchcraft. The pain is not constant, but it flares unpredictably, and she has learned to hide the limp.
· Countless smaller echoes: Headaches from fevers healed, nausea from poisons drawn, brief flashes of sharp pain from lacerations mended. Her body is a library of other people's suffering. She does not resent it. She chose it.

The Refusal

She has never healed herself. Not the exhaustion. Not the chronic pain in her hands. Not the grief. The Healing mutation can technically be turned inward—a healer can mend their own injuries—but the cost is doubled: the healer experiences both the original injury and the sympathetic echo simultaneously. For most healers, self-healing is reserved for life-threatening emergencies only.

Renne has never attempted it, even in emergencies. She tells herself it is because the cost is too high, that a healer who is incapacitated cannot heal others. This is true. It is not the only truth. The deeper truth is that she does not believe she deserves healing. She has carried guilt for fifteen years—for surviving when other children died, for escaping when others were caught, for surrendering her daughter to strangers. The refusal to heal herself is penance. She does not think of it in those terms. She does not think of it at all.

SECTION NINE: PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

Cognitive Architecture

Renne's mind is organized around responsibility. She processes every situation through a single question: Who needs help, and how do I help them? This is not altruism in the abstract. It is a compulsive reorientation of attention toward the most vulnerable person in any given context. She cannot walk past a crying child. She cannot ignore a person in pain. She has tried. She fails every time.

This makes her an exceptional healer and an exceptional leader. It also makes her incapable of self-preservation in any meaningful sense. She will always choose to help others over helping herself. She will always absorb the cost rather than pass it on. She has constructed a life in which her own needs are perpetually deferred, and she has done it so thoroughly that she no longer notices the deferral.

Emotional Landscape

Renne's dominant emotional state is exhaustion, but beneath the exhaustion is grief, and beneath the grief is a stubborn, defiant hope she does not fully trust.

The grief is for Alexandria. She surrendered her daughter seventeen years ago and has never stopped mourning. Every child she saves is a child she imagines might have been Alexandria—might have been collected, trained, marked—and every healing is a small, insufficient atonement. She knows, rationally, that saving other children does not undo the loss of her own. She does it anyway. The grief is not rational. It is the engine of her life.

The hope is harder to locate. It lives in the dried flower she keeps pressed in a book. It lives in the fact that she has never stopped looking for Alexandria, even though she knows the odds of finding her are infinitesimal. It lives in the fact that she has not left Salthaven, even though she knows Hark is there, even though she knows she should avoid him—because some part of her, the part she does not listen to, wants to be found.

Guilt and Penance

Renne's guilt is structural. She feels responsible for everything: for the children she could not save, for the daughter she surrendered, for the network members who risk their lives for her mission. She carries the weight of seventy-three healings in her body and the weight of seventeen years of silence in her chest.

She does not believe she deserves forgiveness. She does not seek it. She works instead—endlessly, relentlessly, using her body as a tool and her exhaustion as a measure of her commitment. The refusal to heal herself is the most honest expression of her psychology: she is paying a debt she does not owe, and she will pay it until she cannot pay anymore.

Attachment Patterns

Renne loves easily and grieves constantly. She has built a family out of the network—Marta, Doren, Kel, Seris, the children who pass through her care—and she loves them with a ferocity that terrifies her. She knows, from experience, that love is the prelude to loss. She loves anyway.

Her relationship with Hark is the exception. She loved him at sixteen and could not keep him. She has avoided him for three years in Salthaven because seeing him would mean telling him about Alexandria, and telling him about Alexandria would mean facing the fact that she kept his daughter from him for seventeen years. She is not ready to face that. She may never be ready.

She is afraid that he will hate her. She is afraid that he will look at her with the cold threat-geometry he gives strangers. She is afraid that he will not care at all. She keeps the dried flower because it is the only proof she has that he once wanted to stay.

Self-Perception

Renne thinks of herself as a tool. Her body is a resource to be spent. Her magic is a debt to be managed. Her exhaustion is a sign that she is working hard enough. She does not think of herself as a person with needs, desires, or limits. She has been running on adrenaline and obligation for so long that she has forgotten what rest feels like.

She does not know that she is loved. Marta loves her. Doren loved her. Kel loves her with the desperate loyalty of a rescued child. Seris loves her with the steady patience of a woman who has seen everything and chosen to stay. The network would collapse without her, not because she is irreplaceable logistically—Marta could run the logistics, Doren could run the sea routes—but because she is the reason they do the work. The children love her. The town protects her.

She does not see any of this. She sees the work that still needs doing, the children still waiting, the debt still accumulating. She is very tired. She keeps going.

SECTION ELEVEN: RELATIONSHIPS

The Dead

Marta and Doren (living at time of profile, but their relationship defined). Marta is Renne's oldest friend and logistical anchor. Doren is the man whose lungs she healed, who has repaid the debt with a decade of service. She loves them both. She does not tell them this in words. She tells them by trusting them with the network, by sharing the burden, by allowing them to see her exhausted and afraid. For Renne, that is intimacy.

The Living

Kel. She found him half-starved on an island when he was ten, feral and mute and flinching at every sound his mutation forced him to hear. She taught him to filter the noise. She gave him a purpose. He is fiercely protective of her, and she loves him with the particular tenderness of someone who has raised a child that was not hers. She does not call him her son. He does not call her his mother. The words are irrelevant.

Seris. The only person who knows the full story. Seris is Renne's confessor, her moral anchor, the voice that tells her to rest when she will not listen. Renne does not know what she would do without her. She is afraid she will have to find out.

Hark. She has not spoken to him in seventeen years. She knows he is in Salthaven—has known for three years—and has avoided him. She tells herself it is operational security. She tells herself he has built a life and does not need her intrusion. She tells herself she is protecting him from the grief she carries. All of these are true. None of them is the real reason. The real reason is Alexandria. She cannot face him until she is ready to tell him the truth, and she has not been ready for seventeen years.

She carries his flower. She has never stopped carrying it.

The Absence

Alexandria. Renne has spent fifteen years looking for her daughter without knowing where to look. She does not know Alexandria's surname—the foundling house would have assigned one. She does not know if Alexandria is alive, if she was collected by the Order, if she is Sensitive or Insensitive, if she is safe. She knows only that her daughter has grey eyes and was born on a winter night and was named Alexandria because Renne wanted her to have something that was hers alone.

She thinks about Alexandria every day. She has never spoken her name aloud except to Seris. The name is a prayer she does not know how to make.

SECTION TWELVE: CURRENT STATUS

Renne lives in Salthaven, when she is not moving between safehouses. She stays at Seris's home, a modest house near the old school. She wears bandaged hands and high-collared shirts. She hums old Water-mage lullabies that make the air cool and damp. She has never healed herself.

She leads the River with Marta, Doren, Kel, and Seris. She has performed seventy-three healings. She is thirty-three years old and has been running a clandestine network for half her life. She is exhausted beyond measure. She keeps going.

She knows Hark is three streets away, above the ropewalk. She has not spoken to him. She keeps a dried flower in a book and tells herself it is just a flower.

She does not know that her daughter is about to arrive in Salthaven, wearing the Order's colors, carrying a mission that will bring all three of them into collision.

She is not ready. She has never been ready. She will do what needs to be done anyway.
